Cognitive Distortions Reference

15 common thinking errors. Use during session to help clients identify patterns. Click any card to expand the challenging question.

1. All-or-Nothing Thinking

Seeing things in black-and-white categories. If performance falls short of perfect, it's total failure.
"If I don't get an A, I'm a complete failure."
Ask: "Is there a middle ground? Can something be partly successful?"

2. Overgeneralization

Seeing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern of defeat, using words like "always" or "never."
"I always mess up interviews. I'll never get a job."
Ask: "Has there ever been a time this wasn't true? What are the exceptions?"

3. Mental Filter

Picking out a single negative detail and dwelling on it exclusively, filtering out all positive aspects.
"My supervisor mentioned one typo in my 20-page report. The whole thing was terrible."
Ask: "What positives might you be filtering out? What would someone else notice?"

4. Disqualifying the Positive

Rejecting positive experiences by insisting they "don't count" for some reason.
"She only said something nice because she felt sorry for me."
Ask: "What evidence is there that this positive feedback isn't genuine?"

5. Jumping to Conclusions

Making negative interpretations without evidence. Includes mind reading and fortune telling.
"He didn't text back. He must be angry at me."
Ask: "What are other possible explanations? Do you have actual evidence?"

6. Magnification / Minimization

Exaggerating the importance of negatives or shrinking the importance of positives (the "binocular trick").
"Making one mistake at work is catastrophic" but "The raise I got is no big deal."
Ask: "If a friend told you this, would you see it the same way?"

7. Emotional Reasoning

Assuming that negative feelings reflect the way things really are: "I feel it, therefore it must be true."
"I feel stupid, so I must actually be stupid."
Ask: "Is there a difference between how you feel and what the facts show?"

8. Should Statements

Using "should," "must," or "ought to" in ways that create guilt when directed at self, anger at others.
"I should be able to handle this without help."
Ask: "What would happen if you replaced 'should' with 'I would prefer'?"

9. Labeling

An extreme form of overgeneralization. Attaching a fixed label instead of describing the specific behavior.
"I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake on that task."
Ask: "Is this label accurate all the time? What would a fairer description be?"

10. Personalization

Seeing yourself as the cause of some external negative event for which you were not primarily responsible.
"The team lost because I wasn't good enough."
Ask: "What other factors contributed? Was this really all about you?"

11. Fortune Telling

Predicting that things will turn out badly and treating the prediction as established fact.
"I just know the presentation will go terribly."
Ask: "How many times have your predictions actually come true?"

12. Mind Reading

Concluding that someone is reacting negatively to you without checking or asking.
"She thinks I'm incompetent. I can tell."
Ask: "Have you checked this out directly? What else might explain their behavior?"

13. Catastrophizing

Expecting disaster. "What if" thinking that escalates to the worst possible outcome.
"What if I make a mistake and get fired and lose my house?"
Ask: "What is the most likely outcome? How have you coped with similar situations before?"

14. Blame

Holding others entirely responsible for your pain, or the reverse: blaming yourself for others' problems.
"It's all her fault I feel this way" or "Everything is my fault."
Ask: "How much of this is within your control? How much is within theirs?"

15. Always Being Right

Needing to prove that your opinions and actions are correct at the expense of others' feelings.
"I don't care if it hurts her. I was right and she needs to know it."
Ask: "What matters more here: being right, or maintaining the relationship?"

Socratic Questioning Guide

Use these questions during session to help clients examine and challenge their automatic thoughts.

Clarifying the Thought

▼
  • "What exactly do you mean when you say ___?"
  • "Can you tell me more about that thought?"
  • "When you say ___, what picture comes to mind?"
  • "How strongly do you believe that right now, on a 0-100 scale?"
  • "Is this a thought, a feeling, or both?"

Examining the Evidence

▼
  • "What evidence supports this thought?"
  • "What evidence goes against it?"
  • "Are you basing this on facts or feelings?"
  • "Have you had experiences that show this thought is not always true?"
  • "If your best friend had this thought, what evidence would you point out to them?"

Considering Alternatives

▼
  • "Is there another way to look at this situation?"
  • "What would you tell a friend who had this thought?"
  • "What are all the possible explanations?"
  • "If you weren't feeling ___, how might you interpret this differently?"
  • "Are there any positives in this situation that you might be overlooking?"

Examining the Impact

▼
  • "What happens when you believe this thought?"
  • "How does this thought affect your behavior?"
  • "Does this thought help you or hold you back?"
  • "What would change if you didn't hold this belief?"
  • "What's the worst that could happen? How likely is that? Could you cope with it?"

Testing the Thought

▼
  • "How could we test whether this thought is accurate?"
  • "What would convince you this thought was wrong?"
  • "What has happened in the past when you thought this way?"
  • "If this thought is true, what's the next step? If it's not, what's the next step?"
  • "What's a small experiment you could try this week?"
TF

Trauma-Focused CBT

PRACTICE components, relaxation techniques, and safety planning for trauma treatment

PRACTICE Components Reference

The eight core components of TF-CBT. Sessions typically follow this sequence, though clinical judgment guides pacing.

P Psychoeducation

Normalizing trauma responses for the child and caregiver. Providing information about the specific type of trauma, prevalence, and common reactions. Helps reduce shame and self-blame.
Key techniques: Age-appropriate trauma education, handouts, books, normalizing statements, caregiver parallel sessions
Sample prompts: "Many kids who have been through what you experienced have similar feelings." / "It's not unusual to feel scared or angry after something like that." / "Your body was trying to protect you."

R Relaxation Skills

Teaching the child and caregiver specific techniques to manage physiological stress responses. These skills are used throughout treatment and especially during trauma narrative work.
Key techniques: Focused breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery, mindfulness, yoga-based movements
Sample prompts: "Let's practice our balloon breathing." / "Tighten your fists really tight... now let them go like spaghetti." / "Picture your safe place."

A Affective Modulation

Building the child's ability to identify, express, and manage difficult emotions. Essential foundation before trauma narrative work begins.
Key techniques: Feeling identification, affect rating scales, positive self-talk, thought interruption, problem-solving, anger management
Sample prompts: "What does your body feel like when you're angry?" / "On a scale of 1-10, how strong is that feeling?" / "What could you say to yourself when you notice that feeling coming?"

C Cognitive Coping

Introducing the cognitive triangle (thoughts-feelings-behaviors) and helping the child understand how thoughts influence emotions and actions. Not yet trauma-specific.
Key techniques: Cognitive triangle exercises, thought-feeling-behavior connections, accurate vs. inaccurate thoughts, best friend role-play
Sample prompts: "If you thought 'this test will be easy,' how would you feel? What if you thought 'I'll definitely fail'?" / "Are there other ways to think about that?"

T Trauma Narrative

Gradual exposure through creating a detailed account of the traumatic experience(s). This is the central component. The narrative is developed over multiple sessions with increasing detail.
Key techniques: Gradual exposure, narrative writing/drawing/dictation, cognitive processing of trauma-specific thoughts, chapters approach
Sample prompts: "We're going to write your story together, at your pace." / "What happened next?" / "What were you thinking right at that moment?" / "What do you know now that you didn't know then?"

I In Vivo Mastery

Gradual real-world exposure to trauma reminders that are objectively safe but currently avoided. Uses a fear hierarchy. Not always needed if avoidance is minimal.
Key techniques: Fear hierarchy construction, graduated exposure tasks, in-session and between-session practice, parent coaching for support
Sample prompts: "Let's make a list of things that remind you of what happened, from least scary to most." / "You practiced going to the park this week. What was that like?"

C Conjoint Sessions

Bringing the child and caregiver together to share the trauma narrative and practice communication skills. Prepares both parties separately before conjoint work.
Key techniques: Caregiver preparation, narrative sharing, therapeutic communication practice, praise and validation coaching
Sample prompts: (To caregiver) "Your child has been working hard on their story and wants to share it with you." / (To child) "You get to decide how much to share."

E Enhancing Safety

Safety planning, body safety skills, and relapse prevention. Addresses real safety concerns and empowers the child to recognize and respond to unsafe situations.
Key techniques: Safety planning, assertiveness training, body safety education, identifying safe adults, bullying prevention, digital safety
Sample prompts: "Who are three adults you could go to if you felt unsafe?" / "What's the difference between a safe touch and an unsafe touch?" / "Let's practice what you could say."

Relaxation Techniques

Scripts and guides for teaching relaxation skills. Can be used in session or sent home as practice assignments.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation Script

▼

Instructions: Read slowly in a calm, steady voice. Pause 5-10 seconds at each "..." mark. The full exercise takes approximately 15 minutes.

Hands and Arms: "Make a fist with both hands. Squeeze tight... feel the tension in your hands and forearms... hold it... and now release. Let your hands go completely limp. Notice the difference between tension and relaxation..."

Arms and Shoulders: "Now stretch your arms out straight and push your hands toward the ceiling... feel the pull in your arms and shoulders... hold it... and let them drop. Let your arms feel heavy and loose..."

Shoulders and Neck: "Shrug your shoulders up to your ears as high as they'll go... hold that tension... feel it in your neck and shoulders... and release. Let your shoulders drop all the way down..."

Face: "Scrunch up your whole face like you just bit into a lemon... squeeze your eyes, nose, mouth... hold it... and relax. Let your face go smooth and soft..."

Stomach: "Tighten your stomach muscles like you're bracing for something... make them hard as a board... hold... and relax. Let your stomach go soft..."

Legs and Feet: "Press your toes down and tighten your calves and thighs... feel the tension all through your legs... hold it... and release. Let your legs feel heavy..."

Full Body: "Now just notice your whole body... from your head all the way down to your toes... feeling heavy, warm, and relaxed... Take a few slow breaths and enjoy this feeling..."

Deep Breathing Exercises

▼

Balloon Breathing (ages 5-10): "Imagine your belly is a balloon. Put your hands on your belly. Breathe in slowly through your nose and fill the balloon... 1... 2... 3... 4. Now slowly let the air out through your mouth like you're blowing through a straw... 1... 2... 3... 4... 5... 6. Watch the balloon get smaller."

4-7-8 Breathing (ages 10+): "Breathe in through your nose for 4 counts... Hold your breath for 7 counts... Exhale completely through your mouth for 8 counts. Repeat 4 times."

Box Breathing (all ages): "Breathe in for 4 counts... Hold for 4 counts... Breathe out for 4 counts... Hold for 4 counts. Imagine tracing a square with each side."

5-Finger Breathing (all ages): "Hold one hand up with fingers spread. With the pointer finger of your other hand, trace up the outside of your thumb while breathing in... trace down the other side while breathing out. Continue for each finger."

Guided Imagery Prompts

▼

Safe Place: "Close your eyes and imagine a place where you feel completely safe and calm. It can be real or imaginary... Look around your safe place. What do you see?... What do you hear?... What do you feel under your feet or hands?... What does the air smell like?... Take a deep breath and let yourself feel how safe this place is..."

Container Exercise: "Imagine a strong container with a very secure lid. It can be anything: a safe, a treasure chest, a vault, a box. Make it any size you need... Now imagine taking the difficult thoughts or memories and placing them into the container, one at a time... Close the lid firmly. Lock it... You can come back and open this whenever you choose."

Light Stream: "Imagine a warm beam of light above you, any color you like... Let it slowly pour over the top of your head like warm honey... Feel it moving down through your body, bringing warmth and relaxation to every muscle it touches... Wherever there's tension or pain, the light dissolves it..."

DBT

Dialectical Behavior Therapy

Mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness skills

Mindfulness Skills

Core mindfulness teaches "What" skills (Observe, Describe, Participate) and "How" skills (Non-judgmentally, One-mindfully, Effectively).

Observe

Pay attention on purpose, to the present moment. Just notice without getting caught up in the experience.

Guided Exercise

▼

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding: Notice 5 things you can see. 4 things you can touch. 3 things you can hear. 2 things you can smell. 1 thing you can taste. Simply notice each one without judging.

Body Scan: Starting from the top of your head, slowly scan down through your body. Notice any sensations: warmth, coolness, tension, tingling, numbness. Don't try to change anything. Just observe.

Describe

Put words on the experience. Label what you observe. "I notice a thought that..." or "I'm feeling a sensation of..."

Guided Exercise

▼

Thought Labeling: For 2 minutes, notice each thought that arises and label it: "There's a worry thought." "There's a memory." "There's a planning thought." Don't follow the thought, just label and let it pass.

Emotion Labeling: When you notice an emotion, describe it factually: "I notice tightness in my chest. My breathing is faster. I would call this anxiety." Stick to observable facts.

Participate

Throw yourself completely into the current activity. Become one with the experience. Act intuitively from wise mind.

Guided Exercise

▼

Mindful Activity: Choose any routine activity (washing dishes, walking, eating). Do it with full attention. When your mind wanders, gently bring it back. Notice every sensation, movement, and moment.

Non-Judgmentally

See but don't evaluate. Take a nonjudgmental stance. Acknowledge the helpful and harmful without judging as "good" or "bad."

Guided Exercise

▼

Judgment Catching: For the next hour, notice every time you make a judgment (good/bad, right/wrong, should/shouldn't). Each time, rephrase it as a fact: "This is bad weather" becomes "It is raining." Count how many judgments you catch.

One-Mindfully

Do one thing at a time. When eating, eat. When worrying, worry. When planning, plan. Do each thing with full attention.

Guided Exercise

▼

Single-Tasking: Choose one task you normally multitask during. Do only that one thing for 10 minutes. Each time you start to do something else, notice it and return to the single task.

Effectively

Focus on what works. Do what needs to be done in each situation. Play by the rules. Act as skillfully as you can.

Guided Exercise

▼

Effectiveness Check: Before reacting to a situation, ask yourself: "What is my goal here? Will what I'm about to do get me closer to or further from that goal?" Choose the effective path, even if it means letting go of being "right."

Distress Tolerance Skills

Crisis survival strategies for when you cannot change the situation and need to get through it without making it worse.
T - I - P - P

TIPP Skills (Rapid Physiological Change)

Use when emotional arousal is extremely high (7+ out of 10). These change body chemistry fast.

  • Temperature: Hold ice cubes, splash cold water on face, or hold a cold pack on eyes/cheeks for 30 seconds. Activates the dive reflex.
  • Intense Exercise: Sprint, do jumping jacks, push-ups, or run stairs for 10-20 minutes. Burns off adrenaline.
  • Paced Breathing: Breathe out slower than you breathe in (e.g., in for 4, out for 8). Activates the parasympathetic nervous system.
  • Progressive (Paired) Muscle Relaxation: Tense and release muscle groups while breathing out, saying "relax" with each release.
S - T - O - P

STOP Skill

  • Stop: Freeze. Don't react. Don't move a muscle.
  • Take a step back: Take a breath. Don't let yourself act impulsively. Give yourself time.
  • Observe: Notice what's happening inside and around you. What are the facts?
  • Proceed mindfully: Act with awareness. Consider your goals. What will be effective?

IMPROVE the Moment

  • Imagery: Imagine relaxing scenes, a safe place, or coping well.
  • Meaning: Find purpose or meaning in the pain.
  • Prayer: Open yourself to a higher power, the universe, or wise mind.
  • Relaxation: Progressive muscle relaxation, hot bath, deep breathing.
  • One thing in the moment: Focus entirely on one task right now.
  • Vacation (brief): Give yourself a brief mental vacation. Take an hour off from adulting.
  • Encouragement: Cheerleading statements. "I can do this." "This will pass."

Use each sense to comfort yourself. Plan these in advance so they're ready during crisis.

Radical Acceptance

Accepting reality as it is, completely and totally, in this moment. Acceptance does not mean approval. It means acknowledging what is.

Emotion Regulation Skills

Skills for understanding and managing emotions, reducing vulnerability, and building positive experiences.
P - L - E - A - S - E

PLEASE Skills (Reducing Vulnerability)

Take care of your body to reduce emotional vulnerability:

  • Physical iLlness: Treat physical illness. See a doctor when needed.
  • Eating: Eat balanced meals. Not too much, not too little.
  • Avoid mood-altering substances: Stay off non-prescribed drugs and limit alcohol.
  • Sleep: Get adequate sleep. Keep a consistent sleep schedule.
  • Exercise: Get regular exercise. 20+ minutes most days.

Opposite Action

When the emotion doesn't fit the facts, or acting on the emotion isn't effective, do the opposite of the emotional urge. Do it ALL THE WAY.

A - B - C

ABC Skills (Building Positive Emotions)

  • Accumulate positives: Do pleasant things that are possible now. Plan for long-term values-based goals.
  • Build mastery: Do things that make you feel competent and effective. Gradually increase difficulty.
  • Cope ahead: Rehearse a plan ahead of time for handling difficult situations.

Interpersonal Effectiveness Skills

Skills for asking for what you want, saying no, and maintaining relationships and self-respect.
D - E - A - R   M - A - N

DEAR MAN (Getting What You Want)

Use when your priority is the OBJECTIVE (getting what you want or saying no).

  • Describe: Describe the current situation. Stick to the facts.
  • Express: Express your feelings and opinions about the situation. Use "I" statements.
  • Assert: Assert yourself by asking for what you want or saying no clearly.
  • Reinforce: Reinforce (reward) the person ahead of time by explaining the positive effects.
  • Mindful: Keep your focus on your objective. Don't be distracted.
  • Appear confident: Use a confident tone and posture. No stammering, whispering, looking down.
  • Negotiate: Be willing to give to get. Offer alternatives. Ask for input.
Practice Scenario:

"Your coworker keeps interrupting you in meetings. You want them to stop, but you also need to maintain a good working relationship. Script your DEAR MAN below:"

G - I - V - E

GIVE (Maintaining the Relationship)

Use when your priority is the RELATIONSHIP.

  • Gentle: Be gentle. No attacks, threats, or judging. No sneering or eye-rolling.
  • Interested: Act interested. Listen. Don't interrupt. Be patient.
  • Validate: Validate the other person's feelings, wants, difficulties, and opinions.
  • Easy manner: Use a little humor. Smile. Be light-hearted. Ease the person.
Practice Scenario:

"A friend is upset that you cancelled plans last minute. They're expressing frustration. Use GIVE to respond while maintaining the friendship."

F - A - S - T

FAST (Maintaining Self-Respect)

Use when your priority is SELF-RESPECT.

  • Fair: Be fair to yourself AND the other person.
  • Apologies (no unnecessary): Don't over-apologize. Don't apologize for being alive, having an opinion, or disagreeing.
  • Stick to values: Don't sell out your values or integrity for short-term gain.
  • Truthful: Don't lie, act helpless, or exaggerate. Stick to the truth.
Practice Scenario:

"Someone is pressuring you to lend them money again, and you've decided you can't keep doing it. Use FAST to maintain your self-respect while responding."

MI

Motivational Interviewing

OARS, change talk, decisional balance, readiness rulers, and common traps

OARS Quick Reference

The four core skills of Motivational Interviewing. Use throughout the conversation to evoke change talk.

O - Open Questions

Questions that can't be answered with "yes" or "no." They invite the client to elaborate and explore.

"What brings you in today?"

"How would you like things to be different?"

"What concerns do you have about your [drinking/use/behavior]?"

"What would your life look like if you made this change?"

"Help me understand what a typical day looks like for you."

"What have you tried before?"

A - Affirmations

Statements that recognize client strengths, effort, and values. They build confidence and reinforce change talk.

"It took a lot of courage to come here today."

"You've been through a lot, and you're still here working on this."

"The fact that you're thinking about change shows real strength."

"You clearly care a lot about your family."

"You showed real determination when you [specific example]."

"That's a creative solution you came up with."

R - Reflections

Reflecting back what the client says to show understanding. Simple reflections repeat/rephrase; complex reflections add meaning or feeling. Aim for 2:1 reflections to questions.

Simple: "So you're saying that mornings are the hardest."

Amplified: "So there's absolutely nothing good about your current situation."

Double-sided: "On one hand you enjoy drinking with friends, and on the other hand you're worried about your health."

Feeling: "It sounds like you feel stuck."

Reframe: "So your stubbornness is actually determination."

S - Summaries

Collecting reflections together. Linking, transitional, or recap summaries. They demonstrate listening and organize themes.

Collecting: "Let me see if I've got this right. You've mentioned..."

Linking: "What you said today connects to something you mentioned last week about..."

Transitional: "We've covered a lot today. You talked about... and... Where does that leave you?"

Change Talk Recognition: DARN-CAT

Listen for and reinforce these types of change talk. Earlier stages (DARN) lead to later commitment (CAT).

Preparatory Change Talk (DARN)

Desire: "I want to..." "I wish I could..." "I'd like to..."

"I wish I could stop smoking."

Ability: "I can..." "I could..." "I might be able to..."

"I was able to quit for 3 months before."

Reason: "I need to because..." "It would help if..."

"My kids need me to be healthy."

Need: "I have to..." "I must..." "Something has to change..."

"I can't keep living like this."

Mobilizing Change Talk (CAT)

Commitment: "I will..." "I'm going to..." "I promise..."

"I'm going to call the clinic tomorrow."

Activation: "I'm ready to..." "I'm willing to..." "I'm prepared to..."

"I'm ready to try medication."

Taking Steps: "I already..." "I started..." "This week I..."

"I threw away the bottles last night."

Common MI Traps to Avoid

Patterns that increase resistance and work against the MI spirit. Recognizing them is the first step to avoiding them.

The Righting Reflex

The natural desire to fix what seems wrong with people. Telling clients why and how they should change. This is the most common trap.
"You really should cut back on drinking. It's clearly causing problems."
Instead: "What role does drinking play in your life right now?"

The Expert Trap

Positioning yourself as the expert with all the answers. This puts the client in a passive role and undermines autonomy.
"What you need to do is go to 90 meetings in 90 days."
Instead: "What do you think might work for you?"

The Labeling Trap

Insisting on diagnostic labels. Arguing about whether someone "is an alcoholic" generates sustain talk and discord.
"Until you admit you're an addict, we can't make progress."
Instead: "Labels aren't important to me. What matters is how you feel about things."

The Question-Answer Trap

Asking too many questions in a row, creating an interrogation feel. Client gives short answers and disengages.
"How much do you drink? How often? When did you start? Does it run in your family?"
Instead: Use more reflections. Aim for a 2:1 reflection-to-question ratio.

The Premature Focus Trap

Jumping to the "problem" before the client is ready. Focusing on your agenda rather than theirs.
"Let's talk about your drug use." (in the first 5 minutes)
Instead: "What would you most like to talk about today?"

The Blaming Trap

Spending time figuring out whose fault the problem is. Clients become defensive regardless of who is being blamed.
"Your parents' drinking is what caused your problems."
Instead: "Blame isn't really useful here. What would you like to do going forward?"
SF

Solution-Focused Brief Therapy

Miracle question, scaling questions, and exception finding techniques

Miracle Question Guide

The miracle question helps clients envision a preferred future. Deliver it slowly and with genuine curiosity.
The Miracle Question:
"I'm going to ask you a strange question. Suppose tonight, while you're sleeping, a miracle happens. The miracle is that the problems that brought you here today are solved. But you don't know the miracle has happened because you were asleep. When you wake up tomorrow morning, what would be the first small sign that tells you something is different?"
Follow-up probes (ask with curiosity, not interrogation):
  • "What would you notice first?"
  • "What would be different about how you feel?"
  • "Who else would notice the miracle happened? What would they see different about you?"
  • "What would your [partner/child/friend] say is different?"
  • "What would you be doing differently?"
  • "On a scale of 0-10, where 10 is the miracle day, where are you now?"
  • "Are there any pieces of the miracle that are already happening, even a little?"
  • "What would be the very next thing you'd notice?"

Scaling Questions

Scaling questions make abstract concepts concrete and measurable. Adapt the template for different domains.

Overall Progress

▼

"On a scale of 0 to 10, where 10 is when the problems that brought you here are resolved, and 0 is the worst it's ever been, where would you say you are today?"

Follow-ups:

  • "What tells you it's a [number] and not a 0?"
  • "What would one step higher look like?"
  • "What would you be doing differently at a [number+1]?"
  • "What have you done to get to a [number]?"

Coping / Safety

▼

"On a scale of 0 to 10, where 10 means you're confident you can stay safe this week, where are you?"

Follow-ups:

  • "What's keeping you at a [number] rather than lower?"
  • "What resources do you have that help you cope?"
  • "If something happened to lower that number, what would you do?"

Motivation

▼

Exception Finding

Identify times when the problem doesn't occur or is less severe. Exceptions contain the seeds of solutions.
Exception-finding questions:
  • "Are there times when the problem doesn't happen or is less intense?"
  • "What's different about those times?"
  • "Tell me about a recent time when things went well."
  • "What were you doing differently then?"
  • "Who was around? Where were you?"
  • "What did you do to make that happen?"
  • "How did you manage to do that?"
  • "What would it take to make that happen more often?"
  • "What would [important person] say you did differently?"
  • "On a scale of 0-10, how often do these exceptions happen now?"

Progress Notes Best Practices

Progress notes document each clinical encounter and serve as the primary legal and clinical record. Well-written notes protect the clinician, support continuity of care, and demonstrate medical necessity.

What to Include
  • Client presentation: Appearance, affect, mood, behavior, speech, and thought process observed during the session
  • Interventions used: Specific therapeutic techniques applied (e.g., cognitive restructuring, exposure, motivational interviewing, psychoeducation)
  • Client response: How the client responded to interventions, including engagement level, emotional reactions, insights gained, and skill demonstration
  • Treatment plan alignment: Connection between session content and treatment plan goals/objectives
  • Risk assessment updates: Any changes in suicidality, homicidality, self-harm, or safety concerns, including safety plan review if applicable
  • Plan for next session: Topics to address, homework assignments, follow-up items, referrals initiated
  • Relevant quotes: Direct client quotes that capture key statements (especially regarding safety, progress, or setbacks)
  • Coordination of care: Contacts with other providers, family involvement, case management activities
What to Avoid
  • Judgmental or subjective language: Avoid words like "manipulative," "non-compliant," "dramatic," "attention-seeking" -- use objective behavioral descriptions instead
  • Excessive detail about third parties: Limit information about non-clients to what is clinically relevant; do not document others' mental health histories
  • Unsubstantiated diagnoses: Do not reference diagnoses not formally assessed or documented in the treatment plan
  • Personal opinions or countertransference: Process reactions in supervision, not in the clinical record
  • Abbreviations without established meaning: Use only standard clinical abbreviations accepted by your agency
  • Vague statements: "Client did well" or "Good session" -- instead, specify what progress looked like
  • Copy-pasting from previous notes: Each note should reflect the unique content of that session
  • Information protected under 42 CFR Part 2: Substance use treatment records require separate consent for disclosure

SOAP Note Format Guide

The SOAP format is one of the most widely used documentation structures in healthcare settings. It provides a logical, sequential record of the clinical encounter.

S — Subjective
What the client reports. Document the client's own words, complaints, concerns, and self-reported symptoms. Include their perspective on progress, medication effects, stressors, and functioning since the last session. Use direct quotes when they capture critical information.

Example: Client reports, "I had two panic attacks this week, but I was able to use the breathing exercises and they passed faster." Reports sleep improved to 6 hours/night (up from 4). Denies suicidal ideation.
O — Objective
What the clinician directly observes. Document observable data: appearance, affect, behavior, psychomotor activity, speech (rate, volume, tone), eye contact, orientation, thought process, and any standardized measure results (e.g., PHQ-9 = 12).

Example: Client arrived on time, casually dressed, groomed. Affect was congruent, mood described as "better." Speech was normal rate and volume. Eye contact was appropriate. No psychomotor agitation or retardation observed. PHQ-9 score: 12 (moderate).
A — Assessment
Clinical formulation and professional judgment. Synthesize subjective and objective data into a clinical impression. Address progress toward treatment plan goals, diagnostic impressions, risk level, and clinical conceptualization. Note any changes in functioning, severity, or presentation.

Example: Client demonstrates incremental progress toward Goal 1 (coping skill development). Panic attack frequency has decreased and client is successfully applying diaphragmatic breathing. Moderate depressive symptoms persist. Risk remains low -- no SI/HI. Continue current treatment approach with emphasis on cognitive restructuring.
P — Plan
Next steps for treatment. Document the plan for ongoing care, including: next appointment date/time, homework assignments, referrals made or pending, coordination with other providers, medication management follow-up, and any modifications to the treatment plan.

Example: Continue weekly individual therapy sessions. Homework: Complete thought record daily, practice progressive muscle relaxation before bed. Referral to psychiatry for medication evaluation submitted. Next session: 07/01/2026 at 2:00 PM.

DAP Note Format Guide

DAP notes combine subjective and objective information into a single "Data" section, making them slightly more concise than SOAP notes. This format is common in community mental health and counseling settings.

D — Data
All relevant information from the session. Combines both what the client reports (subjective) and what the clinician observes (objective). Include presenting concerns, session content, interventions used, client responses, notable quotes, observed affect/behavior, and any screening results.

Example: Client attended scheduled session. Reported increased anxiety related to upcoming custody hearing. Described racing thoughts and difficulty sleeping (3-4 hours/night). Presented with constricted affect and psychomotor restlessness. Clinician utilized grounding techniques (5-4-3-2-1 sensory exercise) and cognitive restructuring to address catastrophic thinking. Client was able to identify 2 alternative outcomes and reported decreased distress by end of session (SUD 8 to 4). GAD-7 administered: score of 14 (moderate).
A — Assessment
Clinical interpretation and progress evaluation. Provide professional analysis of the data, including progress toward treatment goals, diagnostic impressions, functional changes, and risk assessment.

Example: Client continues to meet criteria for Generalized Anxiety Disorder. Situational stressor (custody hearing) is exacerbating baseline symptoms. Client demonstrated ability to apply grounding techniques in session, suggesting skill acquisition is progressing (Goal 2, Objective A). Risk assessment: low -- denies SI/HI, no plan or intent. Protective factors include strong relationship with children and engagement in treatment.
P — Plan
Forward-looking treatment actions. Outline upcoming interventions, homework, referrals, and session scheduling.

Example: Continue weekly sessions with focus on anxiety management pre-hearing. Assign daily practice of grounding exercise and thought record. Coordinate with attorney regarding court date confirmation. Follow up on sleep hygiene handout. Next session: 07/03/2026 at 10:00 AM.

BIRP Note Format Guide

BIRP notes focus on observable behavior and the specific interventions employed. This format is particularly useful for demonstrating active treatment and clinician skill application.

B — Behavior
Observable client behavior and presentation. Document what the clinician directly observes: appearance, affect, mood, behavior, statements, interactions, and symptom presentation. Include both verbal and non-verbal behavior.

Example: Client arrived 10 minutes late, appeared fatigued with dark circles under eyes. Affect was flat, spoke in low monotone. Reported "I don't see the point anymore" when discussing job search. Became tearful when discussing relationship with ex-partner. Denied active suicidal ideation but endorsed passive death wish ("Sometimes I wish I wouldn't wake up").
I — Intervention
Specific clinical interventions used. Detail the therapeutic techniques, strategies, and clinical actions the clinician employed during the session. Be specific about the modality and technique.

Example: Clinician conducted risk assessment using C-SSRS (screen version). Utilized motivational interviewing techniques (reflective listening, affirmation) to explore ambivalence about job search. Applied behavioral activation to identify low-effort pleasurable activities. Reviewed and updated safety plan (Steps 1-3). Provided psychoeducation on sleep hygiene and its relationship to mood.
R — Response
How the client responded to interventions. Document the client's reaction to therapeutic interventions, including engagement level, emotional responses, skill acquisition, insight, and any resistance or barriers.

Example: Client initially minimized symptoms but became more engaged following reflective listening. Identified 3 low-effort activities (walking dog, listening to music, texting friend) and agreed to attempt one daily. Collaborated on safety plan update; identified new coping strategy (calling sister). C-SSRS: passive ideation without plan or intent. Client contracted for safety and agreed to call 988 if SI escalates.
P — Plan
Next steps and follow-up. Include scheduling, homework, referrals, and treatment plan updates.

Example: Increase session frequency to twice weekly given elevated risk. Homework: complete behavioral activation log daily. Coordinate with psychiatrist Dr. Martinez regarding medication adjustment. Client to complete PHQ-9 at next visit. Updated safety plan provided to client. Next session: 07/02/2026 at 11:00 AM.

Common Documentation Errors

These errors frequently appear in audits, peer reviews, and malpractice proceedings. Being aware of them helps maintain defensible, high-quality clinical records.

Late Entries

Notes completed days or weeks after the session. Best practice is to document within 24 hours. If late, label as "Late Entry" with the actual date of service and the date the note was written. Never backdate a note.

Copy-Paste Errors

Reusing previous notes without updating content. Results in identical notes across sessions, incorrect dates, wrong client names, and documentation that does not reflect actual session content. Each note must be unique to the encounter.

Insufficient Detail

Notes that are too brief to demonstrate medical necessity or active treatment. "Client discussed feelings" does not convey what interventions were used or how the client responded. Be specific about what happened.

Over-Documentation

Excessive narrative that includes irrelevant personal details, full session transcripts, or information that could be harmful if subpoenaed. Document what is clinically relevant and necessary for continuity.

Missing Signatures & Dates

Unsigned or undated notes may be considered invalid in audits and legal proceedings. Every entry must include the clinician's signature, credentials, date of service, and date signed. Co-signatures from supervisors must be timely.

Diagnosis-Service Mismatch

Billing for services that do not align with the documented diagnosis or treatment plan. The CPT code, diagnosis, and session content must be congruent. Ensure the note supports the level of service billed.

Altered Records

Using white-out, erasing, or modifying entries without an addendum. In EHR systems, editing a signed note without proper audit trail. Corrections should be made via addendum with original entry intact.

Missing Risk Documentation

Failing to document risk assessment, especially when risk indicators are present. Every session should include at minimum a brief safety screen. Elevated risk requires detailed documentation of assessment, clinical decision-making, and actions taken.

Legal Considerations in Clinical Documentation

Clinical records are legal documents. Understanding the legal framework protects both clinician and client. This reference covers New Hampshire-specific requirements where applicable.

Subpoena-Proof Documentation
  • Write every note as if it will be read aloud in court -- because it might be
  • Use objective, behavioral language rather than subjective characterizations
  • Document clinical reasoning for treatment decisions, especially regarding safety
  • Include rationale for deviations from standard care or treatment plan modifications
  • Record consultations with supervisors and colleagues, including names and recommendations
  • Ensure notes are internally consistent -- contradictions undermine credibility
  • Document informed consent discussions, especially for higher-risk interventions
Client Access to Records
  • Under HIPAA, clients have the right to access their mental health records
  • Psychotherapy notes (as defined by HIPAA -- notes kept separate from the medical record) have additional protections
  • Clinicians may deny access only if there is a substantial risk of harm to the client or another person
  • Requests should be fulfilled within 30 days (one 30-day extension permitted)
  • A reasonable cost-based fee may be charged for copies
Amendment Requests
  • Clients have the right to request amendments to their records under HIPAA
  • The provider may accept or deny the request; if denied, the client can submit a statement of disagreement
  • Original entries must never be deleted or altered -- amendments are addenda
  • Document the date of the amendment request, the decision, and the rationale
Record Retention (New Hampshire)
  • Adults: NH does not have a single statutory retention period for mental health records; best practice is a minimum of 7 years after the last date of service, though some agencies require 10 years
  • Minors: Records should be retained for at least 7 years after the client reaches age 18 (i.e., until age 25), or longer per agency policy
  • After clinician death or practice closure: Records must be maintained or transferred per NH Board of Mental Health Practice rules; clients must be notified
  • Medicare/Medicaid: Federal programs may require longer retention periods (typically 7-10 years from date of service)
  • Litigation hold: If litigation is anticipated or pending, do not destroy any relevant records regardless of retention schedule
Key Statutes & Regulations
  • HIPAA (45 CFR Parts 160, 164): Privacy, security, and breach notification rules
  • 42 CFR Part 2: Confidentiality of substance use disorder patient records (stricter than HIPAA)
  • NH RSA 329-B: Mental Health Practice Act -- governs licensed mental health practitioners in NH
  • NH RSA 330-A: Allied Health Professionals -- applicable to LCMHCs and other licensed counselors
  • NH RSA 169-C:29-34: Mandatory reporting of child abuse and neglect
  • Tarasoff / NH duty to warn: NH recognizes a duty to warn identifiable potential victims of serious harm

Documentation Quick Tips

Practical tips to strengthen your clinical documentation immediately.

1
Use direct quotes. When a client makes a significant statement -- especially regarding safety, progress, or treatment goals -- capture their exact words in quotation marks. Quotes add specificity and credibility that paraphrasing cannot match.
2
Be specific about interventions. Instead of "Provided CBT," write "Utilized cognitive restructuring to challenge client's belief that 'everyone is judging me' by examining evidence for and against the thought." Name the technique and describe how it was applied.
3
Document risk assessments every session. Even a brief statement such as "Client denies suicidal or homicidal ideation; no acute safety concerns at this time" demonstrates that risk was evaluated. When risk is elevated, document the assessment tool used, findings, clinical decision-making, and actions taken.
4
Note no-shows and late cancellations. Document every missed appointment with the date, time, whether the client was contacted, the outcome of the contact attempt, and any clinical concerns (e.g., client with elevated risk who missed session). This creates continuity in the record.
5
Connect every session to the treatment plan. Reference which goal or objective was addressed. This demonstrates medical necessity and active, purposeful treatment rather than "just talking."
6
Document coordination of care. Every phone call to a prescriber, email to a case manager, or consultation with a colleague should be documented with date, method, participants, and outcome. These notes demonstrate comprehensive care.
7
Write for your successor. If another clinician had to take over this case tomorrow, would they understand the client's current status, treatment approach, and next steps from your notes? If not, add more context.
8
Document within 24 hours. Memory degrades quickly. The sooner you write your note, the more accurate and detailed it will be. Set aside time at the end of each day to complete documentation.

1 Writing a Strong Prior Authorization Request

1 Lead with medical necessity. Clearly state why the specific level of care is required. Use clinical language: "Client presents with [diagnosis] of [severity], evidenced by [specific symptoms/functional impairments], necessitating [service level] to [clinical goal]."
2 Document failed lower levels of care. If requesting a higher level, describe what lower-level services have been tried, how long, and why they were insufficient. Payers want to see step-up justification.
3 Include objective measures. Reference PHQ-9, GAD-7, CSSRS, LOCUS, ASAM scores. Quantified data is more persuasive than narrative alone. Include score trends over time if available.
4 Be specific about functional impairment. Describe how symptoms affect daily life: unable to maintain employment, cannot care for children, withdrawn from all social activity, ADL deficits. Payers approve based on functional need, not diagnosis alone.
5 State the treatment plan clearly. Include specific modalities to be used, frequency/duration requested, measurable goals, and expected length of treatment. Vague requests get denied.
6 Include risk factors. Document suicidality, self-harm, substance use, recent hospitalizations, medication non-adherence, homelessness, or other factors that elevate clinical urgency.
7 Submit on time and completely. Incomplete submissions are the #1 cause of delays. Use the payer's specific form if one exists. Include all required attachments (treatment plan, assessment, scores).

2 What to Include in Every Auth Request

  • Client demographics and insurance information
  • Referring/requesting provider information and credentials
  • Current DSM-5 diagnosis (primary and all relevant secondary)
  • Current medications and prescriber information
  • Presenting symptoms with onset, duration, and severity
  • Functional impairment description (work, school, ADLs, relationships)
  • Risk assessment (SI/HI/SH, recent crises, protective factors)
  • Treatment history (what has been tried, duration, outcomes)
  • Current treatment plan with measurable goals and objectives
  • Specific service requested (CPT code, frequency, duration, number of units)
  • Clinical justification matching payer's level-of-care criteria
  • Standardized assessment scores (PHQ-9, GAD-7, LOCUS, ASAM, etc.)
  • Discharge/step-down criteria and plan

3 Appeal Process for Denials

If a prior authorization is denied, you have the right to appeal. Federal and state law (including NH mental health parity) protect access to medically necessary behavioral health services.

  1. Review the denial letter carefully. Identify the specific reason for denial (medical necessity, missing information, wrong code, out-of-network, etc.). The denial must cite the clinical criteria used.
  2. Request a peer-to-peer review. Most payers allow the treating clinician to speak directly with the payer's reviewing clinician. This is often the fastest route to reversal. Request within 5 business days of denial.
  3. File a formal written appeal (Level 1). Include the denial reference number, additional clinical documentation addressing the specific denial reason, updated assessments, and a detailed letter of medical necessity.
  4. Request an external review (Level 2) if internal appeal is denied. NH law allows external review by an independent review organization (IRO). The payer must inform you of this right in the denial letter.
  5. File a complaint with the NH Insurance Department if you believe the denial violates parity law or is otherwise improper. NH Insurance Dept: (800) 852-3416.
  6. Continue providing clinically necessary services while the appeal is pending if patient safety requires it. Document the clinical necessity and the pending appeal status.

4 NH Mental Health Parity Law

New Hampshire RSA 417-E requires insurers to provide coverage for mental health and substance use disorders that is no more restrictive than coverage for medical/surgical conditions. Key provisions:

  • Financial requirements (copays, deductibles, coinsurance) cannot be more restrictive for MH/SUD than for medical/surgical
  • Treatment limitations (visit limits, prior auth requirements) must be comparable to those applied to medical/surgical benefits
  • Non-quantitative treatment limitations (medical necessity criteria, step therapy, fail-first protocols) must be applied no more stringently than for medical benefits
  • Network adequacy must be maintained for behavioral health at comparable levels to medical/surgical
  • Payers must disclose the criteria used for medical necessity determinations upon request
Parity tip: If a payer denies MH services that would be covered for an analogous medical condition (e.g., denying residential MH treatment while covering inpatient rehab for medical conditions), this may be a parity violation. Document the comparison and include it in your appeal. Contact the NH Insurance Department for parity complaints: (800) 852-3416.

HIPAA Quick Reference

Essential HIPAA compliance reference for clinical practice, including NH-specific privacy laws

Protected Health Information (PHI)

PHI is any individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a covered entity or its business associate, in any form or medium. PHI includes:

  • Demographic data: Names, dates (birth, admission, discharge, death), phone/fax numbers, email addresses, Social Security numbers, medical record numbers, health plan beneficiary numbers
  • Geographic data: Street address, city, county, ZIP code (anything more specific than state)
  • Clinical information: Diagnoses, treatment plans, progress notes, medication lists, lab results, imaging records, referral information
  • Financial information: Insurance information, billing records, payment history, prior authorization details
  • Digital identifiers: IP addresses, device serial numbers, biometric identifiers (fingerprints, voiceprints), full-face photos and comparable images
  • Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code
Key principle: If information can be used alone or in combination with other data to identify an individual AND it relates to their health condition, provision of healthcare, or payment for healthcare, it is PHI.

PHI includes information in ANY form: paper records, electronic records (ePHI), verbal communications, photographs, video/audio recordings, and text messages.

Minimum Necessary Standard

Covered entities must make reasonable efforts to limit PHI to the minimum necessary to accomplish the intended purpose of the use, disclosure, or request.

  • Internal uses: Identify persons who need access to PHI and limit access based on role. A billing clerk does not need access to therapy notes.
  • Routine disclosures: Develop standard protocols for routine or recurring requests (e.g., insurance claims always include diagnosis and CPT, not full clinical notes).
  • Non-routine disclosures: Develop criteria for reviewing individual requests on a case-by-case basis.
  • Requests to other entities: When requesting PHI from another organization, limit the request to what is reasonably necessary.
Exceptions to minimum necessary: Treatment disclosures between providers, disclosures to the individual, disclosures with a valid authorization, disclosures required by law, and disclosures required by HHS for compliance investigations.

Practical tip: When faxing records, sending referrals, or coordinating care, ask yourself: "Does the recipient actually need ALL of this information, or just a subset?"

Client Rights Under HIPAA

Right to Access (45 CFR 164.524)
  • Clients have the right to inspect and obtain a copy of their PHI in designated record sets
  • Must respond within 30 days (one 30-day extension allowed with written notice)
  • May charge a reasonable, cost-based fee for copies
  • May deny access only in limited circumstances (e.g., psychotherapy notes, information compiled for legal proceedings, information that may endanger the individual or another person)
  • Client can request records in electronic format if maintained electronically
Right to Amendment (45 CFR 164.526)
  • Clients may request amendments to PHI they believe is inaccurate or incomplete
  • Must respond within 60 days (one 30-day extension allowed)
  • May deny if the information was not created by the entity, is not part of the designated record set, is accurate and complete, or is not available for access
  • If denied, client can submit a statement of disagreement to be included in the record
Right to Accounting of Disclosures (45 CFR 164.528)
  • Clients can request a list of disclosures made in the prior 6 years
  • Excludes disclosures for treatment, payment, and healthcare operations
  • Excludes disclosures to the individual, with authorization, for facility directory, to family/friends involved in care, for national security
  • Must include: date, recipient, description of information, purpose
Right to Request Restrictions (45 CFR 164.522)
  • Clients can request restrictions on uses and disclosures for treatment, payment, or healthcare operations
  • The covered entity is not required to agree to a restriction, except: if the disclosure is to a health plan for payment/operations and the PHI relates to a service paid out-of-pocket in full, the restriction must be honored
  • Clients can request confidential communications (e.g., "Only contact me on my cell phone, not my home phone")

Breach Notification Requirements

A breach is an impermissible use or disclosure of PHI that compromises the security or privacy of the information.

What Constitutes a Breach
  • Sending PHI to the wrong fax number, email, or mailing address
  • Stolen or lost unencrypted laptop, phone, or USB drive containing ePHI
  • Unauthorized employee access to medical records ("snooping")
  • Improper disposal of paper records (not shredded) or electronic media (not wiped)
  • Ransomware or hacking incidents affecting systems with ePHI
  • Verbal disclosure of PHI in a public area where unauthorized persons can overhear
Notification requirements:
Individual notice: Within 60 days of discovery, notify affected individuals in writing.
HHS notice: If fewer than 500 individuals affected, report annually. If 500+, notify HHS within 60 days.
Media notice: If 500+ individuals in a single state/jurisdiction, notify prominent media outlets within 60 days.
Breach Risk Assessment (4 Factors)
  • Nature and extent of PHI: Types of identifiers and likelihood of re-identification
  • Unauthorized person: Who improperly used or received the PHI
  • Whether PHI was actually acquired or viewed: (vs. just exposed to possibility)
  • Extent of mitigation: Steps taken to reduce risk of harm

De-identification Standards

De-identified health information is not PHI and is not subject to HIPAA restrictions. HIPAA provides two methods:

Safe Harbor Method (45 CFR 164.514(b))

Remove the following 18 identifiers:

  • Names
  • Geographic data smaller than a state (street address, city, county, ZIP codes with population <20,000)
  • All dates (except year) directly related to an individual, all ages over 89
  • Phone numbers, fax numbers, email addresses
  • Social Security numbers, medical record numbers, health plan beneficiary numbers
  • Account numbers, certificate/license numbers
  • Vehicle identifiers and serial numbers, device identifiers and serial numbers
  • Web URLs, IP addresses
  • Biometric identifiers (fingerprints, voiceprints)
  • Full-face photographs and comparable images
  • Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code
Expert Determination Method (45 CFR 164.514(a))

A qualified statistical/scientific expert determines that the risk of identifying an individual from the information is "very small" and documents the methods and results.

Clinical practice tip: When using case examples for training, presentations, or consultation, use the Safe Harbor method: change names, remove dates, alter demographics, and modify details enough that no reasonable person could identify the client.

Business Associate Agreements (BAAs)

A business associate is any person or entity that performs functions or activities on behalf of a covered entity that involve the use or disclosure of PHI.

Common Business Associates in Clinical Practice
  • EHR vendors: Any electronic health record system that stores or processes PHI
  • Billing/claims processing companies: Third-party billers, clearinghouses
  • Telehealth platforms: Video conferencing tools used for clinical sessions
  • Cloud storage services: If used to store any documents containing PHI
  • Answering services: If they receive PHI (client names, appointment details)
  • IT service providers: If they have access to systems containing ePHI
  • Shredding companies: Services that destroy paper records containing PHI
  • Transcription services: If they process clinical dictation containing PHI
BAA requirements: A written BAA must be in place BEFORE the business associate receives any PHI. The BAA must specify permissible uses/disclosures, require safeguards, require breach notification, and require return or destruction of PHI upon termination. Without a BAA, sharing PHI with a vendor is a HIPAA violation.

Social Media & Technology Guidelines

Do NOT
  • Post any information about clients on social media, even without names (combined details can identify)
  • Use personal email (Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook.com) to send or receive PHI unless encrypted and covered by a BAA
  • Use standard SMS/text messaging to communicate PHI with clients
  • Take photos or recordings of clients without explicit written consent
  • Accept or initiate friend/follow requests with current clients on social media
  • Search for clients on social media or Google (unless documented clinical justification)
  • Use consumer-grade apps (Dropbox personal, Google Drive personal, iMessage) for PHI without a BAA
  • Leave PHI visible on computer screens in public areas
  • Discuss client information in elevators, hallways, cafeterias, or other public spaces
Best Practices
  • Use only HIPAA-compliant, BAA-covered platforms for telehealth (e.g., Doxy.me, SimplePractice, Zoom for Healthcare)
  • Enable encryption on all devices that may contain ePHI
  • Use secure messaging platforms with BAAs for client communication
  • Implement automatic screen locks (15 minutes or less) on all devices
  • Use strong, unique passwords and enable multi-factor authentication
  • Review social media privacy settings regularly
  • Develop a social media policy and discuss digital boundaries in informed consent

Common HIPAA Violations in Clinical Practice

  • Hallway conversations: Discussing client details where others can overhear (waiting rooms, hallways, break rooms, restaurants)
  • Unattended records: Leaving paper charts, fax transmissions, or printed records in unsecured areas
  • Screen visibility: EHR screens visible to non-authorized persons (other clients, cleaning staff, visitors)
  • Improper disposal: Throwing paper records in regular trash instead of shredding; not wiping electronic media before disposal
  • Lost/stolen devices: Unencrypted laptops, phones, or USB drives containing ePHI
  • Wrong recipient: Faxing to wrong number, emailing wrong address, mailing to wrong person
  • Unauthorized access: Employees accessing records of clients they are not treating ("curiosity browsing")
  • Social media posts: Posting about client cases even with altered details (combined details can identify)
  • Verbal disclosures: Confirming a person is a client to someone without authorization (including acknowledging you know someone in public)
  • Shared workstations: Not logging out of EHR between users
  • Missing BAAs: Using vendors/platforms for PHI without business associate agreements
  • Insufficient training: Staff not trained on HIPAA requirements annually
Penalties: HIPAA violations can result in civil monetary penalties from $100 to $50,000+ per violation (up to $1.5M per year per violation category), criminal penalties including imprisonment, and state-level enforcement actions. Individual clinicians can be held personally liable.

New Hampshire Privacy Laws Beyond HIPAA

New Hampshire has additional privacy protections that may be more restrictive than HIPAA. When state law is more protective, it takes precedence.

RSA 329:26 — Privileged Communications
  • Communications between a licensed mental health professional and client are privileged
  • Cannot be disclosed without client consent except in limited circumstances
  • Exceptions: mandatory reporting (abuse, neglect), duty to warn (Tarasoff-type), court orders
RSA 135-C:19-a — Confidentiality of Mental Health Records
  • Mental health records maintained by the state mental health system have additional protections
  • Disclosure requires written, informed consent specifying: information to be disclosed, purpose, recipient, expiration date
  • Consent is revocable at any time
RSA 169-C:29 — Child Abuse Reporting
  • Mandatory reporters (including all licensed mental health professionals) must report suspected child abuse or neglect
  • Reports go to DCYF (Division for Children, Youth, and Families)
  • Good faith reporters have civil and criminal immunity
  • Failure to report is a misdemeanor
RSA 161-F:46 — Adult Protective Services
  • Mandatory duty to report suspected abuse, neglect, or exploitation of adults who are incapacitated
  • Reports go to BEAS (Bureau of Elderly and Adult Services)
RSA 141-G — HIV/AIDS Confidentiality
  • HIV test results have enhanced confidentiality protections in NH
  • Specific written consent required for disclosure of HIV-related information
  • More restrictive than general HIPAA requirements
General rule for NH clinicians: When in doubt, apply the more restrictive standard. If both HIPAA and NH law apply, follow whichever provides greater protection for the client. Consult your agency's compliance officer or legal counsel for complex situations.

42 CFR Part 2 Guide

Substance use disorder treatment record protections — more restrictive than HIPAA

What 42 CFR Part 2 Protects

Critical: 42 CFR Part 2 protects records related to substance use disorder (SUD) treatment from a Part 2 program. These protections are MORE restrictive than HIPAA. Part 2 records cannot be disclosed the way other health records can, even for treatment, payment, or healthcare operations, without proper consent.

Protected information includes:

  • Records of the identity, diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment of any patient maintained in connection with an SUD treatment program
  • Any information that would identify a person as having or having had a substance use disorder
  • Information about a patient's attendance at or participation in an SUD treatment program
  • Any record created by a Part 2 program that identifies a patient as being or having been diagnosed with an SUD

Part 2 applies regardless of format: paper, electronic, verbal, or any other form of communication.

Why the extra protection? Congress enacted 42 CFR Part 2 because fear of prosecution, discrimination, or loss of employment, housing, or child custody could deter people from seeking SUD treatment. These protections exist to remove barriers to treatment access.

Part 2 Consent Requirements

Consent for disclosure of Part 2 records must be:

  • In writing (electronic consent may be permitted under the 2024 final rule)
  • Specific: Must name or describe each person or entity to whom disclosure will be made
  • Purpose-limited: Must state the specific purpose for each disclosure
  • Revocable: Patient can revoke consent at any time (orally or in writing)
  • Time-limited: Must contain an expiration date or event
Required Elements of Part 2 Consent
  • Name of the patient
  • Name or general description of the program making the disclosure
  • Name of the individual or organization receiving the disclosure
  • Specific purpose for the disclosure
  • How much and what kind of information will be disclosed
  • Patient's right to revoke consent and how to do so
  • Expiration date, event, or condition
  • Date the consent is signed
  • Signature of the patient (or authorized representative)
HIPAA general authorization is NOT sufficient for Part 2 records. A standard HIPAA release of information does not meet Part 2 consent requirements. Part 2 consent forms must include ALL elements listed above and must specifically reference SUD treatment records.

Re-disclosure Prohibition

Re-disclosure prohibition: Any person or entity that receives Part 2 records is prohibited from re-disclosing those records to any other person or entity unless a new, separate consent is obtained from the patient or an exception applies.

All Part 2 disclosures must include the following re-disclosure notice:

"This record which has been disclosed to you is protected by federal confidentiality rules (42 CFR Part 2). The federal rules prohibit you from making any further disclosure of information in this record that identifies a patient as having or having had a substance use disorder either directly, by reference to publicly available information, or through verification of such identification by another person unless further disclosure is expressly permitted by the written consent of the individual whose information is being disclosed or as otherwise permitted by 42 CFR Part 2. A general authorization for the release of medical or other information is NOT sufficient for this purpose (see 42 CFR Part 2.31). The federal rules restrict any use of the information to investigate or prosecute with regard to a crime any patient with a substance use disorder, except as provided at 42 CFR 2.12(c)(5) and 2.65."

This notice must accompany every disclosure of Part 2 information, whether verbal, written, or electronic.

What Qualifies as a "Part 2 Program"

Part 2 applies to any program that:

  • Is federally assisted (receives federal funding, tax-exempt status, CMS-authorized DEA registration to dispense controlled substances for SUD treatment, or is conducted by a federal department/agency)
  • AND holds itself out as providing, and provides, substance use disorder diagnosis, treatment, or referral for treatment
Common Part 2 Programs
  • Inpatient/residential SUD treatment facilities
  • Outpatient SUD treatment programs
  • Opioid Treatment Programs (OTPs / methadone clinics)
  • Community mental health centers that provide SUD treatment as an identified unit or with staff whose primary function is SUD treatment
  • Any employee assistance program (EAP) that provides SUD diagnosis, treatment, or referral
  • Programs receiving SAMHSA block grants
When Part 2 Does NOT Apply
  • A general medical practice that treats a patient who happens to have a SUD (unless the provider holds itself out as providing SUD treatment)
  • A private practitioner who is not federally assisted and does not hold out as an SUD treatment provider
  • Emergency departments (for emergency care, not SUD treatment specifically)
  • Information about SUD obtained outside of a Part 2 program (e.g., a client discloses substance use to their general therapist who is not part of a Part 2 program)

Exceptions to Part 2 Consent Requirements

Part 2 records may be disclosed without patient consent only in these limited circumstances:

1. Medical Emergencies
  • Disclosure to medical personnel who need the information to treat a condition posing an immediate threat to the patient's health
  • Must be documented within 72 hours: patient name, who received the info, why the emergency, what was disclosed
2. Audit and Evaluation
  • Disclosures to entities performing audits or evaluations of the program (e.g., federal/state oversight agencies, third-party payers with an agreement, quality improvement organizations)
  • Patient-identifying information may not be copied or removed except by authorized agents
3. Qualified Service Organization Agreements (QSOAs)
  • Disclosure to entities providing services to the program (similar to business associates under HIPAA)
  • Requires a written QSOA specifying the services and prohibiting re-disclosure
4. Court Orders (Not Subpoenas)
  • A court order under Part 2's specific procedures (not a standard subpoena or court order)
  • The court must find "good cause" after balancing public interest vs. patient injury
  • A regular subpoena alone is NOT sufficient to compel disclosure of Part 2 records
5. Crimes on Premises / Against Program Personnel
  • Information about suspected crimes committed on program premises or against program personnel may be reported to law enforcement
  • Limited to the circumstances of the incident only
6. Child Abuse Reporting
  • Part 2 does NOT prevent mandatory child abuse and neglect reporting under state law
  • However, the report should be limited to the facts of the abuse/neglect, not the patient's SUD treatment status, unless directly relevant
7. Research
  • Disclosure for scientific research with proper safeguards and IRB approval
  • Researcher must have a research protocol protecting patient identity

2024 Final Rule Changes (Effective February 2024)

The Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023 (CARES Act 3.0) and the subsequent 2024 final rule brought significant changes to 42 CFR Part 2:

Key Changes
  • Single consent for TPO: Patients may provide a single consent for disclosures for Treatment, Payment, and Health Care Operations (TPO), rather than requiring separate, entity-specific consents. This aligns Part 2 more closely with HIPAA for TPO purposes.
  • HIPAA protections apply: Once Part 2 records are disclosed pursuant to a TPO consent, they may be further used or disclosed in accordance with HIPAA (including the Privacy Rule and Breach Notification Rule), but additional Part 2 protections still apply.
  • Anti-discrimination protections: SUD records disclosed under the new framework cannot be used to discriminate against patients in employment, housing, access to courts, social services, or other areas.
  • Continued prohibition on use in criminal proceedings: Part 2 records still cannot be used to investigate or prosecute patients, even with the new consent framework.
  • Electronic consent: Consent may be provided in electronic form.
  • Segmentation challenges: Health IT systems may need to implement segmentation to appropriately tag and restrict Part 2 data.
Important: The 2024 rule does NOT eliminate Part 2 protections. It modifies the consent framework to reduce barriers to care coordination while maintaining core protections against use of SUD records in criminal proceedings and ensuring anti-discrimination safeguards. Clinicians should consult their compliance department for agency-specific implementation guidance.

Practical Tips for Clinicians Working with Co-occurring Clients

  • Separate consent forms: Use a Part 2-specific consent form for SUD treatment records, separate from your general HIPAA authorization. Even with the 2024 rule changes, ensure your consent meets all Part 2 elements.
  • Be careful with referral letters: When referring a client to another provider, do not include SUD treatment information unless you have a valid Part 2 consent. A general mental health referral should not mention SUD treatment from a Part 2 program.
  • Coordination of care: When coordinating with other providers about a co-occurring client, obtain separate Part 2 consent for the SUD-related information. You can discuss the mental health aspects under HIPAA but need Part 2 consent for SUD aspects.
  • Documentation practices: Consider maintaining SUD treatment notes separately from general mental health notes in your EHR, or ensure your EHR can properly segment Part 2 records.
  • Emergency situations: In a true medical emergency, you can share Part 2 information with treating medical personnel without consent. Document the emergency within 72 hours.
  • Insurance/billing: Under the 2024 rule, a TPO consent may cover billing disclosures. Prior to implementing this, confirm with your compliance department.
  • Court proceedings: If you receive a subpoena for SUD treatment records, do NOT comply without first consulting legal counsel. A standard subpoena is not sufficient under Part 2.
  • Staff training: Ensure all staff who handle Part 2 records understand the re-disclosure prohibition and the required notice language.
