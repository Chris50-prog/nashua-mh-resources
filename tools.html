Cognitive Distortions Reference

15 common thinking errors. Use during session to help clients identify patterns. Click any card to expand the challenging question.

1. All-or-Nothing Thinking

Seeing things in black-and-white categories. If performance falls short of perfect, it's total failure.
"If I don't get an A, I'm a complete failure."
Ask: "Is there a middle ground? Can something be partly successful?"

2. Overgeneralization

Seeing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern of defeat, using words like "always" or "never."
"I always mess up interviews. I'll never get a job."
Ask: "Has there ever been a time this wasn't true? What are the exceptions?"

3. Mental Filter

Picking out a single negative detail and dwelling on it exclusively, filtering out all positive aspects.
"My supervisor mentioned one typo in my 20-page report. The whole thing was terrible."
Ask: "What positives might you be filtering out? What would someone else notice?"

4. Disqualifying the Positive

Rejecting positive experiences by insisting they "don't count" for some reason.
"She only said something nice because she felt sorry for me."
Ask: "What evidence is there that this positive feedback isn't genuine?"

5. Jumping to Conclusions

Making negative interpretations without evidence. Includes mind reading and fortune telling.
"He didn't text back. He must be angry at me."
Ask: "What are other possible explanations? Do you have actual evidence?"

6. Magnification / Minimization

Exaggerating the importance of negatives or shrinking the importance of positives (the "binocular trick").
"Making one mistake at work is catastrophic" but "The raise I got is no big deal."
Ask: "If a friend told you this, would you see it the same way?"

7. Emotional Reasoning

Assuming that negative feelings reflect the way things really are: "I feel it, therefore it must be true."
"I feel stupid, so I must actually be stupid."
Ask: "Is there a difference between how you feel and what the facts show?"

8. Should Statements

Using "should," "must," or "ought to" in ways that create guilt when directed at self, anger at others.
"I should be able to handle this without help."
Ask: "What would happen if you replaced 'should' with 'I would prefer'?"

9. Labeling

An extreme form of overgeneralization. Attaching a fixed label instead of describing the specific behavior.
"I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake on that task."
Ask: "Is this label accurate all the time? What would a fairer description be?"

10. Personalization

Seeing yourself as the cause of some external negative event for which you were not primarily responsible.
"The team lost because I wasn't good enough."
Ask: "What other factors contributed? Was this really all about you?"

11. Fortune Telling

Predicting that things will turn out badly and treating the prediction as established fact.
"I just know the presentation will go terribly."
Ask: "How many times have your predictions actually come true?"

12. Mind Reading

Concluding that someone is reacting negatively to you without checking or asking.
"She thinks I'm incompetent. I can tell."
Ask: "Have you checked this out directly? What else might explain their behavior?"

13. Catastrophizing

Expecting disaster. "What if" thinking that escalates to the worst possible outcome.
"What if I make a mistake and get fired and lose my house?"
Ask: "What is the most likely outcome? How have you coped with similar situations before?"

14. Blame

Holding others entirely responsible for your pain, or the reverse: blaming yourself for others' problems.
"It's all her fault I feel this way" or "Everything is my fault."
Ask: "How much of this is within your control? How much is within theirs?"

15. Always Being Right

Needing to prove that your opinions and actions are correct at the expense of others' feelings.
"I don't care if it hurts her. I was right and she needs to know it."
Ask: "What matters more here: being right, or maintaining the relationship?"

Socratic Questioning Guide

Use these questions during session to help clients examine and challenge their automatic thoughts.

Clarifying the Thought

▼
  • "What exactly do you mean when you say ___?"
  • "Can you tell me more about that thought?"
  • "When you say ___, what picture comes to mind?"
  • "How strongly do you believe that right now, on a 0-100 scale?"
  • "Is this a thought, a feeling, or both?"

Examining the Evidence

▼
  • "What evidence supports this thought?"
  • "What evidence goes against it?"
  • "Are you basing this on facts or feelings?"
  • "Have you had experiences that show this thought is not always true?"
  • "If your best friend had this thought, what evidence would you point out to them?"

Considering Alternatives

▼
  • "Is there another way to look at this situation?"
  • "What would you tell a friend who had this thought?"
  • "What are all the possible explanations?"
  • "If you weren't feeling ___, how might you interpret this differently?"
  • "Are there any positives in this situation that you might be overlooking?"

Examining the Impact

▼
  • "What happens when you believe this thought?"
  • "How does this thought affect your behavior?"
  • "Does this thought help you or hold you back?"
  • "What would change if you didn't hold this belief?"
  • "What's the worst that could happen? How likely is that? Could you cope with it?"

Testing the Thought

▼
  • "How could we test whether this thought is accurate?"
  • "What would convince you this thought was wrong?"
  • "What has happened in the past when you thought this way?"
  • "If this thought is true, what's the next step? If it's not, what's the next step?"
  • "What's a small experiment you could try this week?"
TF

Trauma-Focused CBT

PRACTICE components, relaxation techniques, and safety planning for trauma treatment

PRACTICE Components Reference

The eight core components of TF-CBT. Sessions typically follow this sequence, though clinical judgment guides pacing.

P Psychoeducation

Normalizing trauma responses for the child and caregiver. Providing information about the specific type of trauma, prevalence, and common reactions. Helps reduce shame and self-blame.
Key techniques: Age-appropriate trauma education, handouts, books, normalizing statements, caregiver parallel sessions
Sample prompts: "Many kids who have been through what you experienced have similar feelings." / "It's not unusual to feel scared or angry after something like that." / "Your body was trying to protect you."

R Relaxation Skills

Teaching the child and caregiver specific techniques to manage physiological stress responses. These skills are used throughout treatment and especially during trauma narrative work.
Key techniques: Focused breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery, mindfulness, yoga-based movements
Sample prompts: "Let's practice our balloon breathing." / "Tighten your fists really tight... now let them go like spaghetti." / "Picture your safe place."

A Affective Modulation

Building the child's ability to identify, express, and manage difficult emotions. Essential foundation before trauma narrative work begins.
Key techniques: Feeling identification, affect rating scales, positive self-talk, thought interruption, problem-solving, anger management
Sample prompts: "What does your body feel like when you're angry?" / "On a scale of 1-10, how strong is that feeling?" / "What could you say to yourself when you notice that feeling coming?"

C Cognitive Coping

Introducing the cognitive triangle (thoughts-feelings-behaviors) and helping the child understand how thoughts influence emotions and actions. Not yet trauma-specific.
Key techniques: Cognitive triangle exercises, thought-feeling-behavior connections, accurate vs. inaccurate thoughts, best friend role-play
Sample prompts: "If you thought 'this test will be easy,' how would you feel? What if you thought 'I'll definitely fail'?" / "Are there other ways to think about that?"

T Trauma Narrative

Gradual exposure through creating a detailed account of the traumatic experience(s). This is the central component. The narrative is developed over multiple sessions with increasing detail.
Key techniques: Gradual exposure, narrative writing/drawing/dictation, cognitive processing of trauma-specific thoughts, chapters approach
Sample prompts: "We're going to write your story together, at your pace." / "What happened next?" / "What were you thinking right at that moment?" / "What do you know now that you didn't know then?"

I In Vivo Mastery

Gradual real-world exposure to trauma reminders that are objectively safe but currently avoided. Uses a fear hierarchy. Not always needed if avoidance is minimal.
Key techniques: Fear hierarchy construction, graduated exposure tasks, in-session and between-session practice, parent coaching for support
Sample prompts: "Let's make a list of things that remind you of what happened, from least scary to most." / "You practiced going to the park this week. What was that like?"

C Conjoint Sessions

Bringing the child and caregiver together to share the trauma narrative and practice communication skills. Prepares both parties separately before conjoint work.
Key techniques: Caregiver preparation, narrative sharing, therapeutic communication practice, praise and validation coaching
Sample prompts: (To caregiver) "Your child has been working hard on their story and wants to share it with you." / (To child) "You get to decide how much to share."

E Enhancing Safety

Safety planning, body safety skills, and relapse prevention. Addresses real safety concerns and empowers the child to recognize and respond to unsafe situations.
Key techniques: Safety planning, assertiveness training, body safety education, identifying safe adults, bullying prevention, digital safety
Sample prompts: "Who are three adults you could go to if you felt unsafe?" / "What's the difference between a safe touch and an unsafe touch?" / "Let's practice what you could say."

Relaxation Techniques

Scripts and guides for teaching relaxation skills. Can be used in session or sent home as practice assignments.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation Script

▼

Instructions: Read slowly in a calm, steady voice. Pause 5-10 seconds at each "..." mark. The full exercise takes approximately 15 minutes.

Hands and Arms: "Make a fist with both hands. Squeeze tight... feel the tension in your hands and forearms... hold it... and now release. Let your hands go completely limp. Notice the difference between tension and relaxation..."

Arms and Shoulders: "Now stretch your arms out straight and push your hands toward the ceiling... feel the pull in your arms and shoulders... hold it... and let them drop. Let your arms feel heavy and loose..."

Shoulders and Neck: "Shrug your shoulders up to your ears as high as they'll go... hold that tension... feel it in your neck and shoulders... and release. Let your shoulders drop all the way down..."

Face: "Scrunch up your whole face like you just bit into a lemon... squeeze your eyes, nose, mouth... hold it... and relax. Let your face go smooth and soft..."

Stomach: "Tighten your stomach muscles like you're bracing for something... make them hard as a board... hold... and relax. Let your stomach go soft..."

Legs and Feet: "Press your toes down and tighten your calves and thighs... feel the tension all through your legs... hold it... and release. Let your legs feel heavy..."

Full Body: "Now just notice your whole body... from your head all the way down to your toes... feeling heavy, warm, and relaxed... Take a few slow breaths and enjoy this feeling..."

Deep Breathing Exercises

▼

Balloon Breathing (ages 5-10): "Imagine your belly is a balloon. Put your hands on your belly. Breathe in slowly through your nose and fill the balloon... 1... 2... 3... 4. Now slowly let the air out through your mouth like you're blowing through a straw... 1... 2... 3... 4... 5... 6. Watch the balloon get smaller."

4-7-8 Breathing (ages 10+): "Breathe in through your nose for 4 counts... Hold your breath for 7 counts... Exhale completely through your mouth for 8 counts. Repeat 4 times."

Box Breathing (all ages): "Breathe in for 4 counts... Hold for 4 counts... Breathe out for 4 counts... Hold for 4 counts. Imagine tracing a square with each side."

5-Finger Breathing (all ages): "Hold one hand up with fingers spread. With the pointer finger of your other hand, trace up the outside of your thumb while breathing in... trace down the other side while breathing out. Continue for each finger."

Guided Imagery Prompts

▼

Safe Place: "Close your eyes and imagine a place where you feel completely safe and calm. It can be real or imaginary... Look around your safe place. What do you see?... What do you hear?... What do you feel under your feet or hands?... What does the air smell like?... Take a deep breath and let yourself feel how safe this place is..."

Container Exercise: "Imagine a strong container with a very secure lid. It can be anything: a safe, a treasure chest, a vault, a box. Make it any size you need... Now imagine taking the difficult thoughts or memories and placing them into the container, one at a time... Close the lid firmly. Lock it... You can come back and open this whenever you choose."

Light Stream: "Imagine a warm beam of light above you, any color you like... Let it slowly pour over the top of your head like warm honey... Feel it moving down through your body, bringing warmth and relaxation to every muscle it touches... Wherever there's tension or pain, the light dissolves it..."

DBT

Dialectical Behavior Therapy

Mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness skills

Mindfulness Skills

Core mindfulness teaches "What" skills (Observe, Describe, Participate) and "How" skills (Non-judgmentally, One-mindfully, Effectively).

Observe

Pay attention on purpose, to the present moment. Just notice without getting caught up in the experience.

Guided Exercise

▼

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding: Notice 5 things you can see. 4 things you can touch. 3 things you can hear. 2 things you can smell. 1 thing you can taste. Simply notice each one without judging.

Body Scan: Starting from the top of your head, slowly scan down through your body. Notice any sensations: warmth, coolness, tension, tingling, numbness. Don't try to change anything. Just observe.

Describe

Put words on the experience. Label what you observe. "I notice a thought that..." or "I'm feeling a sensation of..."

Guided Exercise

▼

Thought Labeling: For 2 minutes, notice each thought that arises and label it: "There's a worry thought." "There's a memory." "There's a planning thought." Don't follow the thought, just label and let it pass.

Emotion Labeling: When you notice an emotion, describe it factually: "I notice tightness in my chest. My breathing is faster. I would call this anxiety." Stick to observable facts.

Participate

Throw yourself completely into the current activity. Become one with the experience. Act intuitively from wise mind.

Guided Exercise

▼

Mindful Activity: Choose any routine activity (washing dishes, walking, eating). Do it with full attention. When your mind wanders, gently bring it back. Notice every sensation, movement, and moment.

Non-Judgmentally

See but don't evaluate. Take a nonjudgmental stance. Acknowledge the helpful and harmful without judging as "good" or "bad."

Guided Exercise

▼

Judgment Catching: For the next hour, notice every time you make a judgment (good/bad, right/wrong, should/shouldn't). Each time, rephrase it as a fact: "This is bad weather" becomes "It is raining." Count how many judgments you catch.

One-Mindfully

Do one thing at a time. When eating, eat. When worrying, worry. When planning, plan. Do each thing with full attention.

Guided Exercise

▼

Single-Tasking: Choose one task you normally multitask during. Do only that one thing for 10 minutes. Each time you start to do something else, notice it and return to the single task.

Effectively

Focus on what works. Do what needs to be done in each situation. Play by the rules. Act as skillfully as you can.

Guided Exercise

▼

Effectiveness Check: Before reacting to a situation, ask yourself: "What is my goal here? Will what I'm about to do get me closer to or further from that goal?" Choose the effective path, even if it means letting go of being "right."

Distress Tolerance Skills

Crisis survival strategies for when you cannot change the situation and need to get through it without making it worse.
T - I - P - P

TIPP Skills (Rapid Physiological Change)

Use when emotional arousal is extremely high (7+ out of 10). These change body chemistry fast.

  • Temperature: Hold ice cubes, splash cold water on face, or hold a cold pack on eyes/cheeks for 30 seconds. Activates the dive reflex.
  • Intense Exercise: Sprint, do jumping jacks, push-ups, or run stairs for 10-20 minutes. Burns off adrenaline.
  • Paced Breathing: Breathe out slower than you breathe in (e.g., in for 4, out for 8). Activates the parasympathetic nervous system.
  • Progressive (Paired) Muscle Relaxation: Tense and release muscle groups while breathing out, saying "relax" with each release.
S - T - O - P

STOP Skill

  • Stop: Freeze. Don't react. Don't move a muscle.
  • Take a step back: Take a breath. Don't let yourself act impulsively. Give yourself time.
  • Observe: Notice what's happening inside and around you. What are the facts?
  • Proceed mindfully: Act with awareness. Consider your goals. What will be effective?

I - M - P - R - O - V - E

IMPROVE the Moment

  • Imagery: Imagine relaxing scenes, a safe place, or coping well.
  • Meaning: Find purpose or meaning in the pain.
  • Prayer: Open yourself to a higher power, the universe, or wise mind.
  • Relaxation: Progressive muscle relaxation, hot bath, deep breathing.
  • One thing in the moment: Focus entirely on one task right now.
  • Vacation (brief): Give yourself a brief mental vacation. Take an hour off from adulting.
  • Encouragement: Cheerleading statements. "I can do this." "This will pass."

Self-Soothe with Five Senses

Use each sense to comfort yourself. Plan these in advance so they're ready during crisis.

Radical Acceptance

Accepting reality as it is, completely and totally, in this moment. Acceptance does not mean approval. It means acknowledging what is.

Emotion Regulation Skills

Skills for understanding and managing emotions, reducing vulnerability, and building positive experiences.
P - L - E - A - S - E

PLEASE Skills (Reducing Vulnerability)

Take care of your body to reduce emotional vulnerability:

  • Physical iLlness: Treat physical illness. See a doctor when needed.
  • Eating: Eat balanced meals. Not too much, not too little.
  • Avoid mood-altering substances: Stay off non-prescribed drugs and limit alcohol.
  • Sleep: Get adequate sleep. Keep a consistent sleep schedule.
  • Exercise: Get regular exercise. 20+ minutes most days.

Opposite Action

When the emotion doesn't fit the facts, or acting on the emotion isn't effective, do the opposite of the emotional urge. Do it ALL THE WAY.

A - B - C

ABC Skills (Building Positive Emotions)

  • Accumulate positives: Do pleasant things that are possible now. Plan for long-term values-based goals.
  • Build mastery: Do things that make you feel competent and effective. Gradually increase difficulty.
  • Cope ahead: Rehearse a plan ahead of time for handling difficult situations.

Interpersonal Effectiveness Skills

Skills for asking for what you want, saying no, and maintaining relationships and self-respect.
D - E - A - R   M - A - N

DEAR MAN (Getting What You Want)

Use when your priority is the OBJECTIVE (getting what you want or saying no).

  • Describe: Describe the current situation. Stick to the facts.
  • Express: Express your feelings and opinions about the situation. Use "I" statements.
  • Assert: Assert yourself by asking for what you want or saying no clearly.
  • Reinforce: Reinforce (reward) the person ahead of time by explaining the positive effects.
  • Mindful: Keep your focus on your objective. Don't be distracted.
  • Appear confident: Use a confident tone and posture. No stammering, whispering, looking down.
  • Negotiate: Be willing to give to get. Offer alternatives. Ask for input.
Practice Scenario:

"Your coworker keeps interrupting you in meetings. You want them to stop, but you also need to maintain a good working relationship. Script your DEAR MAN below:"

G - I - V - E

GIVE (Maintaining the Relationship)

Use when your priority is the RELATIONSHIP.

  • Gentle: Be gentle. No attacks, threats, or judging. No sneering or eye-rolling.
  • Interested: Act interested. Listen. Don't interrupt. Be patient.
  • Validate: Validate the other person's feelings, wants, difficulties, and opinions.
  • Easy manner: Use a little humor. Smile. Be light-hearted. Ease the person.
Practice Scenario:

"A friend is upset that you cancelled plans last minute. They're expressing frustration. Use GIVE to respond while maintaining the friendship."

F - A - S - T

FAST (Maintaining Self-Respect)

Use when your priority is SELF-RESPECT.

  • Fair: Be fair to yourself AND the other person.
  • Apologies (no unnecessary): Don't over-apologize. Don't apologize for being alive, having an opinion, or disagreeing.
  • Stick to values: Don't sell out your values or integrity for short-term gain.
  • Truthful: Don't lie, act helpless, or exaggerate. Stick to the truth.
Practice Scenario:

"Someone is pressuring you to lend them money again, and you've decided you can't keep doing it. Use FAST to maintain your self-respect while responding."

MI

Motivational Interviewing

OARS, change talk, decisional balance, readiness rulers, and common traps

OARS Quick Reference

The four core skills of Motivational Interviewing. Use throughout the conversation to evoke change talk.

O - Open Questions

Questions that can't be answered with "yes" or "no." They invite the client to elaborate and explore.

"What brings you in today?"

"How would you like things to be different?"

"What concerns do you have about your [drinking/use/behavior]?"

"What would your life look like if you made this change?"

"Help me understand what a typical day looks like for you."

"What have you tried before?"

A - Affirmations

Statements that recognize client strengths, effort, and values. They build confidence and reinforce change talk.

"It took a lot of courage to come here today."

"You've been through a lot, and you're still here working on this."

"The fact that you're thinking about change shows real strength."

"You clearly care a lot about your family."

"You showed real determination when you [specific example]."

"That's a creative solution you came up with."

R - Reflections

Reflecting back what the client says to show understanding. Simple reflections repeat/rephrase; complex reflections add meaning or feeling. Aim for 2:1 reflections to questions.

Simple: "So you're saying that mornings are the hardest."

Amplified: "So there's absolutely nothing good about your current situation."

Double-sided: "On one hand you enjoy drinking with friends, and on the other hand you're worried about your health."

Feeling: "It sounds like you feel stuck."

Reframe: "So your stubbornness is actually determination."

S - Summaries

Collecting reflections together. Linking, transitional, or recap summaries. They demonstrate listening and organize themes.

Collecting: "Let me see if I've got this right. You've mentioned..."

Linking: "What you said today connects to something you mentioned last week about..."

Transitional: "We've covered a lot today. You talked about... and... Where does that leave you?"

Change Talk Recognition: DARN-CAT

Listen for and reinforce these types of change talk. Earlier stages (DARN) lead to later commitment (CAT).

Preparatory Change Talk (DARN)

Desire: "I want to..." "I wish I could..." "I'd like to..."

"I wish I could stop smoking."

Ability: "I can..." "I could..." "I might be able to..."

"I was able to quit for 3 months before."

Reason: "I need to because..." "It would help if..."

"My kids need me to be healthy."

Need: "I have to..." "I must..." "Something has to change..."

"I can't keep living like this."

Mobilizing Change Talk (CAT)

Commitment: "I will..." "I'm going to..." "I promise..."

"I'm going to call the clinic tomorrow."

Activation: "I'm ready to..." "I'm willing to..." "I'm prepared to..."

"I'm ready to try medication."

Taking Steps: "I already..." "I started..." "This week I..."

"I threw away the bottles last night."

Common MI Traps to Avoid

Patterns that increase resistance and work against the MI spirit. Recognizing them is the first step to avoiding them.

The Righting Reflex

The natural desire to fix what seems wrong with people. Telling clients why and how they should change. This is the most common trap.
"You really should cut back on drinking. It's clearly causing problems."
Instead: "What role does drinking play in your life right now?"

The Expert Trap

Positioning yourself as the expert with all the answers. This puts the client in a passive role and undermines autonomy.
"What you need to do is go to 90 meetings in 90 days."
Instead: "What do you think might work for you?"

The Labeling Trap

Insisting on diagnostic labels. Arguing about whether someone "is an alcoholic" generates sustain talk and discord.
"Until you admit you're an addict, we can't make progress."
Instead: "Labels aren't important to me. What matters is how you feel about things."

The Question-Answer Trap

Asking too many questions in a row, creating an interrogation feel. Client gives short answers and disengages.
"How much do you drink? How often? When did you start? Does it run in your family?"
Instead: Use more reflections. Aim for a 2:1 reflection-to-question ratio.

The Premature Focus Trap

Jumping to the "problem" before the client is ready. Focusing on your agenda rather than theirs.
"Let's talk about your drug use." (in the first 5 minutes)
Instead: "What would you most like to talk about today?"

The Blaming Trap

Spending time figuring out whose fault the problem is. Clients become defensive regardless of who is being blamed.
"Your parents' drinking is what caused your problems."
Instead: "Blame isn't really useful here. What would you like to do going forward?"
SF

Solution-Focused Brief Therapy

Miracle question, scaling questions, and exception finding techniques

Miracle Question Guide

The miracle question helps clients envision a preferred future. Deliver it slowly and with genuine curiosity.
The Miracle Question:
"I'm going to ask you a strange question. Suppose tonight, while you're sleeping, a miracle happens. The miracle is that the problems that brought you here today are solved. But you don't know the miracle has happened because you were asleep. When you wake up tomorrow morning, what would be the first small sign that tells you something is different?"
Follow-up probes (ask with curiosity, not interrogation):
  • "What would you notice first?"
  • "What would be different about how you feel?"
  • "Who else would notice the miracle happened? What would they see different about you?"
  • "What would your [partner/child/friend] say is different?"
  • "What would you be doing differently?"
  • "On a scale of 0-10, where 10 is the miracle day, where are you now?"
  • "Are there any pieces of the miracle that are already happening, even a little?"
  • "What would be the very next thing you'd notice?"

Scaling Questions

Scaling questions make abstract concepts concrete and measurable. Adapt the template for different domains.

Overall Progress

▼

"On a scale of 0 to 10, where 10 is when the problems that brought you here are resolved, and 0 is the worst it's ever been, where would you say you are today?"

Follow-ups:

  • "What tells you it's a [number] and not a 0?"
  • "What would one step higher look like?"
  • "What would you be doing differently at a [number+1]?"
  • "What have you done to get to a [number]?"

Coping / Safety

▼

"On a scale of 0 to 10, where 10 means you're confident you can stay safe this week, where are you?"

Follow-ups:

  • "What's keeping you at a [number] rather than lower?"
  • "What resources do you have that help you cope?"
  • "If something happened to lower that number, what would you do?"

Motivation

▼

"On a scale of 0 to 10, how motivated are you to work on this right now?"

Follow-ups:

  • "What makes it a [number] and not lower?"
  • "What would need to happen for your motivation to go up?"
  • "When was the last time it was higher?"

Relationship Satisfaction

▼

"On a scale of 0 to 10, where 10 is the best your relationship could be, where would you place it today?"

Follow-ups:

  • "Where would your partner/friend/family member rate it?"
  • "What's one thing that would move it up half a point?"
  • "What was it at its highest? What was different then?"

Exception Finding

Identify times when the problem doesn't occur or is less severe. Exceptions contain the seeds of solutions.
Exception-finding questions:
  • "Are there times when the problem doesn't happen or is less intense?"
  • "What's different about those times?"
  • "Tell me about a recent time when things went well."
  • "What were you doing differently then?"
  • "Who was around? Where were you?"
  • "What did you do to make that happen?"
  • "How did you manage to do that?"

Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) — Key Screening Questions

These are the triage screening questions from the C-SSRS. For full administration, use the complete validated instrument. This is a reference aid, not a replacement for training.
Important: Any "yes" to questions 3-5 requires immediate clinical assessment and action. Any "yes" to questions 4 or 5 is considered high risk.
# Question Response Risk Level
1 Wish to be Dead: "Have you wished you were dead or wished you could go to sleep and not wake up?" If yes, ask Q2
2 Suicidal Thoughts: "Have you actually had any thoughts of killing yourself?" If yes, ask Q3-5
3 Suicidal Thoughts with Method: "Have you been thinking about how you might do this?" Moderate risk
4 Suicidal Intent (without plan): "Have you had these thoughts and had some intention of acting on them?" High risk
5 Suicidal Intent with Plan: "Have you started to work out or worked out the details of how to kill yourself? Do you intend to carry out this plan?" High risk
6 Suicidal Behavior (Lifetime): "Have you ever done anything, started to do anything, or prepared to do anything to end your life?" If yes, inquire about recency
