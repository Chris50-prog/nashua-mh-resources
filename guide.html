Immediate Danger

CRITICAL — Act Now
Call 911 immediately. Do not delay. If weapons are present, active self-harm is occurring, or violence is happening, law enforcement and EMS are needed for scene safety.
Ensure your own safety first. Do not put yourself in physical danger. Remove yourself from the immediate area if necessary. Do not attempt to physically intervene.
Contact your supervisor immediately or the on-call supervisor if after hours. Inform them of the situation and the 911 call.
If safe, stay on scene and keep others away. Keep the client engaged verbally if possible. Do not approach weapons or attempt to disarm. Wait for emergency responders.
Call NH Rapid Response once the immediate danger is contained for crisis follow-up and MCRT dispatch if needed.
Document everything as soon as safely possible: timeline of events, what you observed, who was contacted, and actions taken.

Suicidal Ideation with Plan/Means

CRITICAL — Immediate Intervention
Do not leave the client alone. Stay with them or ensure someone is present at all times until the crisis is resolved.
Initiate a full safety assessment. Determine: specific plan, access to means, timeline/intent, prior attempts, protective factors. Use the Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) if available.
Call NH Rapid Response at 1-833-710-6477 to request Mobile Crisis Response Team (MCRT) dispatch. Provide all assessment findings.
Restrict access to means. Work with the client and available supports to remove or secure firearms, medications, sharps, or other identified means. This is the single most evidence-based suicide prevention intervention.
Contact your supervisor and inform them of the situation and your assessment findings.
Consider IEA (Involuntary Emergency Admission). If the client refuses voluntary treatment and meets criteria, transport to the nearest ED: Southern NH Medical Center ED, 8 Prospect St, Nashua. Only a physician/APRN at the ED can initiate the IEA.
Document the assessment, risk level, interventions, and rationale for clinical decisions.

Suicidal Ideation without Plan

HIGH — Intervention Needed
Complete a thorough safety assessment. Even without a plan, assess: frequency of thoughts, duration, intensity, prior history, substance use, recent losses, and protective factors.
Collaboratively create or update a Safety Plan. Use the Stanley-Brown model: warning signs, internal coping, social contacts, crisis contacts, means restriction, reason for living. See the Safety Plan guide below for full instructions.
Means restriction counseling. Even without a specific plan, reducing access to lethal means is protective. Discuss firearms, medications, and other potential means.
Increase frequency of contact. Schedule a follow-up within 24-48 hours. Consider increasing session frequency. Provide crisis numbers for between sessions.
Consult your supervisor. Discuss the case, your assessment, and the plan going forward. Document the consultation.
Consider medication evaluation if the client is not currently on medications, or contact the prescriber if current medications may need adjustment.
Document the assessment, safety plan, risk level, and plan for follow-up.

Homicidal Ideation

CRITICAL — Duty to Warn Assessment
Assess the threat thoroughly. Determine: is there a specific identifiable target? Is there a plan? Does the client have access to means? Is there intent and capability? What is the timeline?
Contact your supervisor immediately. Homicidal ideation triggers Duty to Warn obligations. Do not make this assessment alone. Consult on whether the Tarasoff/Duty to Warn threshold is met.
If Duty to Warn is triggered: Notify the identifiable potential victim(s) and notify law enforcement. In NH, the duty to warn applies when a client communicates a serious threat of physical violence against a reasonably identifiable victim.
If immediate danger exists, call 911. Do not wait to complete an assessment if the threat is imminent.
Consider hospitalization. If the client is acutely homicidal and refuses voluntary treatment, consider IEA through the ED. Contact NH Rapid Response for MCRT if appropriate.
Document extensively. Record the threat, your assessment, who was consulted, what actions were taken and why, and the rationale for your clinical decisions regarding Duty to Warn.
911 (if immediate danger) 1-833-710-6477 NH Rapid Response (603) 889-6147 GNMHC Crisis

Psychotic Episode

HIGH — De-escalation + Medical Assessment
Ensure safety. Assess whether the client is a danger to self or others. If yes, refer to the Immediate Danger protocol above. If not, proceed with de-escalation.
De-escalate. Speak calmly, use simple and direct language, do not argue with delusions or hallucinations, validate the client's experience without confirming false beliefs ("I can see this is very distressing for you"), reduce stimulation, maintain a safe distance.
Assess for medical causes. New-onset psychosis, sudden changes, or atypical presentations may indicate a medical emergency: substance intoxication/withdrawal, head injury, infection, metabolic disorder, medication reaction. If medical cause is suspected, transport to the ED.
Contact the prescriber. If the client has an existing prescriber, call to report the episode and request guidance. Medication adjustment or emergency medication may be needed.
Call NH Rapid Response at 1-833-710-6477 for MCRT dispatch if the client is in the community and needs on-site crisis assessment.
Consider ER evaluation. If the episode is severe, the client is not responding to de-escalation, or there is concern for medical causes, transport to Southern NH Medical Center ED. Call ahead at (603) 577-2540.
Notify supervisor and document the episode, your observations, interventions, and disposition.
1-833-710-6477 NH Rapid Response (603) 577-2540 SNHMC ED (603) 889-6147 GNMHC Crisis

Substance Use Crisis / Overdose Risk

CRITICAL — Medical Emergency
Call 911 immediately if overdose is suspected. Signs: unresponsive, slow/stopped breathing, blue lips or fingernails, pinpoint pupils (opioids), seizures, chest pain. Do not wait to be sure — call 911.
Administer Narcan (naloxone) if available and opioid overdose is suspected. Narcan can be given intranasally or via injection. It only works on opioid overdoses but will not harm the person if the overdose is not opioid-related. A second dose may be needed after 2-3 minutes.
Place the person in the recovery position (on their side) to prevent choking if they vomit. Do not leave them alone. Monitor breathing until EMS arrives.
Reassure about NH's Good Samaritan Law. NH RSA 318-B:28-b provides limited immunity from prosecution for persons who seek medical assistance for a drug overdose. The person experiencing the overdose and the person calling for help are both protected.
If not an active overdose but acute intoxication/withdrawal: Assess for dangerous withdrawal symptoms (alcohol/benzo withdrawal can be fatal: seizures, delirium tremens). Transport to the ER if medically unstable. Contact NH Doorway at (603) 836-6540 for treatment placement.
Post-crisis follow-up: Connect the client with SUD treatment through NH Doorway. Ensure the client and their contacts have Narcan. Provide harm reduction resources.

Child Abuse/Neglect Disclosure

HIGH — Mandated Reporting Required
You are a mandated reporter. Under NH RSA 169-C:29, you are legally required to report any suspected child abuse or neglect. You do NOT need proof — reasonable suspicion is the threshold. Failure to report is a misdemeanor.
If the child is in immediate danger, call 911 first. Then proceed with the DCYF report.
Call DCYF Central Intake at 1-800-894-5533. Available 24/7/365. Identify yourself as a mandated reporter. Provide all known information: child's name, age, address, parent/caregiver names, nature of the abuse/neglect, how you became aware, and any safety concerns.
Get a report number and the intake worker's name. Ask whether the report was accepted for assessment or investigation.
Do NOT investigate or interview the child in detail. That is DCYF's job. A forensic interview through the Child Advocacy Center is the gold standard. Do not contact the alleged perpetrator.
Document your report in the clinical record: date, time, intake worker name, report number, what you reported, and any instructions given.
Notify your supervisor. Discuss the report and any clinical implications for ongoing treatment.
1-800-894-5533 DCYF Central Intake (24/7) 911 (if child in immediate danger)

Elder Abuse Disclosure

HIGH — Mandatory Report Required
You are required to report. Under NH RSA 161-F:46, suspected abuse, neglect, self-neglect, or exploitation of an incapacitated adult (60+ or dependent adult) must be reported to the Bureau of Elderly and Adult Services (BEAS).
If the elder is in immediate danger, call 911 first.
Call BEAS at 1-800-949-0470. Available 24/7. Report the suspected abuse: physical abuse, emotional abuse, sexual abuse, neglect by a caregiver, self-neglect, or financial exploitation. Provide all known details about the elder and the situation.
Types to assess: Physical (unexplained injuries), emotional (intimidation, isolation), sexual, neglect (failure to provide care), self-neglect (unable to care for self), financial exploitation (misuse of funds, property, or assets).
Document your report in the clinical record with date, time, and details of the report.
Notify your supervisor and discuss safety planning and ongoing clinical care.
1-800-949-0470 BEAS (24/7) 911 (if immediate danger)

Domestic Violence Disclosure

MODERATE — Safety Planning + Resources
Ensure the conversation is safe. Confirm the abuser is not present or listening. If on the phone, ask: "Is it safe to talk right now?" If the client or children are in immediate danger, call 911.
Believe and validate. "I believe you. This is not your fault." Do not ask "Why don't you just leave?" Leaving is the most dangerous time.
Create a safety plan even if the client is not ready to leave. Include: safe place to go, packed essentials bag, code word with a trusted person, crisis numbers memorized, plan for children if applicable.
Provide the National DV Hotline: 1-800-799-7233 (24/7). Also provide the local Bridges crisis line: (603) 883-3044 (24/7). Bridges can provide: emergency shelter, safety planning, legal advocacy, court accompaniment, counseling, and housing assistance.
Remember mandated reporting obligations. If children are in the home and witnessing DV, this is reportable to DCYF at 1-800-894-5533. NH considers children's exposure to DV a form of abuse/neglect.
Do NOT contact the abuser. Do not attempt to mediate. Do not provide couples counseling (contraindicated when DV is present). Be careful with documentation the abuser might access.
Ongoing support. Respect the client's timeline. Continue to provide access to resources. Document appropriately.
1-800-799-7233 National DV Hotline (24/7) (603) 883-3044 Bridges Crisis Line (24/7) Text START to 88788 1-800-894-5533 DCYF (if children involved)

Acute Distress (Not Crisis)

STANDARD — Clinical Support
First, rule out crisis. Before proceeding, confirm: no suicidal/homicidal ideation, no psychosis, no immediate safety concern. If any are present, go back and select the appropriate scenario.
Ground the client. Use grounding techniques: 5-4-3-2-1 senses exercise (name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste), deep breathing (4 in, 4 hold, 4 out), or progressive muscle relaxation. Match the technique to what works for this client.
Validate and normalize. "It makes sense that you're feeling this way given what you're going through." Help the client label and articulate what they are experiencing.
Identify the trigger. What happened? What changed? Understanding the precipitant helps with coping planning and prevents future escalation.
Develop or review a coping plan. Identify what has worked in the past and what the client can do between now and the next session. Be specific: "Tonight, when you start feeling overwhelmed, you will [specific action]."
Schedule a follow-up within 24-72 hours. If the client does not have an upcoming appointment, create one. If appropriate, provide crisis numbers as a safety net: 988 or Text HOME to 741741.
Document the presenting distress, your assessment that the situation does not meet crisis criteria, interventions used, the coping plan, and the follow-up plan.

▼

When a client is in psychiatric crisis, knowing the right number to call can be the difference between a safe resolution and an escalation. Here is the complete decision tree.

Step-by-Step: Accessing Crisis Services

Assess the immediate situation. Is the client in immediate physical danger? Are there weapons present? Is there a medical emergency (overdose, self-harm requiring medical attention)? If YES to any of these, call 911 first, then crisis services.
Call NH Rapid Response at 1-833-710-6477. This is the front door to NH's behavioral health crisis system. A trained crisis counselor will assess the situation by phone and determine the appropriate response, which may include dispatching the local MCRT.
Have key information ready. Client name, age, location, brief description of the crisis, history of mental health treatment, current medications, any weapons or substance use, insurance information, and any safety concerns for the crisis team.
Stay with the client if safe to do so. Keep the client engaged and away from means. If you called Rapid Response, the MCRT typically arrives within 1-2 hours (varies by demand and location). MCRT can respond to homes, schools, community settings, and ERs.
If the client needs an emergency psychiatric evaluation: Direct or transport the client to Southern NH Medical Center ED (8 Prospect St, Nashua). Phone: (603) 577-2540 (ED direct). The ACCESS program provides crisis stabilization and psychiatric assessment. You can call ahead to notify the ED.
If hospitalization is needed: The ED team will initiate the IEA (Involuntary Emergency Admission) process if the client meets criteria and won't go voluntarily. Bed placement is managed through the statewide psychiatric bed board. SNHMC has an inpatient behavioral health unit at 29 Northwest Blvd (West Building).
Post-crisis follow-up. Within 24-48 hours, reconnect with the client. Ensure follow-up appointments are scheduled. If the client was seen by MCRT, they will provide recommendations. If the client was hospitalized, coordinate with the inpatient team for discharge planning. Ensure a safety plan is in place (see Safety Plan guide below).
Clinician Tip
GNMHC Crisis Stabilization Apartments offer an alternative to hospitalization for clients who need more than outpatient support but don't require inpatient care. These are short-term (3-7 days) supervised residential settings with structured programming. Access through GNMHC crisis services at (603) 889-6147.
About IEAs in New Hampshire
An Involuntary Emergency Admission (IEA) requires a physician or APRN to certify that the person is mentally ill and poses a danger to self or others, or is unable to provide for basic needs. A Probate Court hearing must occur within 3 business days. IEAs can only be initiated at a hospital ED. MCRT clinicians cannot initiate an IEA in the field — they can recommend the client be transported to the ED for evaluation.

2 How to Create a Safety Plan

▼

The Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention is an evidence-based, brief intervention for suicidal individuals. It is a prioritized list of coping strategies and resources that the client can use during a suicidal crisis. Complete this collaboratively with the client.

Step-by-Step: Building the Safety Plan

Identify Warning Signs. Ask: "What thoughts, feelings, behaviors, or situations signal that a crisis may be developing?" Help the client identify their personal warning signs. Examples: feeling hopeless, withdrawing from others, increased substance use, thinking "everyone would be better off without me," not sleeping, increased irritability. Write these down in the client's own words.
Internal Coping Strategies. Ask: "What are things you can do on your own, without contacting anyone, to take your mind off your problems?" These are things the client can do alone to distract or self-soothe. Examples: go for a walk, listen to music, take a shower, exercise, practice deep breathing, watch a favorite show, journal, play with a pet. Write at least 3-5 strategies.
Social Contacts Who Can Help Distract. List people and social settings that can provide distraction. These are people the client can reach out to for company or conversation, without necessarily telling them about the crisis. Include names and phone numbers. Examples: a friend to go to coffee with, a family member to call, a place to go (coffee shop, gym, library).
People to Contact During Crisis. List family members, friends, or others the client trusts enough to tell they are in crisis. Include names and phone numbers. Also list professionals and agencies:
988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline
1-833-710-6477 NH Rapid Response
(603) 889-6147 GNMHC Crisis
Text HOME to 741741
Your direct number / on-call number if applicable.
Professional and Agency Contacts. List the client's therapist, psychiatrist, case manager, PCP. Include:
- Clinician name and phone
- Agency crisis line (if applicable)
- Nearest emergency department: Southern NH Medical Center ED, 8 Prospect St, Nashua (603) 577-2540
Making the Environment Safe. Work with the client to reduce access to lethal means. Ask: "Are there things in your environment that could be dangerous during a crisis?" Firearms: remove from the home or lock with a trusted person holding the key. Medications: reduce supply, have someone else manage them. Other means: address as appropriate. This step is the single most evidence-based intervention for preventing suicide completion.
Identify One Reason for Living. Ask the client to identify at least one thing worth living for. This anchors the plan. It can be a person, a pet, a goal, a value, or anything meaningful to them. Write it at the top or bottom of the plan.
Implementation Tips
  • Complete the plan collaboratively with the client, not for them. Use their words.
  • Give the client a copy. Keep a copy in the chart. Photograph it for their phone.
  • Review and update the plan regularly, especially after any crisis or change in circumstances.
  • The plan is meant to be used sequentially: start with step 1, and if that doesn't work, move to step 2, and so on.
  • Means restriction (step 6) is the highest-impact component. Do not skip it.
  • Document the safety plan creation in your clinical note, including the date and that the client received a copy.

3 How to File a DCYF Report (Child Abuse/Neglect)

▼

Under NH RSA 169-C:29, healthcare providers, mental health professionals, teachers, law enforcement, and many others are mandated reporters. You must report any suspected child abuse or neglect immediately. You do not need proof — a reasonable suspicion is sufficient.

DCYF Central Intake Hotline
1-800-894-5533 Available 24/7/365
If a child is in immediate danger, call 911 first.

When to Report

  • Physical abuse: Unexplained injuries, injuries inconsistent with explanation, pattern injuries, excessive discipline
  • Sexual abuse: Any disclosure or indicators of sexual contact with a child
  • Neglect: Failure to provide food, shelter, clothing, medical care, supervision, or education appropriate for the child's age
  • Emotional abuse: Chronic pattern of behaviors that impair a child's emotional development (often reported alongside other types)
  • Exposure to domestic violence: A child witnessing domestic violence is considered a form of abuse/neglect in NH
Reporting Threshold
You are required to report when you have reason to suspect abuse or neglect. You do NOT need to investigate, confirm, or gather evidence. The investigation is DCYF's job. Failure to report can result in criminal penalties (misdemeanor in NH) and civil liability.

Step-by-Step: Filing the Report

Gather available information before calling. You don't need everything, but have what you can: child's name, age/DOB, address, parent/caregiver names, school, your observations or the disclosure, how you became aware, any immediate safety concerns, and whether the child has disclosed to anyone else.
Call DCYF Central Intake at 1-800-894-5533. A trained intake worker will take your report. Be prepared to provide all known information about the child, family, and your concerns. Reports can be made anonymously, but identified reporters receive follow-up about whether the report was accepted for investigation. As a mandated reporter, you should identify yourself.
Be specific in your report. Describe what you observed, what was disclosed to you, and when. Use direct quotes when possible. Describe the child's demeanor and any physical indicators. Avoid clinical jargon — describe behaviors and observations in plain language.
The intake worker will determine whether to accept the report. Reports are categorized as: accepted for assessment, accepted for investigation, or screened out. If screened out and you disagree, you can ask to speak with a supervisor. You can also call back if new information arises.
Document your report. In your clinical record, note: date and time of the report, the intake worker's name if provided, the report number (ask for one), what you reported, and any instructions the intake worker gave you. This protects you and creates a record.
Support the family through the process. Unless doing so would endanger the child, maintain your therapeutic relationship with the family. You can let the parent know that a report was made (unless DCYF instructs otherwise). Explain that reporting is required by law and that the goal is to help the family get support, not to punish. Connect the family to resources: family therapy, parenting classes, respite care, substance use treatment if applicable.
Clinician Tips
  • Do NOT interview the child in detail or conduct your own investigation. A forensic interview by the Child Advocacy Center is the gold standard.
  • Do NOT contact the alleged perpetrator to "verify" the report. This can compromise the investigation and endanger the child.
  • If you are unsure whether something rises to the level of reporting, call DCYF Central Intake and consult with them. They can help you decide.
  • Nashua DCYF Office is located at 137 Temple St (within DHHS building). If you need to coordinate with a DCYF worker on an open case, contact the Nashua office.
  • For adult protective services (elder/vulnerable adult abuse), call 1-800-949-0470 (BEAS).

4 How to Help a Client in a Domestic Violence Situation

▼

Domestic violence requires a specialized response. The primary resource in Greater Nashua is Bridges: Domestic & Sexual Violence Support. Their advocates are trained to help with every aspect of the situation. Your role is to connect the client to Bridges and support the process.

Key DV Numbers
(603) 883-3044 Bridges Crisis Line (24/7)
1-800-799-7233 National DV Hotline (24/7)
Text START to 88788 National DV Hotline text
1-866-644-3574 NH DV Hotline (24/7)

Step-by-Step: Responding to DV Disclosure

Ensure safety of the conversation. Before discussing DV, confirm the client is in a safe place to talk and that the abuser is not present or listening. If on the phone, ask: "Is it safe to talk right now?" If not, arrange a safer time/method.
Believe and validate. Say: "I believe you. This is not your fault. You don't deserve this." Avoid asking "Why don't you just leave?" — leaving is the most dangerous time for a DV survivor.
Assess immediate danger. Is the client safe right now? Are there children in the home? Has the violence escalated recently? Has the abuser threatened to kill? Has the abuser used weapons? Has the abuser threatened the children? If the client or children are in immediate danger, call 911.
Connect with Bridges. Call the Bridges crisis line at (603) 883-3044 (24/7). Bridges can provide: emergency shelter (confidential location), safety planning, legal advocacy for protective orders, court accompaniment, individual and group counseling, children's services, housing assistance, and community education. The client can call themselves, or with permission, you can call together or on their behalf.
Help create a safety plan. Even if the client is not ready to leave, a safety plan is critical. Include: a safe place to go in an emergency, a bag packed with essentials (ID, medications, important documents, children's items, some cash), a code word with a trusted friend/family member, the Bridges crisis number memorized, and a plan for the children.
Protective orders. If the client wants a protective order: go to Nashua District Court, 25 Walnut St, phone (603) 880-3333. Bridges advocates provide free assistance filing. A temporary order can be granted same-day by a judge. A full hearing is scheduled within 30 days. Types: Domestic Violence Protective Order (RSA 173-B) and Stalking Protective Order (RSA 633:3-a).
Address children's needs. If there are children involved, remember your mandated reporting obligations (children witnessing DV is reportable). Bridges has children's services. Consider referral to child therapy: GNMHC child services at (603) 889-6147, or Waypoint at (603) 518-4000.
Shelter placement. If the client needs shelter: Bridges operates a confidential emergency shelter in Nashua. The client will be met at a safe location and transported. If Bridges shelter is full, they can coordinate with other shelters in NH. For non-DV shelter: Nashua Soup Kitchen & Shelter at (603) 889-7770.
Ongoing support. DV situations rarely resolve quickly. Continue checking in with the client. Respect their timeline and decisions. Provide ongoing access to Bridges and legal resources. Document DV history in the clinical record (with client's awareness), as this can be important for legal proceedings and custody cases.
Safety Considerations for Clinicians
  • Never contact the abuser to "mediate" or "get the other side of the story."
  • Do not provide couples counseling when DV is present — this is contraindicated and can be dangerous.
  • Be careful with documentation that the abuser might access (patient portal, shared email).
  • If the abuser accompanies the client to sessions, find a safe way to screen for DV (e.g., separate check-in).

5 How to Refer a Client to Outpatient Mental Health Services

▼

When a client needs mental health treatment beyond what you can provide, knowing where to refer and how to make the referral smooth is essential. The pathway depends on the client's insurance, acuity, and treatment needs.

When to Refer

  • Client needs ongoing psychotherapy and you are not their therapist
  • Client needs psychiatric medication evaluation or management
  • Client needs a higher level of care (PHP, IOP) than you provide
  • Client has a serious mental illness (SMI) and needs comprehensive services (CSP, ACT)
  • Client needs specialized treatment you don't provide (EMDR, DBT, eating disorders, etc.)

Step-by-Step: Referring to GNMHC

Gather client information. Before calling, have ready: client's full name, DOB, address, phone number, insurance information (ID number, MCO name), brief clinical summary (presenting concerns, diagnoses, medications, risk factors), and any prior treatment history.
Call GNMHC at (603) 889-6147 and ask for Intake/Access. Provide the client information and a brief clinical summary. The intake coordinator will schedule an assessment, typically within 1-2 weeks. For urgent needs, request an expedited intake.
Prepare the client for what to expect. The intake assessment is comprehensive (1-2 hours). The client will be asked about their mental health history, substance use, medical history, social situation, trauma history, and current symptoms. Based on the assessment, GNMHC determines the appropriate level of care and assigns a treatment team.
Send records with a signed release. If you have clinical records that would help the intake team, send them with a signed ROI (release of information). This is especially important for medication history, prior assessments, and risk documentation.
Follow up. Check with the client after their intake appointment. Confirm they were connected to services and are scheduled for ongoing treatment. If there were barriers (transportation, no-show, waitlist), help problem-solve.
Key Facts About GNMHC
  • GNMHC is the designated Community Mental Health Center (CMHC) for Greater Nashua (Nashua, Hudson, Litchfield, Merrimack, Milford, Hollis, Brookline, and surrounding towns).
  • They are mandated by state contract to serve all individuals with serious mental illness (SMI) and cannot refuse service to this population.
  • They accept Medicaid (all NH MCOs), Medicare, most private insurance, and offer sliding-scale fees for uninsured. No one is turned away for inability to pay.
  • Services include: outpatient therapy, psychiatry, Community Support Program (CSP), ACT team, crisis services, supported employment, housing support, integrated dual-disorder treatment, and more.

Step-by-Step: The Application Process

Help the client apply. Contact Social Security Administration: 1-800-772-1213 or visit the Nashua SSA office at 70 Commercial St, Nashua, NH 03060. Appointments are recommended. The client can start the application online at ssa.gov. Applications can be filed for SSI, SSDI, or both simultaneously.
Gather medical documentation. The application requires a list of all medical providers, hospitals, clinics, and treatment dates. The more complete the medical record, the better. Include: diagnoses, treatment history, hospitalizations, medications (current and past), and any functional assessments.
Write a detailed functional assessment letter. This is where YOU, as the clinician, make the biggest impact. SSA cares about functional limitations, not just diagnoses. Focus on:
  • How the disability limits ability to work — not just diagnosis
  • Specific functional limitations: "Client cannot concentrate for more than 15 minutes," "Client misses 3+ days per month due to symptoms," "Client cannot stand for more than 10 minutes," "Client's paranoia prevents them from being around coworkers"
  • Impact on Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)
  • Number and duration of hospitalizations
  • Medication side effects that affect functioning
  • Persistence of symptoms despite treatment
SSA sends the case to Disability Determination Services (DDS). DDS reviews the medical evidence and makes a decision. This takes 3-6 months, often longer. The client may be sent for a consultative examination (CE) with an SSA-contracted examiner.
If denied (most initial claims are denied), appeal within 60 days. The appeal process has four levels: (1) Reconsideration, (2) Hearing before an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ), (3) Appeals Council review, (4) Federal Court. Most cases are won at the ALJ hearing stage.
Consider a disability attorney or advocate. Private disability attorneys work on contingency (25% of back benefits, max $7,200 fee). They are especially valuable at the ALJ hearing stage. Also:
(603) 228-0432 Disability Rights Center - NH (free advocacy)
1-800-639-5290 NH Legal Assistance (can help with denied claims)
Clinician Tips for Supporting the Claim
  • Use specific, measurable language in your letters. "Severely impaired" is vague. "Cannot maintain attention for more than 10 minutes, would be off-task 25%+ of the workday" is actionable.
  • Respond promptly to records requests from DDS. Delayed records = delayed decisions.
  • If the client has a CE scheduled, provide them with any records they should bring.
  • Continue treating and documenting. Gaps in treatment weaken claims. If the client misses appointments due to symptoms, document that too.
  • For clients under 50, the bar for disability is higher. SSA evaluates whether the client can do ANY work, not just their prior work.

17 How to Complete an ANSA Assessment

▼

The Adult Needs and Strengths Assessment (ANSA) is the standardized assessment tool used in the NH community mental health system. It drives treatment planning and helps determine level of care. Here is a practical guide to completing it well.

Purpose and Timing

  • Completed at intake, at every treatment plan review (typically every 6 months), and at discharge
  • Measures the client's needs and strengths across multiple domains
  • Scores directly inform treatment planning and level-of-care decisions
  • Used statewide to track outcomes and inform system planning

The Rating Scale (0-3)

RatingFor NEEDS ItemsFor STRENGTHS Items
0No evidence of need. No action needed.Well-developed strength. Can be used in treatment planning.
1History or mild need. Monitor/watchful waiting. Preventive action may be appropriate.Useful strength but needs development. Include in treatment plan to build.
2Moderate need. Action required. Should be addressed in the treatment plan.Identified but not useful yet. Strength-building efforts needed.
3Severe/acute need. Immediate or intensive action required. Crisis-level.No strength identified. Strength-building is a priority.
Critical Distinction
For NEEDS: higher = worse (more need). For STRENGTHS: higher = worse (less strength). This is counter-intuitive and a common error. A "3" on a needs item means CRISIS-LEVEL need. A "3" on a strengths item means NO strength in that area.

Domain-by-Domain Tips

Life Domain Functioning. Rate housing, daily living, employment/education, social functioning, legal involvement, and medical/physical health. Be specific: "client is couch-surfing and has no lease" = housing need of 2 or 3, not 1.
Behavioral/Emotional Needs. Rate psychiatric symptoms, substance use, trauma, impulse control, anger management, etc. Use the rating period (typically last 30 days). Don't rate a historical issue as current unless it's still active.
Risk Behaviors. Rate suicidality, self-harm, danger to others, legal involvement. Be honest — underrating risk items to avoid triggering additional requirements is dangerous and clinically irresponsible.
Strengths. Don't skip strengths. Rate family support, social connectedness, resilience, spiritual/cultural, vocational, educational, and other strengths. Strengths are critical for treatment planning. A client with strong family support (0) has a different treatment path than one with no supports (3).
Use all available information. Don't rate based only on the clinical interview. Use collateral sources, records, and observations. Talk to the treatment team. A client may deny substance use to you but have a positive drug screen in their medical record.

Common Scoring Errors

  • Central tendency bias: Rating everything as 1 or 2 to avoid extremes. Use the full scale.
  • Confusing history with current: Rate the current period, not lifetime. A past hospitalization doesn't mean current SI is a 3.
  • Underrating risk items: This is the most dangerous error. Rate honestly.
  • Ignoring strengths: Strengths are not optional. A strength profile of all 3s is a red flag for reviewers.
  • Not updating: The ANSA should change over time. If every review looks identical, you're not tracking progress.
Using ANSA Results for Treatment Planning
Items rated 2 or 3 should be addressed in the treatment plan. Items rated 0 or 1 on strengths should be leveraged as resources. The ANSA is a direct bridge to the ISP (Individual Service Plan). Map each 2/3 need to a treatment goal, and each 0/1 strength to a resource.

18 How to Complete a CANS Assessment

▼

The Child and Adolescent Needs and Strengths (CANS) is the companion to the ANSA, used for children and adolescents in the NH community mental health system. The same 0-3 rating system applies, but there are important age-specific considerations.

Same Rating System, Different Context

The 0-3 scale works the same as the ANSA (see guide above). For NEEDS: 0 = no need, 3 = severe/crisis. For STRENGTHS: 0 = well-developed, 3 = none identified.

Age-Specific Considerations

Young children (0-5): Focus on developmental milestones, attachment, caregiver capacity, and early childhood trauma. Behavioral concerns at this age often reflect the caregiving environment more than the child's internal state. Rate developmental domains carefully — use developmental screening tools (ASQ, MCHAT) to inform ratings.
School-age children (6-12): School functioning is a critical domain. Obtain information from teachers and school staff. Rate behavioral issues in context — a child acting out at school may be responding to instability at home. Include peer relationships, academic performance, and special education status.
Adolescents (13-17): Increased emphasis on risk behaviors (substance use, sexual behavior, legal involvement, self-harm). Adolescents may minimize or deny issues. Use collateral sources. Rate developmental tasks: identity, independence, peer relationships. Consider transition planning for youth approaching 18.

Caregiver Involvement

  • The CANS includes a Caregiver domain. Rate caregiver strengths and needs separately.
  • A child's functioning cannot be understood apart from the caregiving environment.
  • Caregiver needs (substance use, mental health, DV, financial stress) directly impact the child's treatment plan.
  • Involving caregivers in the assessment process (when safe and appropriate) improves accuracy and buy-in.

School Coordination

  • With a signed release, obtain information from school counselors, teachers, and special education staff.
  • The CANS school domain should reflect actual school performance and behavior, not just the parent's or child's report.
  • For children with IEPs, coordinate CANS findings with IEP goals when possible.
  • If a child needs school-based MH services, GNMHC has embedded school-based clinicians in some Nashua schools. Call (603) 889-6147 to inquire.
Transition Planning for Youth Approaching 18
Start transition planning well before the 18th birthday. Key issues: will the youth qualify for adult MH services (GNMHC adult system)? SSI application if applicable (can apply at age 18 based on their own income, not parent's). Health insurance transition (can stay on parent's plan to 26 if applicable, or Medicaid). Housing if leaving the family home. Education and employment planning.

19 How to Write an Individual Service Plan (ISP)

▼

The Individual Service Plan (ISP) is the central document that guides treatment. In NH's CMHC system, the ISP must be person-centered, based on the ANSA/CANS assessment, and reviewed at least every six months. Here is how to write one that is both clinically useful and compliant.

Person-Centered Planning Principles

  • The client is the primary author of their plan. Their goals, in their words.
  • Ask: "What do you want your life to look like?" not "What do you think you need to work on?"
  • The plan addresses what matters TO the client, not just what matters ABOUT the client.
  • Include the client's strengths, preferences, and cultural considerations.
  • The client signs the plan, indicating they participated in its development and agree with its contents.

Step-by-Step: Building the ISP

Start with the ANSA/CANS results. Every item rated 2 or 3 on needs should potentially be addressed in the plan. Prioritize with the client — you don't have to address everything at once. Items rated 0 or 1 on strengths should be leveraged as resources in the plan.
Write SMART goals. Each goal should be:
Specific — clearly defined
Measurable — you can tell when it's achieved
Achievable — realistic given the client's situation
Relevant — meaningful to the client
Time-bound — has a target date

Bad example: "Client will improve mental health."
Good example: "Within 6 months, Chris will report a reduction in panic attacks from 4x/week to 1x/week or less, as evidenced by self-report and clinician observation."
Link needs to goals to interventions. Each goal should have: (1) the identified need from the ANSA/CANS, (2) the client's goal in their own words, (3) specific measurable objectives, and (4) the interventions that will be used to achieve it (therapy modality, case management activities, medication, referrals, etc.).
Select appropriate interventions. Be specific: not just "therapy" but "weekly individual CBT focused on cognitive restructuring of anxious thoughts." Include who will provide the intervention, frequency, and modality. If referring to outside services, name them. If the client needs a resource (housing application, SNAP, legal aid), include it as a case management intervention.
Set review timelines. NH requires ISP review at least every 6 months. Set the review date. At review: update the ANSA/CANS, assess progress on each goal, modify goals as needed, discharge goals that are met, add new goals as appropriate.
Get client signature and provide a copy. The client must sign the plan indicating participation and agreement. Provide a copy to the client. If the client declines to sign, document why and continue working together on revisions.
NH-Specific Requirements
  • CMHCs have specific ISP templates and requirements. Use the one your agency provides.
  • The ISP must include: identified needs (from ANSA/CANS), client strengths, goals, objectives, interventions, responsible providers, frequency of services, and review date.
  • For clients receiving CSP or ACT services, the ISP is typically more detailed and includes rehabilitation goals.
  • If the client has co-occurring MH/SUD, the ISP should address both in an integrated fashion, not in separate plans.

20 How to Document Clinical Sessions

▼

Good clinical documentation protects the client, protects you, supports treatment, and meets regulatory requirements. Here is a practical guide to writing notes that are both clinically useful and defensible.

SOAP Format

SectionWhat to IncludeExamples
S (Subjective) Client's self-report. What they tell you in their own words. Their perception of symptoms, mood, events, progress. "Client reported feeling 'much less anxious this week' and stated she practiced deep breathing daily. Denied SI/HI."
O (Objective) Your clinical observations. Mental Status Exam (MSE) findings. Behavioral observations. Test results. Collateral information. "Client was alert, oriented x4, with euthymic affect and normal speech. Appeared well-groomed. Made appropriate eye contact. No psychomotor agitation noted."
A (Assessment) Your clinical formulation. Current diagnostic impressions. Progress toward treatment goals. Risk assessment. New developments. "Client demonstrates improvement in managing anxiety (Goal 1). Panic attacks reduced from 4x/week to 2x/week since implementing CBT breathing techniques. Low risk at this time."
P (Plan) Next steps. Continued interventions. Referrals. Homework/between-session tasks. Next appointment. Any changes to treatment plan. "Continue weekly CBT. Introduce cognitive restructuring next session. Client will practice thought records daily. Next appointment: 7/1/26. Referral to psychiatry for medication evaluation initiated."

Mental Status Exam (MSE) Documentation

  • Appearance: grooming, hygiene, dress, age appearance, notable features
  • Behavior: psychomotor activity, eye contact, cooperation, agitation, restlessness
  • Speech: rate, volume, tone, spontaneity, coherence
  • Mood: client's stated mood (subjective, use their words in quotes)
  • Affect: observed emotional expression (flat, blunted, constricted, full, labile). Congruence with mood.
  • Thought process: logical, linear, tangential, circumstantial, loosened, flight of ideas
  • Thought content: SI/HI (always document), delusions, obsessions, phobias, paranoia
  • Perception: hallucinations (auditory, visual), illusions
  • Cognition: orientation (person, place, time, situation), attention, memory, concentration
  • Insight: awareness of illness and need for treatment
  • Judgment: ability to make appropriate decisions

What to Include and What NOT to Include

DO Include
  • Specific interventions used (name the technique: "cognitive restructuring," "motivational interviewing," "skills coaching")
  • Client's response to interventions
  • Progress toward ISP goals (reference the specific goal)
  • Risk assessment at every session (even if brief: "Denied SI/HI. Low risk at this time.")
  • Any changes to medication, referrals made, or coordination of care
  • Client's own words (in quotes) when clinically significant
  • Any safety concerns or plans
Do NOT Include
  • Your personal opinions or judgments about the client's character
  • Vague statements without clinical basis ("client seems manipulative")
  • Information about third parties not relevant to treatment
  • Detailed content of therapy that could harm the client if the record is subpoenaed (process notes can be kept separately)
  • Countertransference reactions (process these in supervision, not in the chart)
  • Speculative diagnoses without clinical evidence

Risk Documentation

Document risk assessment at every clinical contact, even if brief. At minimum:

  • Suicidal ideation: present or denied. If present: passive vs. active, plan, intent, means, timeline.
  • Homicidal ideation: present or denied. If present: target, plan, intent, means.
  • Self-harm: recent or denied.
  • Risk level: low, moderate, or high — with rationale.
  • Protective factors: reasons for living, social support, engagement in treatment.
  • Action taken: safety plan reviewed, crisis numbers provided, hospitalization considered, etc.
Golden Rule of Documentation
Write every note as if it will be read by: (1) a new clinician taking over the case tomorrow, (2) a judge in a malpractice case, (3) the client themselves (they have a right to their records). If the note makes sense to all three audiences, it's good documentation.

21 Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

▼

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a structured, time-limited psychotherapy based on the cognitive model: our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected, and changing maladaptive thinking patterns leads to changes in emotions and behavior.

Core Model: The Cognitive Triangle

The foundation of CBT is the relationship between three elements:

  • Thoughts (cognitions) — How we interpret events. Automatic thoughts, core beliefs, cognitive distortions.
  • Feelings (emotions/physiology) — Emotional and physical responses triggered by our interpretations.
  • Behaviors (actions) — What we do (or avoid) in response to our thoughts and feelings.

Each element influences the others. CBT intervenes at the thought and behavior levels to produce change in all three.

Key Techniques

  • Cognitive Restructuring: Identifying automatic negative thoughts, evaluating the evidence for and against them, and developing more balanced alternative thoughts. Uses Socratic questioning.
  • Behavioral Activation: Scheduling and engaging in pleasurable and mastery activities to combat withdrawal and low mood. Especially effective for depression.
  • Exposure: Gradual, systematic confrontation with feared stimuli (in vivo, imaginal, or interoceptive). Core technique for anxiety disorders, phobias, OCD, and PTSD.
  • Thought Records: Structured worksheets where clients record triggering situations, automatic thoughts, emotions, evidence for/against, and balanced thoughts. The primary homework tool in CBT.
  • Behavioral Experiments: Testing predictions derived from negative beliefs through real-world experiments.
  • Activity Monitoring/Scheduling: Tracking daily activities and mood to identify patterns and plan changes.
  • Problem-Solving Training: Structured approach to identifying problems, generating solutions, evaluating options, and implementing plans.

Typical Session Structure

Check-in and mood rating (5 min) — Brief mood check, review of the week, set agenda collaboratively.
Homework review (10 min) — Review thought records, behavioral experiments, or other between-session assignments. Problem-solve barriers.
Session topic / skills work (25-30 min) — Teach new skill, apply cognitive restructuring to current issues, conduct exposure, or practice behavioral activation planning.
Summary and homework assignment (5-10 min) — Summarize key takeaways, assign specific between-session practice, get client feedback on the session.

Evidence Base

Strong Evidence For
Depression (MDD), generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), social anxiety disorder, panic disorder, specific phobias, OCD, PTSD, insomnia, eating disorders (bulimia), chronic pain, and substance use disorders. CBT has the largest evidence base of any psychotherapy. Effective across ages and cultures.

Best For

  • Populations: Adults, adolescents, children (adapted). Effective across cultural groups.
  • Diagnoses: Depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, OCD, insomnia, eating disorders, chronic pain, substance use disorders.
  • When to use: Client can engage in structured homework, has capacity for self-reflection, presents with identifiable cognitive distortions or avoidance patterns.

When NOT to Use (or Use with Caution)

Considerations
  • Active psychosis or severe cognitive impairment (inability to engage in cognitive work)
  • Severe intellectual disability without adaptation
  • Client in acute crisis requiring stabilization before structured therapy
  • Pervasive emotional dysregulation (consider DBT first to build distress tolerance skills)
  • Some clients experience the structured format as invalidating — build rapport first

Training Requirements

No formal certification required to practice CBT, but competency training is strongly recommended. The Academy of Cognitive and Behavioral Therapies (A-CBT) offers certification. Beck Institute provides foundational and advanced training. Many graduate programs include CBT coursework, but supervised clinical practice is essential for competence.

Recommended Reading

  • Beck, J.S. (2020). Cognitive Behavior Therapy: Basics and Beyond (3rd ed.). Guilford Press. — The definitive clinical guide.
  • Greenberger, D. & Padesky, C.A. (2015). Mind Over Mood (2nd ed.). Guilford Press. — Excellent client workbook.

22 Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

▼

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) was developed by Marsha Linehan for chronically suicidal individuals with borderline personality disorder. It balances acceptance and change through four core skill modules, delivered in a comprehensive treatment structure.

The 4 Skill Modules

1. Mindfulness (Core Module)

  • What Skills: Observe, Describe, Participate
  • How Skills: Non-judgmentally, One-mindfully, Effectively
  • Wise Mind: Integration of reasonable mind (logic) and emotion mind (feelings)
  • Foundation for all other modules; taught first and revisited throughout treatment

2. Distress Tolerance

  • Crisis Survival Skills: TIPP (Temperature, Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation), STOP skill, Pros/cons, Distraction (ACCEPTS), Self-soothing with five senses
  • Reality Acceptance Skills: Radical acceptance, Turning the mind, Willingness vs. willfulness, Half-smiling
  • Goal: survive the crisis WITHOUT making it worse

3. Emotion Regulation

  • Understanding emotions: Function of emotions, model for describing emotions
  • Reducing vulnerability: ABC PLEASE (Accumulate positives, Build mastery, Cope ahead, treat PhysicaL illness, balanced Eating, avoid mood-Altering substances, balanced Sleep, Exercise)
  • Changing emotions: Check the facts, Opposite action, Problem solving
  • Goal: understand, reduce vulnerability to, and change unwanted emotions

4. Interpersonal Effectiveness

  • DEAR MAN: Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce, Mindful, Appear confident, Negotiate — for getting what you need
  • GIVE: Gentle, Interested, Validate, Easy manner — for maintaining relationships
  • FAST: Fair, no Apologies (unnecessary), Stick to values, Truthful — for maintaining self-respect
  • Goal: get what you need while maintaining relationships and self-respect

Treatment Structure

Individual Therapy1:1 with DBT therapistWeekly, 50-60 min
Skills GroupGroup format, 6-10 clientsWeekly, 2-2.5 hours
Phone CoachingBrief between-session contactAs needed (before maladaptive behavior)

Comprehensive DBT includes all four components. A full skills cycle takes approximately 24 weeks and is typically completed twice (approximately one year of treatment).

Evidence Base

Strong Evidence For
Borderline personality disorder, chronic suicidality, self-harm, substance use disorders (co-occurring with BPD), binge eating disorder. Growing evidence for treatment-resistant depression, PTSD (DBT-PE), and eating disorders.

Best For

  • Populations: Adults and adolescents with pervasive emotion dysregulation. Adapted versions exist for adolescents (DBT-A) and pre-adolescents.
  • Diagnoses: BPD, chronic suicidality, self-harm, emotion dysregulation, co-occurring SUD/BPD.
  • When to use: Client has pervasive difficulty regulating emotions, pattern of impulsive/self-destructive behavior, interpersonal chaos, chronic suicidality.

Training Requirements

Comprehensive DBT requires intensive training. Behavioral Tech (Linehan's organization) offers the gold-standard DBT Intensive Training (10 days over 2 parts plus consultation year). DBT-Linehan Board of Certification offers clinician certification. At minimum, clinicians should have formal DBT training before running groups or providing individual DBT.

  • Linehan, M.M. (2014). DBT Skills Training Handouts and Worksheets (2nd ed.). Guilford Press. — Client materials.

23 EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)

▼

EMDR is an integrative psychotherapy approach that uses bilateral stimulation (eye movements, taps, or tones) to help the brain process and integrate traumatic memories. Developed by Francine Shapiro, EMDR is based on the Adaptive Information Processing (AIP) model.

The 8 Phases of EMDR

Phase 1: History and Treatment Planning. Comprehensive history, identify target memories, develop treatment plan. Assess client readiness and stability.
Phase 2: Preparation. Establish therapeutic alliance, explain EMDR process, teach self-regulation skills (safe place/calm place visualization, containment exercises, grounding). Ensure client has tools to manage distress between sessions.
Phase 3: Assessment. Identify the target memory, associated image, negative cognition (NC), desired positive cognition (PC), Validity of Cognition (VoC, 1-7 scale), emotions, Subjective Units of Disturbance (SUDs, 0-10), and body location of disturbance.
Phase 4: Desensitization. Process the target memory using bilateral stimulation (BLS). Client holds the target memory in mind while following the therapist's fingers, tapping, or auditory tones. Sets of BLS continue until SUDs reach 0 or an ecological valid level.
Phase 5: Installation. Strengthen the positive cognition (PC) and pair it with the original memory using BLS. Continue until VoC reaches 7 (fully true).
Phase 6: Body Scan. Client mentally scans their body while holding the target memory and positive cognition. Process any residual tension or disturbance with additional BLS.
Phase 7: Closure. Return client to equilibrium. Use self-regulation techniques if needed. Brief on what to expect between sessions (continued processing, dreams, new memories). Instruct client to keep a log of any disturbances.
Phase 8: Reevaluation. At the next session, assess the previously processed memory. Check SUDs and VoC. Determine if additional processing is needed or if it is time to move to the next target.

Bilateral Stimulation Methods

  • Eye movements: Client tracks therapist's fingers moving horizontally (most researched method)
  • Tactile tapping: Alternating taps on hands, knees, or shoulders (useful for telehealth via client self-tapping)
  • Auditory tones: Alternating tones through headphones
  • Butterfly hug: Client crosses arms and alternately taps shoulders (self-administered)

Evidence Base

Strong Evidence For
PTSD in adults (recognized by WHO, APA, VA/DoD, NICE as first-line treatment). Growing evidence for childhood trauma, complex PTSD, phobias, grief, anxiety disorders, and depression with traumatic origins. Comparable outcomes to prolonged exposure for PTSD, often with fewer sessions.

When to Refer for EMDR

  • Client has identifiable traumatic memories driving current symptoms
  • Client has PTSD or trauma-related symptoms not responding to talk therapy alone
  • Client is stable enough to tolerate trauma processing (adequate affect regulation, no active suicidality, no active substance dependence)
  • Client is motivated and willing to engage with the protocol
Use Caution / Do Not Refer When
  • Active psychosis or dissociative identity disorder (requires specialized EMDR training)
  • Active substance dependence (stabilize first)
  • Severe dissociation without adequate containment skills
  • Medical conditions where strong emotional arousal is contraindicated (certain cardiac/neurological conditions)
  • Ongoing traumatic situation (e.g., living with abuser) — stabilize environment first

Training Requirements

EMDR requires specialized training beyond a graduate degree. EMDRIA (EMDR International Association) approves training programs. Basic EMDR training is typically 40+ hours (Parts 1 and 2) plus 10 hours of consultation. EMDRIA certification requires additional supervised hours and consultation. Only clinicians who have completed at minimum the basic EMDR training should conduct EMDR therapy.

Recommended Reading

  • Shapiro, F. (2018). Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) Therapy: Basic Principles, Protocols, and Procedures (3rd ed.). Guilford Press.
  • Shapiro, F. (2012). Getting Past Your Past. Rodale. — Accessible client-oriented book.

24 Motivational Interviewing (MI)

▼

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a collaborative, person-centered counseling approach designed to strengthen an individual's motivation for and commitment to change. Developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick, MI is particularly effective when clients are ambivalent about behavior change.

The Spirit of MI

MI is defined more by its spirit than its techniques. The four elements of the MI spirit are:

  • Partnership: MI is done "with" and "for" the client, not "to" or "on" them. The clinician is not the expert on the client's life.
  • Acceptance: Absolute worth, accurate empathy, autonomy support, and affirmation. You do not have to approve of the behavior to accept the person.
  • Compassion: Actively promoting the client's welfare and prioritizing their needs.
  • Evocation: Drawing out the client's own motivations and resources rather than imposing external ones. The client has what they need; the clinician helps them find it.

OARS: Core MI Skills

SkillDescriptionExample
Open QuestionsQuestions that cannot be answered with yes/no. Invite elaboration and exploration."What concerns you most about your drinking?" (not "Are you concerned about your drinking?")
AffirmationsStatements that recognize client strengths, efforts, and values. Not praise; genuine recognition."You showed real courage coming in today. That tells me something about your commitment to your kids."
ReflectionsReflecting back what the client said, often going beyond the surface. Simple reflections repeat; complex reflections add meaning.Client: "I know I should quit but I just can't." Reflection: "Part of you really wants to make a change, and another part feels stuck."
SummariesCollecting reflections together, linking themes, transitioning. Demonstrates active listening and helps organize."So let me pull together what you've shared: you're worried about your health, your kids are noticing, and yet smoking has been your main coping tool for 20 years..."

Stages of Change Model (Prochaska & DiClemente)

  • Precontemplation: Not yet considering change. Focus: build rapport, plant seeds, raise awareness without pushing.
  • Contemplation: Ambivalent — aware of the problem but not committed. Focus: explore ambivalence, develop discrepancy, evoke change talk.
  • Preparation: Planning to change soon. Focus: strengthen commitment, develop a plan, explore options.
  • Action: Actively making changes. Focus: support, problem-solve barriers, reinforce successes.
  • Maintenance: Sustaining changes. Focus: prevent relapse, consolidate gains, build new identity.
  • Relapse: Returned to old behavior (not a stage but a common event). Focus: normalize, recommit, learn from the experience.

Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk

Key MI Concept
Change talk is any client speech that favors change (Desire, Ability, Reasons, Need, Commitment, Taking steps, Activation — DARN-CAT). Clinician goal: evoke, recognize, and reinforce change talk.

Sustain talk is client speech favoring the status quo. Do not argue against it. Reflect it, explore it, and gently tip the balance toward change. Arguing against sustain talk increases resistance.

Evidence Base

Strong Evidence For
Substance use disorders (alcohol, drugs, tobacco), medication adherence, diet and exercise, treatment engagement, dual diagnosis. MI is effective as a standalone brief intervention and as a prelude to other treatments. Effective across cultures, settings, and clinical populations.

Best For

  • Populations: Any client who is ambivalent about behavior change. All ages (adapted for adolescents).
  • Diagnoses/Situations: SUD, treatment non-adherence, health behavior change, co-occurring disorders, pre-treatment engagement.
  • When to use: Client is ambivalent or resistant to change. Any time you notice yourself arguing FOR change and the client arguing AGAINST it (the "righting reflex").

Training Requirements

MI is widely taught in graduate programs and continuing education. MINT (Motivational Interviewing Network of Trainers) provides trainer certification. Most effective training includes didactic instruction, practice with feedback, and coding of sessions (MITI coding). Competence requires ongoing practice and supervision; a 2-day workshop alone is insufficient.

Recommended Reading

  • Miller, W.R. & Rollnick, S. (2023). Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change and Grow (4th ed.). Guilford Press. — The definitive text.
  • Rosengren, D.B. (2017). Building Motivational Interviewing Skills: A Practitioner Workbook (2nd ed.). Guilford Press. — Practical exercises for building MI skills.

25 Prolonged Exposure (PE)

▼

Prolonged Exposure (PE) is a specific, manualized cognitive-behavioral therapy for PTSD developed by Edna Foa. It is based on emotional processing theory: trauma memories are poorly processed and stored in a "fear structure" that is activated by reminders, driving avoidance. PE helps the brain process the trauma by confronting trauma-related memories and situations in a safe, therapeutic context.

Rationale

Avoidance maintains PTSD. By repeatedly approaching trauma memories (imaginal exposure) and real-world situations that are safe but avoided due to trauma associations (in vivo exposure), the fear response diminishes through habituation and the memory is processed and integrated. The client learns: the memory itself is not dangerous, anxiety decreases on its own, and they can cope.

Key Components

  • Psychoeducation: Explain the rationale for exposure, PTSD symptoms, and common trauma reactions. Normalize the client's experience.
  • Breathing Retraining: Teach diaphragmatic breathing as a general anxiety management tool (not to reduce distress during exposure).
  • In Vivo Exposure: Gradual confrontation with real-world situations, places, or activities the client avoids because of trauma associations. Create a hierarchy (0-100 SUDs) and work from moderate to high.
  • Imaginal Exposure: Client recounts the traumatic memory aloud in the present tense, with eyes closed, in vivid sensory detail. Typically 30-45 minutes per session. Recorded for between-session listening.

SUDs Scale (Subjective Units of Disturbance)

RatingDescription
0No distress, completely relaxed
25Mild distress, noticeable but manageable
50Moderate distress, uncomfortable but can tolerate
75High distress, very uncomfortable, difficult to tolerate
100Maximum distress, worst anxiety imaginable

Typical Treatment Course

8-15 sessions, each 90 minutes. Sessions 1-2: psychoeducation, breathing, in vivo hierarchy development. Sessions 3+: imaginal exposure (30-45 min) with processing discussion, plus ongoing in vivo assignments. Client listens to imaginal recording daily between sessions.

Evidence Base

Strong Evidence For
PTSD (VA/DoD, APA, and NICE guidelines designate PE as first-line treatment). Effective for all trauma types: combat, sexual assault, childhood abuse, motor vehicle accidents, natural disasters. One of the two most-researched treatments for PTSD (alongside CPT).

Best For

  • Populations: Adults with PTSD from any trauma type. Adapted for adolescents (PE-A).
  • When to use: Client has PTSD with significant avoidance, client is willing and able to engage with trauma memory, client is stable enough for trauma processing.
Contraindications / Cautions
Active suicidality (stabilize first). Active substance dependence (some protocols allow concurrent treatment). Severe dissociation during exposure (may require grounding modifications). Ongoing threat/unsafe living situation. Not a contraindication: comorbid depression, anger, guilt, shame, or complex trauma (PE is effective for these).

Training Requirements

PE requires specific training in the manualized protocol. The Center for the Treatment and Study of Anxiety (Foa's center at UPenn) and the National Center for PTSD provide training. The VA/DoD PE training includes a workshop plus consultation calls with case review. PE certification is available through the Center for the Treatment and Study of Anxiety.

Recommended Reading

  • Foa, E.B., Hembree, E.A., & Rothbaum, B.O. (2019). Prolonged Exposure Therapy for PTSD: Therapist Guide (2nd ed.). Oxford University Press.
  • Foa, E.B. & Rothbaum, B.O. (1998). Treating the Trauma of Rape. Guilford Press.

26 Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)

▼

Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT) is a manualized cognitive therapy for PTSD developed by Patricia Resick. CPT focuses on how the client has made meaning of the traumatic event and helps them identify and challenge maladaptive beliefs ("stuck points") that maintain PTSD symptoms.

Core Concepts

  • Stuck Points: Maladaptive beliefs about the trauma, self, others, or the world that keep the client "stuck" in PTSD. Examples: "It was my fault," "I can never trust anyone again," "The world is completely dangerous," "I should have been able to prevent it."
  • Assimilation vs. Accommodation: Assimilation = distorting the trauma to fit prior beliefs ("It must have been my fault because bad things don't happen to good people"). Accommodation = changing beliefs to account for the trauma in a balanced way ("Bad things can happen to anyone; it wasn't my fault"). Over-accommodation = changing beliefs too far ("No one can ever be trusted").
  • Five Themes: Safety, Trust, Power/Control, Esteem, Intimacy — disruptions in these areas are common after trauma.

Key Tools

  • ABC Worksheet: Activating event, Belief/stuck point, Consequence (emotion). Helps clients connect thoughts to feelings. Used early in treatment to build cognitive awareness.
  • Challenging Questions Worksheet: Systematic questions to evaluate stuck points: What is the evidence? Are you confusing a thought with a fact? Is the thought based on habit or fact? What would you tell a friend?
  • Patterns of Problematic Thinking Worksheet: Identifies cognitive distortions in stuck points (jumping to conclusions, exaggerating, oversimplifying, mind reading, emotional reasoning).
  • Challenging Beliefs Worksheet: Comprehensive worksheet integrating all prior tools. The primary homework tool in the second half of CPT.
  • Impact Statement: Written at the start (why the trauma happened and its impact) and end (how beliefs have changed) of treatment. The comparison demonstrates progress.

Treatment Course

12 sessions, each 50 minutes (can also be delivered in 60-minute sessions). Highly structured and manualized. Two formats: CPT (with written trauma account) and CPT-C (cognitive-only, no written account — the more commonly used version, with comparable outcomes).

Evidence Base

Strong Evidence For
PTSD (VA/DoD, APA, NICE guidelines designate CPT as first-line treatment alongside PE). Effective for all trauma types. Strong evidence from military/veteran populations, sexual assault survivors, and diverse civilian populations. One of the two most-researched PTSD treatments.

Best For

  • Populations: Adults with PTSD. Adapted versions for adolescents and group delivery.
  • When to use: Client has identifiable stuck points related to trauma, client can engage in written homework, client may prefer a cognitive approach over exposure-based approach.
  • CPT vs. PE: CPT may be preferred when the client has prominent guilt/shame cognitions, prefers cognitive work over reliving the trauma, or when the trauma involves multiple events (CPT can address stuck points across events without processing each one individually).

Training Requirements

CPT requires training in the specific protocol. CPT training is available through the CPT for PTSD website (cpt.musc.edu), the VA/DoD (for VA clinicians), and MUSC's National Crime Victims Center. Training typically includes a 2-day workshop plus consultation calls with case review and certification process.

Recommended Reading

  • Resick, P.A., Monson, C.M., & Chard, K.M. (2017). Cognitive Processing Therapy for PTSD: A Comprehensive Manual. Guilford Press.
  • Resick, P.A. et al. (2016). Cognitive Processing Therapy: Veteran/Military Version: Therapist and Patient Materials Manual. VA/DoD.

27 Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

▼

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) (pronounced "act," not the individual letters) is a third-wave behavioral therapy developed by Steven Hayes. ACT aims to increase psychological flexibility — the ability to be present, open up to difficult experiences, and take action guided by values, even in the presence of pain.

The Hexaflex: 6 Core Processes

The ACT model is organized around six interrelated processes that produce psychological flexibility:

  • Acceptance: Willingness to experience difficult thoughts, feelings, and sensations without trying to eliminate or avoid them. The opposite of experiential avoidance. "Make room for the feeling rather than fighting it."
  • Cognitive Defusion: Changing the relationship with thoughts rather than changing their content. Seeing thoughts as thoughts, not as literal truths. Techniques: "I'm having the thought that...," word repetition, thought labeling, metaphors (passengers on the bus).
  • Present Moment Awareness: Flexible, non-judgmental attention to the here and now. Mindfulness practices, grounding. Making contact with the present rather than living in the past or future.
  • Self-as-Context (The Observing Self): Connecting with a transcendent sense of self that is the context for experience, not the content. "You are the sky; thoughts and feelings are the weather." Provides perspective and reduces fusion with any single experience.
  • Values: Clarifying what truly matters to the client — their chosen life directions, not goals. Values are ongoing directions (being a caring parent) not achievable endpoints (getting a promotion). Values provide motivation and meaning.
  • Committed Action: Taking concrete, values-driven action, even in the presence of difficult thoughts and feelings. Setting goals linked to values and following through. "What would you do if the anxiety was not in charge?"

Experiential Avoidance

The Core Problem in ACT
Experiential avoidance is the attempt to avoid, suppress, or escape unwanted private experiences (thoughts, feelings, memories, sensations). ACT posits that experiential avoidance is a primary driver of psychopathology. When clients organize their lives around NOT feeling certain things, they narrow their lives and often increase their suffering. ACT offers an alternative: willingness and valued living alongside pain.

Common ACT Metaphors and Exercises

  • Passengers on the Bus: You are the bus driver. Scary/negative thoughts are passengers. You can drive toward your values even with noisy passengers.
  • Tug of War with a Monster: Drop the rope. Stop fighting the thought/feeling and redirect energy toward values.
  • Quicksand: The more you struggle, the deeper you sink. Accepting and spreading out is the way forward.
  • Values Card Sort: Client sorts value cards to clarify personal values hierarchy.
  • The Choice Point: Visual tool showing the moment of choice: move toward values or move away (avoidance).

Evidence Base

Growing Evidence For
Depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, chronic pain, substance use disorders, psychosis (adjunctive), workplace stress, diabetes management, and general psychological well-being. ACT's evidence base is growing rapidly. Listed as evidence-based by SAMHSA's NREPP. Particularly strong for chronic pain and conditions involving experiential avoidance.

Best For

  • Populations: Adults and adolescents. Adapted for children, older adults, and various cultural groups.
  • Diagnoses/Situations: Chronic pain, treatment-resistant depression/anxiety, clients who have "tried everything," existential distress, values confusion, persistent avoidance patterns.
  • When to use: Client's attempts to control or eliminate symptoms are backfiring, client is disconnected from values, client is caught in experiential avoidance cycles, or when traditional CBT cognitive restructuring feels too "heady."

Training Requirements

No formal certification required. The Association for Contextual Behavioral Science (ACBS) offers peer-reviewed ACT trainer status. Training opportunities range from introductory workshops to intensive immersion training. Experiential learning is emphasized — ACT therapists benefit from practicing ACT processes themselves.

Recommended Reading

  • Hayes, S.C. & Strosahl, K.D. (2010). A Practical Guide to Acceptance and Commitment Therapy. Springer.
  • Harris, R. (2019). ACT Made Simple (2nd ed.). New Harbinger. — Outstanding clinical primer, highly readable.

28 Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT)

▼

Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) was developed by Steve de Shazer and Insoo Kim Berg. Rather than analyzing problems and their causes, SFBT focuses on solutions — what is already working and how to do more of it. It assumes clients have the resources they need and the therapist's job is to help them access those resources.

Core Principles

  • If it isn't broken, don't fix it
  • If something is working, do more of it
  • If something isn't working, do something different
  • Small steps can lead to large changes
  • The solution is not necessarily related to the problem
  • Clients are the experts on their own lives

Key Techniques

The Miracle Question

"Suppose tonight, while you are sleeping, a miracle happens and the problem that brought you here is solved. But because you were sleeping, you don't know the miracle happened. When you wake up tomorrow morning, what would be the first small sign that tells you something is different?" This orients the client toward a detailed, concrete vision of their desired future.

Scaling Questions

"On a scale of 1 to 10, where 10 is the miracle day and 1 is the worst things have ever been, where are you right now?" Follow up: "What would it take to move from a [current number] to a [current +1]?" Scaling questions create measurable progress, build hope, and break overwhelming problems into small steps.

Exception Finding

"When was the last time the problem was even a little bit better? What was different about that time? What were you doing differently?" Exceptions to the problem pattern reveal existing client strengths and solutions. The therapist helps the client do more of what is already working.

Compliments

Genuine, specific observations about client strengths, resources, and successes. Compliments are based on what the client has said, not therapist opinion. "Given how difficult this week was, the fact that you still got your kids to school every day shows remarkable dedication."

Coping Questions

"How have you managed to keep going with all of this?" Used when the client cannot identify exceptions. Highlights resilience and coping even in the absence of perceived progress.

Evidence Base

Moderate-to-Good Evidence For
Depression (mild-to-moderate), school-based behavioral problems, couple and family therapy, substance use, occupational rehabilitation, and child welfare. Particularly effective in brief treatment contexts (3-8 sessions). Listed on SAMHSA's NREPP.

Best For

  • Populations: Adults, adolescents, children, couples, and families. Culturally adaptable.
  • Diagnoses/Situations: Mild-to-moderate depression, behavioral issues, couple/family conflict, school problems, work stress, mandated clients, clients who don't want to "dig into the past."
  • When to use: Client is motivated but stuck, client is solution-oriented, time-limited treatment context, mandated clients (reframes the conversation away from the problem/pathology), client has clear goals.
Limitations
Not well-suited as standalone treatment for severe mental illness, active psychosis, trauma requiring processing, or conditions requiring medication management. Can be frustrating for clients who feel their problem is being minimized. Best combined with other approaches for complex presentations.

Training Requirements

No formal certification required. The Solution Focused Brief Therapy Association (SFBTA) provides training resources and hosts annual conferences. Many trainings are available as continuing education. Competence develops through practice and supervision from SFBT-trained supervisors.

Recommended Reading

  • de Shazer, S. et al. (2007). More Than Miracles: The State of the Art of Solution-Focused Brief Therapy. Routledge.
  • Ratner, H., George, E., & Iveson, C. (2012). Solution Focused Brief Therapy: 100 Key Points and Techniques. Routledge. — Concise, practical reference.

29 Trauma-Focused CBT (TF-CBT)

▼

Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) is an evidence-based treatment for children and adolescents (ages 3-18) who have experienced trauma. Developed by Judith Cohen, Anthony Mannarino, and Esther Deblinger, TF-CBT integrates trauma-sensitive interventions with cognitive-behavioral, family, and humanistic principles.

PRACTICE Components

TF-CBT is structured around the PRACTICE acronym, with components delivered in a phased approach:

P — Psychoeducation and Parenting Skills. Educate the child and caregiver about trauma, common trauma reactions, and the treatment process. Teach parenting skills (praise, active ignoring, behavior management) to the caregiver.
R — Relaxation Skills. Teach the child (and caregiver) relaxation techniques: focused breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery, mindfulness. These provide tools for managing trauma-related physiological arousal.
A — Affective Modulation. Help the child identify and express feelings, develop an expanded emotional vocabulary, and learn skills for managing intense emotions (feeling thermometer, thought interruption, positive self-talk).
C — Cognitive Coping (Cognitive Triangle). Teach the connection between thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. Help the child identify and challenge inaccurate or unhelpful thoughts. Age-appropriate cognitive restructuring.
T — Trauma Narrative and Processing. The child creates a detailed account of their trauma experience(s) through writing, drawing, or other media. The therapist helps the child process the narrative and challenge trauma-related cognitive distortions (e.g., self-blame, feeling permanently damaged).
I — In Vivo Mastery of Trauma Reminders. Gradual exposure to trauma reminders (situations, places) that are objectively safe but avoided due to trauma associations. Create a fear hierarchy and work up systematically.
C — Conjoint Child-Parent Sessions. The child shares their trauma narrative with the caregiver in a therapeutic setting. The caregiver is prepared to respond supportively. Strengthens the parent-child relationship and models healthy communication about trauma.
E — Enhancing Safety and Future Development. Safety planning, body safety skills, healthy relationship skills, and planning for the future. Prevent revictimization and build resilience.

Caregiver Involvement

Essential Component
Caregiver involvement is critical to TF-CBT's effectiveness. Caregivers participate in parallel sessions where they learn the same skills as the child, process their own reactions to the child's trauma, and prepare for conjoint sessions. A supportive, non-offending caregiver is the single strongest predictor of positive outcomes for traumatized children. If no caregiver is available, TF-CBT can be adapted, but outcomes are typically stronger with caregiver participation.

Evidence Base

Strong Evidence For
PTSD, depression, anxiety, and behavioral problems in children and adolescents related to trauma including sexual abuse, physical abuse, domestic violence, traumatic grief, community violence, disaster, and multiple traumas. TF-CBT has more randomized controlled trials supporting its effectiveness than any other child trauma treatment. Recognized by SAMHSA, NCTSN, and multiple international guidelines.

Best For

  • Populations: Children and adolescents ages 3-18 with trauma-related symptoms. Adapted for complex trauma, traumatic grief, and children in foster care.
  • When to use: Child/adolescent has experienced trauma and displays PTSD, anxiety, depression, or behavioral symptoms. A non-offending caregiver is available and willing to participate.
  • Treatment length: Typically 12-25 sessions (individual sessions for child and caregiver, plus conjoint sessions).

Training Requirements

TF-CBT requires specific training. The free web-based training is available at tfcbt.org (TF-CBTWeb2.0). After completing the online training, clinicians should participate in live consultation with a TF-CBT trainer. TF-CBT certification is available through the TF-CBT National Therapist Certification Program (requires training, consultation, and case completion).

Recommended Reading

  • Cohen, J.A., Mannarino, A.P., & Deblinger, E. (2017). Treating Trauma and Traumatic Grief in Children and Adolescents (2nd ed.). Guilford Press. — The definitive clinical manual.
  • tfcbt.org — Free web-based training course (TF-CBTWeb2.0). Excellent starting point.

30 Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (CAMS)

▼

CAMS is a suicide-specific, evidence-based therapeutic framework developed by David Jobes. Unlike most treatments where suicide is a secondary target, CAMS makes suicidality the primary focus of treatment. The clinician and client sit side-by-side and collaboratively assess and treat the client's suicidal drivers.

The Suicide Status Form (SSF)

The SSF is the core clinical tool of CAMS. It structures assessment, treatment planning, tracking, and outcome/disposition. The SSF is completed collaboratively with the client at every session.

SSF Assessment Components

  • Quantitative ratings (1-5 scales): Psychological pain, Stress, Agitation, Hopelessness, Self-hate — all rated by the client
  • Overall risk: Client and clinician each rate overall suicide risk (1-5)
  • Reasons for living vs. reasons for dying: Client lists both, providing insight into ambivalence and protective factors
  • One thing that would help: Client identifies the single most important thing that would reduce suicidality
  • Suicidal drivers: Collaboratively identified direct and indirect drivers of suicidal desire (e.g., "feeling like a burden," "relationship loss," "chronic pain," "job loss")

Treatment Framework

Initial session: Assessment and stabilization. Complete the SSF collaboratively (seated side-by-side). Develop the CAMS Stabilization Plan (not a safety plan but a treatment plan specifically targeting suicidal drivers). Identify suicidal drivers and collaboratively develop a treatment plan that directly targets those drivers.
Interim sessions: Track and treat. Re-administer the SSF quantitative ratings at the start of each session to track suicidal status. Use evidence-based interventions to treat each identified driver (e.g., CBT for hopelessness, behavioral activation for isolation, problem-solving for job loss). The treatment plan is updated as drivers change.
Outcome/disposition. When three consecutive sessions show all SSF ratings below threshold, the client and clinician collaborate on transitioning from CAMS to standard care. CAMS resolution criteria are clearly defined and data-driven, not based on clinician intuition alone.

Suicide-Specific Focus

What Makes CAMS Different
Most therapies treat the diagnosis (depression, PTSD, etc.) and assume suicidality will improve as the underlying condition improves. CAMS directly targets suicidality as its own problem. The collaborative, side-by-side approach is central: the clinician and client are co-authors of the assessment and treatment plan. This reduces the power differential and increases client engagement and honesty.

Evidence Base

Growing Strong Evidence
Multiple RCTs demonstrating CAMS reduces suicidal ideation faster than treatment-as-usual, reduces overall symptom distress, and is associated with high client satisfaction. Effective across settings (outpatient, inpatient, emergency) and populations (military, college students, community mental health). Designated as evidence-based by SAMHSA and recommended by the VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guidelines for suicide risk assessment.

Best For

  • Populations: Adults and adolescents presenting with suicidal ideation or behavior. Effective across diagnoses.
  • When to use: Any client presenting with suicidal ideation, regardless of underlying diagnosis. Particularly useful when suicidality is a primary concern rather than simply a symptom of another condition.
  • Setting: Outpatient (primary setting), inpatient, ED, college counseling centers, military settings.

Training Requirements

CAMS training is available through the CAMS-care organization (cams-care.com). Training includes online courses (CAMS-care Essentials), live workshops, and a role-play-based e-learning program. Formal training is strongly recommended before implementing CAMS, as the collaborative approach and SSF require specific clinical skills. CAMS-care certification is available for individuals and organizations.

Recommended Reading

  • Jobes, D.A. (2016). Managing Suicidal Risk: A Collaborative Approach (2nd ed.). Guilford Press. — The comprehensive clinical guide.
  • Jobes, D.A. & Joiner, T.E. (2019). "The CAMS Approach to Suicide Risk." Psychiatry, 82(2), 123-130.

31 The 6 Principles of Trauma-Informed Care

▼

Trauma-informed care is not a specific clinical intervention but a framework for how services are delivered. SAMHSA identifies six key principles that guide trauma-informed practice at every level — from individual clinical encounters to organizational culture.

The 6 Principles (SAMHSA, 2014)

1. Safety. Throughout the organization, staff and clients feel physically and psychologically safe. This includes the physical environment (well-lit, clean, welcoming, no barriers to exit) and interpersonal interactions (consistent, predictable, respectful). Ask: "Does this client feel safe in this space? What might threaten their sense of safety?"
2. Trustworthiness and Transparency. Organizational operations and decisions are conducted with transparency. Trust is built through clear expectations, consistent follow-through, and honest communication. Explain what you are doing and why. No surprises. Keep your word. When you cannot do something, say so directly.
3. Peer Support. Mutual self-help and peer support are integral to the organizational and service delivery approach. People with shared experiences of trauma build trust, establish safety, and offer hope. Peer specialists, support groups, and recovery communities embody this principle.
4. Collaboration and Mutuality. The organization recognizes that healing happens in relationships and in the meaningful sharing of power and decision-making. Leveling power differences between staff and clients. "What do YOU think would help?" rather than "Here's what you need to do." Treatment planning is collaborative, not prescriptive.
5. Empowerment, Voice, and Choice. The organization aims to strengthen the staff's, clients', and family members' experience of choice, recognizes that individuals are experts in their own lives, and fosters individuals' sense of control over their recovery. Offer choices whenever possible. "Would you prefer to meet in the morning or afternoon?" matters more than you think.
6. Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues. The organization moves past cultural stereotypes and biases, offers access to gender-responsive services, leverages the healing value of traditional cultural connections, and recognizes and addresses historical trauma. This includes understanding how systemic racism, generational poverty, and historical oppression function as traumatic experiences.
The Foundational Question
Trauma-informed care shifts the question from "What is wrong with you?" to "What happened to you?" This reframing is not just philosophical. It changes how we understand behavior, how we design services, how we interact with clients, and how we structure organizations. Every "difficult" behavior has a function and often originated as a survival strategy.

Putting Principles into Practice

  • Before every session: Check the environment. Is it private? Comfortable? Free of triggers? Can the client see the exit?
  • At every intake: Explain confidentiality, limits, and what to expect. Give the client control over the pace. "We can stop at any time."
  • In every interaction: Be consistent, follow through on commitments, explain changes, offer choices, share power.
  • In documentation: Use strengths-based, person-first language. Describe behaviors, not character.
  • In the organization: Advocate for policies that reflect TIC principles. Trauma-informed care is everyone's responsibility.

32 ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences)

▼

The ACE Study (Felitti et al., 1998; CDC-Kaiser Permanente) demonstrated a powerful relationship between childhood adversity and adult health outcomes. Understanding ACEs is foundational to trauma-informed practice.

The 10 ACE Categories

The original ACE Study assessed 10 types of childhood adversity in three domains:

Abuse (3 categories)

  • Physical abuse: Pushed, grabbed, slapped, hit, or had something thrown at you by a parent/household adult
  • Emotional abuse: Parent/household adult swore at, insulted, humiliated you, or acted in a way that made you afraid of being physically hurt
  • Sexual abuse: An adult or person at least 5 years older touched you sexually or had you touch them, or attempted or completed sexual intercourse with you

Neglect (2 categories)

  • Physical neglect: Not enough food, dirty clothing, no one to protect you; parents too drunk/high to care for you
  • Emotional neglect: No one in the family made you feel important, special, or loved; family members didn't look out for each other or feel close

Household Dysfunction (5 categories)

  • Mother treated violently: Mother or stepmother pushed, hit, slapped, kicked, threatened with weapon
  • Household substance abuse: Lived with someone who was a problem drinker/alcoholic or who used drugs
  • Household mental illness: Lived with someone who was depressed, mentally ill, or who attempted suicide
  • Parental separation or divorce
  • Incarcerated household member: A household member went to prison

The Dose-Response Relationship

ACE Score and Health Impact
The ACE score (0-10) has a graded, dose-response relationship to health outcomes. As the ACE score increases, the risk of the following increases dramatically:
  • ACE score 4+: 4-12x increased risk for alcoholism, drug abuse, depression, and suicide attempt
  • ACE score 4+: 2-4x increased risk for smoking, poor self-rated health, 50+ sexual partners, STIs
  • ACE score 4+: 1.4-1.6x increased risk for physical inactivity and severe obesity
  • ACE score 6+: Life expectancy shortened by nearly 20 years
  • Leading causes of death in adults (heart disease, cancer, COPD, liver disease) are all correlated with ACE scores

The Mechanism: Toxic Stress

ACEs produce their long-term effects through toxic stress — prolonged activation of the body's stress response system in the absence of adequate adult support. This affects:

  • Brain development: Altered development of the prefrontal cortex, amygdala, and hippocampus. Impaired executive function, emotional regulation, and memory.
  • Stress response system: Chronically elevated cortisol, altered HPA axis functioning. The body stays in "survival mode."
  • Immune system: Chronic inflammation, impaired immune function, increased autoimmune risk.
  • Gene expression: Epigenetic changes that can be transmitted across generations.

Protective Factors and Resilience

ACEs Are Not Destiny
The most powerful protective factor is the presence of at least one stable, caring adult in the child's life. Other protective factors include: strong social connections, sense of mastery and control, faith/cultural traditions, access to concrete support in times of need, and social-emotional competence. Resilience is not a trait people either have or don't — it can be built at any age.

Clinical Application

  • ACE screening can be integrated into clinical assessments, but should be done with care and follow-up resources. Never screen without the capacity to respond.
  • ACE scores are population-level indicators, not diagnostic tools for individuals. A person with an ACE score of 8 may be thriving; a person with a score of 2 may be struggling.
  • ACEs help explain, not excuse, behavior. They provide context for clinical formulation.
  • The expanded ACE framework includes community-level adversities: poverty, discrimination, community violence, bullying, and foster care placement.

33 Recognizing Trauma Responses

▼

Trauma responses are the body's automatic survival strategies. They are not choices, character flaws, or pathology — they are the nervous system doing exactly what it evolved to do. Recognizing them in clients (and in ourselves) is a core TIC competency.

The Four Trauma Responses

ResponseNervous System StateBehavioral SignsClinical Presentation
Fight Sympathetic activation (mobilization toward threat) Anger, irritability, aggression, arguing, rigid boundaries, controlling behavior, clenched jaw/fists May present as "oppositional," "hostile," "difficult." Often mistaken for a personality issue rather than a trauma response.
Flight Sympathetic activation (mobilization away from threat) Restlessness, anxiety, panic, leaving situations, workaholism, over-exercising, perfectionism, inability to sit still May present as anxiety disorders, panic disorder, ADHD-like symptoms, or "non-compliance" with treatment.
Freeze Dorsal vagal shutdown (immobilization) Numbness, dissociation, feeling stuck, flat affect, "checking out," collapse, inability to act, speechlessness May present as depression, dissociative disorders, "resistance," "lack of motivation," or "not trying."
Fawn Social engagement system co-opted for survival People-pleasing, inability to say no, over-accommodating, merging with others' needs, loss of identity, codependency May present as "cooperative" and "easy" but is actually a survival strategy. Look for the absence of self-advocacy and difficulty expressing needs.

The Window of Tolerance

Developed by Dan Siegel, the window of tolerance describes the zone of arousal in which a person can function effectively and process information:

  • Hyperarousal (above the window): Fight/flight activation. Anxiety, panic, racing thoughts, hypervigilance, anger, inability to relax, emotional flooding. The nervous system is in "too much" mode.
  • Window of Tolerance (optimal zone): Alert but calm. Can think clearly, feel emotions without being overwhelmed, engage in relationships, and learn new information. This is where therapeutic work is most effective.
  • Hypoarousal (below the window): Freeze/shutdown. Numbness, dissociation, fatigue, flat affect, disconnection, collapse. The nervous system is in "too little" mode.
Clinical Implication
Trauma narrows the window of tolerance. Clients with extensive trauma histories may have a very narrow window, shifting rapidly between hyperarousal and hypoarousal. The first goal of trauma treatment is often to widen the window of tolerance through grounding, self-regulation skills, and co-regulation in the therapeutic relationship BEFORE processing trauma content.

Understanding Triggers

A trigger is any stimulus (sensory, emotional, relational, environmental) that activates a trauma response because it is associated with the original traumatic experience. Triggers can be:

  • Sensory: Smells, sounds, sights, textures, tastes (cologne, sirens, raised voice, being touched from behind)
  • Emotional: Feeling helpless, trapped, rejected, shamed, out of control
  • Relational: Authority figures, people who resemble the perpetrator, power dynamics, conflict
  • Environmental: Small spaces, specific locations, darkness, crowds, medical settings
  • Temporal: Anniversaries, seasons, time of day associated with the trauma
  • Internal: Physical sensations (elevated heart rate, muscle tension) that mimic the trauma state

Grounding Techniques for Trauma Responses

  • 5-4-3-2-1: Name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. Engages the senses to bring the person back to the present.
  • Grounding through feet: Press feet firmly into the floor. Notice the pressure, temperature, texture. "Notice that the ground is holding you right now."
  • Cold water/ice: Hold ice or splash cold water on the face. Activates the dive reflex and shifts the nervous system state.
  • Orienting: "Look around the room. Tell me what you see. Where are you right now? What year is it? You are safe here."
  • Bilateral tapping: Alternate tapping on knees or butterfly hug. Bilateral stimulation can help regulate the nervous system.
  • Breathing: Box breathing (4-4-4-4), extended exhale (4 in, 6 out). Exhale-emphasis activates the parasympathetic nervous system.

Person-First Language Principles

  • Put the person before the diagnosis: "person with schizophrenia," not "schizophrenic"
  • Avoid defining people by their trauma: "trauma survivor," not "trauma victim" (if the person prefers); avoid "damaged" or "broken"
  • Recognize agency: "person who uses substances," not "substance abuser"
  • Avoid language of permanence when the situation can change: "currently experiencing homelessness," not "the homeless"
  • Follow the client's lead on identity language — some people prefer identity-first language (e.g., "autistic person" rather than "person with autism")

In Clinical Documentation

Documentation Language
  • Describe behavior, not character: "Client raised voice and stood abruptly" (behavior) not "Client was aggressive" (character judgment)
  • Use quotation marks for client's own words: Client reported feeling "like a failure"
  • Note context: "Client did not attend session (reported transportation breakdown)" not "Client no-showed"
  • Separate observation from interpretation: "Client's hands were shaking and voice was trembling [observation]. This appeared consistent with anxiety [clinical interpretation]."
  • Avoid casual pejorative language: no "histrionic," "dramatic," "needy," "entitled," or "playing the victim" in clinical notes

Empowering Language in Session

  • "You've survived incredibly difficult things. What has helped you get through?" (highlights resilience)
  • "You are the expert on your own life. What do you think would help?" (affirms autonomy)
  • "That response makes sense given what you've been through." (normalizes and validates)
  • "What would you like to be different?" (invites agency, not compliance)
  • "You have choices here. What feels right for you?" (restores sense of control)

35 Organizational Trauma-Informed Care

▼

Trauma-informed care is not just a clinical approach — it is an organizational commitment. A trauma-informed organization embeds the six TIC principles into its culture, policies, physical space, and workforce practices. You cannot provide trauma-informed care in a trauma-inducing environment.

Creating a Trauma-Informed Environment

Physical Space

  • Welcoming entrance: Clear signage, natural light, comfortable seating, visible reception staff. The first 30 seconds in a building set the tone.
  • Safety signals: Clients can see exits, windows are not blocked, spaces are not cramped, lighting is adequate. No locked doors without clear explanation.
  • Waiting areas: Comfortable, not overcrowded, with resources visible. Magazines, information about services, calming decor. Play area for children.
  • Private spaces: Soundproof enough for confidential conversations. No interruptions during sessions.
  • Sensory considerations: Minimize harsh lighting, strong scents, loud noises. These can be triggering.
  • Restrooms: Accessible, clean, private. Gender-neutral options when possible.
  • Outdoor space: Access to outdoor areas when possible. Walking meetings can be more comfortable for some clients than office sessions.

Policies and Procedures

  • Universal screening: Screen for trauma history (with appropriate follow-up) rather than waiting for disclosure. ACE screening, trauma screeners integrated into intake.
  • Informed consent: Clear, thorough, in plain language. Explain limits of confidentiality, what treatment involves, client rights, and how to provide feedback or file a complaint.
  • Flexible scheduling: Accommodate clients who may have difficulty with rigid schedules due to trauma-related challenges (sleep disruption, avoidance, transportation barriers).
  • No-wrong-door approach: Clients should be able to access services regardless of where they enter the system. Minimize referral chains and waiting periods.
  • Restraint/seclusion minimization: In inpatient or residential settings, minimize or eliminate the use of restraints and seclusion. These practices are re-traumatizing.
  • Grievance processes: Accessible, non-punitive complaint processes. Clients should feel safe raising concerns without fear of retaliation or discharge.
  • Data collection: Collect only necessary information. Explain why you are asking personal questions. Allow clients to decline to answer.

Workforce Practices

  • Hiring: Include TIC competencies in job descriptions and interviews. Hire for empathy and cultural humility alongside clinical skills.
  • Training: Universal TIC training for ALL staff (clinical, admin, maintenance, security). Front-desk staff are often the first point of contact. Ongoing training, not one-time events.
  • Supervision: Reflective supervision that addresses the impact of the work on the clinician. Not just case review — process the emotional experience of the work.
  • Wellness: Organizational investment in staff wellness: reasonable caseloads, adequate breaks, wellness programming, EAP access, and a culture that does not stigmatize self-care.
  • Peer support: Employ people with lived experience of trauma, mental health challenges, and recovery. Their presence sends a message of hope and reduces power dynamics.
Organizational Self-Assessment
Use standardized TIC organizational assessment tools to evaluate where your organization stands and identify areas for improvement. Examples: the SAMHSA TIC Implementation Resource, the Trauma-Informed Care Implementation Resource Center (traumainformedcare.chcs.org), or the Attitudes Related to Trauma-Informed Care (ARTIC) scale for measuring staff attitudes.

36 Secondary Traumatic Stress / Vicarious Trauma

▼

Working with traumatized populations takes a toll. Secondary Traumatic Stress (STS), Vicarious Trauma (VT), Compassion Fatigue, and Burnout are occupational hazards of trauma work. Recognizing and addressing them is not optional — it is an ethical and professional responsibility.

Key Concepts

TermDefinitionKey Feature
Secondary Traumatic Stress (STS)PTSD-like symptoms (intrusions, avoidance, hyperarousal) resulting from indirect exposure to trauma through clinical workSudden onset, mirrors PTSD; related to specific client exposure
Vicarious Trauma (VT)Cumulative shifts in the clinician's worldview, beliefs, and sense of safety resulting from empathic engagement with traumatized clientsGradual shift in beliefs about self, others, and the world; changes cognitions about safety, trust, esteem
Compassion FatigueReduced capacity for empathy and compassion; emotional exhaustion from caring for suffering peopleLoss of empathy; the "cost of caring"
BurnoutExhaustion, cynicism, and reduced efficacy from chronic workplace stress (not specific to trauma work)Organizational/systemic; develops gradually; related to workload, control, and fairness

Warning Signs for Clinicians

Watch For These in Yourself and Colleagues
  • Emotional signs: Feeling numb or emotionally flat, irritability, anxiety or dread before sessions, difficulty experiencing positive emotions, crying easily, hopelessness about clients or the world
  • Cognitive signs: Intrusive images from client sessions, difficulty concentrating, cynicism ("nothing will help"), hypervigilance (seeing danger everywhere), questioning your competence, persistent thoughts about specific clients
  • Behavioral signs: Avoidance (canceling sessions, dreading certain clients, procrastinating on notes), isolation from colleagues, increased substance use, changes in eating/sleeping, calling in sick more often, poor boundaries (over-involvement or under-involvement)
  • Physical signs: Chronic fatigue, headaches, GI problems, weakened immune system, muscle tension, sleep disruption
  • Professional signs: Decreased empathy, going through the motions, poor documentation, ethical boundary violations, questioning career choice, reduced clinical effectiveness
  • Worldview shifts (VT-specific): "The world is not safe," "You can't trust anyone," "People are fundamentally bad," "Nothing I do makes a difference"

Prevention Strategies

  • Balanced caseload: Mix trauma-focused cases with other work when possible. Limit the number of back-to-back trauma-processing sessions.
  • Regular supervision: Not just case consultation — process the impact of the work on you. Seek supervisors trained in STS/VT.
  • Peer support: Connect with colleagues who understand the work. Debrief after difficult sessions. Break isolation.
  • Boundaries: Leave work at work. Set limits on after-hours availability. Practice saying no. Know your limits and honor them.
  • Self-monitoring: Use the Professional Quality of Life Scale (ProQOL) or the Secondary Traumatic Stress Scale (STSS) regularly to track your own wellness.
  • Personal therapy: Clinicians benefit from their own therapy, especially when doing trauma work. This is not weakness; it is professional development.
  • Training: Continued education in trauma and self-care helps maintain competence and perspective.

Self-Care Plan Template

Build Your Plan
A self-care plan should be specific, scheduled, and non-negotiable — not aspirational. Cover these domains:
  • Physical: Exercise, sleep hygiene, nutrition, medical care. What specifically? When? How often?
  • Emotional: Therapy, journaling, creative expression, activities that bring joy. What fills your cup?
  • Social: Time with friends/family not in the field, community involvement, non-work relationships. Who nourishes you?
  • Professional: Supervision, consultation, training, career development, workload management. What keeps you growing?
  • Spiritual/Existential: Meditation, nature, worship, reflection, connection to purpose/meaning. What grounds you?
  • Warning signs and action plan: My early warning signs are [specific]. When I notice them, I will [specific actions]. My accountability person is [name].
For Supervisors and Managers
STS/VT prevention is not solely the clinician's responsibility. Organizational strategies matter more than individual self-care: reasonable caseloads, flexible scheduling, reflective supervision, staff wellness programs, a culture that normalizes the impact of the work, and leadership that models self-care. If your staff are burning out, the first question is about the system, not the individual.

Mandated Reporting

NH mandated reporting obligations, procedures, legal protections, and decision-making guidance

38 NH Mandated Reporting Guide

▼

New Hampshire law requires certain professionals to report suspected abuse and neglect of children, the elderly, and incapacitated adults. This is a legal obligation, not a discretionary choice. The threshold for reporting is reasonable suspicion — you do not need proof that abuse has occurred. If you suspect it, you must report it.

Who Must Report

Under NH RSA 169-C:29 (children) and NH RSA 161-F:46 (adults), the following are mandated reporters:

  • All licensed mental health professionals (LMHCs, LICSWs, psychologists, etc.)
  • Physicians and surgeons
  • Nurses (RNs, LPNs, APRNs)
  • Teachers and school personnel
  • Childcare workers
  • Law enforcement officers
  • Clergy (when acting in a professional capacity)
  • Social workers
  • Foster parents
  • Hospital personnel
  • Any other person paid to care for a child or vulnerable adult
When in Doubt, Report
The law protects good-faith reporters and penalizes failure to report. If you are unsure whether something constitutes abuse or neglect, make the report. Let the investigating agency make that determination.

What to Report — Child Abuse and Neglect

Physical Abuse

  • Unexplained bruises or welts, especially in patterns or on areas not typically injured in play
  • Burns (cigarette, immersion, patterned from objects)
  • Unexplained fractures or dislocations
  • Injuries inconsistent with the explanation given
  • Flinching at sudden movements or being touched

Sexual Abuse

  • Age-inappropriate sexual knowledge or behavior
  • Physical complaints in the genital area
  • Disclosure of sexual contact (direct or indirect)
  • Fear of specific persons or places
  • Regressive behaviors (bedwetting, thumb-sucking in older children)

Neglect

  • Physical neglect: Malnutrition, inadequate clothing for the weather, poor hygiene, unsafe or unsanitary living conditions
  • Educational neglect: Chronic truancy, not enrolled in school, failure to provide for special education needs
  • Emotional neglect: Marked indifference to the child’s emotional or developmental needs

Emotional Abuse / Maltreatment

  • Persistent belittling, rejection, terrorizing, or isolating of the child
  • Child shows extreme anxiety, depression, withdrawal, or aggression
  • Caregiver consistently blames, shames, or demeans the child

Exposure to Domestic Violence

  • Witnessing domestic violence is considered a form of child abuse/neglect in New Hampshire
  • Children who witness DV may present with trauma symptoms even if not directly targeted

What to Report — Adult and Elder Abuse

Physical Abuse

  • Unexplained injuries or bruises in various stages of healing
  • Signs of physical restraint (wrist or ankle marks)
  • Injuries that do not match the explanation provided

Sexual Abuse

  • Unexplained STIs or genital infections
  • Genital bruising or bleeding
  • Fearfulness around specific individuals

Emotional Abuse

  • Verbal threats, intimidation, or humiliation by caregivers or family
  • Isolation from social contacts, friends, or family members
  • Caregiver controlling or dismissive behavior in sessions

Neglect

  • Self-neglect: Inability to provide for basic needs (food, shelter, hygiene, medical care) due to cognitive or physical limitations
  • Neglect by caregiver: Inadequate food, medical care, hygiene, or shelter provided by a person responsible for the adult’s care

Financial Exploitation

  • Sudden changes in financial situation or living conditions
  • Missing funds, property, or personal belongings
  • Unauthorized use of ATM or credit cards
  • Coerced changes to wills, power of attorney, or beneficiary designations
  • New “friends” or “caregivers” with sudden financial involvement

How to Report — Step by Step

Reporting Child Abuse/Neglect

Call DCYF Central Intake at 1-800-894-5533 (24/7 hotline). Identify yourself as a mandated reporter. Provide the information you have — you do not need all details to make the call.

Document in the client record that a report was made. Include date, time, who you spoke with at DCYF, and the report number if one was given.
Inform your supervisor that a report has been filed.

Information Needed for the Report

  • Who: Victim name, age, and identifying information; alleged perpetrator name and relationship to victim
  • What: Type of abuse or neglect; specific observations and behaviors
  • When: Dates and times of observations or disclosures
  • Where: Location of the incidents or observations
  • Witnesses: Any other persons with knowledge of the situation
  • Severity and immediacy: How serious is the danger and how urgent is the situation
  • Prior reports: Any previous reports you are aware of
Do Not Delay
You do NOT need to have all of this information to make a report. Report what you know. Do not delay reporting to gather more information.

Documentation Requirements

What to Document in the Client Record

  • Date and time of the report
  • Agency reported to (DCYF or BEAS)
  • Name of the person you spoke with
  • Report number (if provided)
  • Factual observations that led to the report
  • That the client was informed of the report (if applicable and safe to do so)

What NOT to Document

  • Do not include investigation details
  • Do not include opinions about whether abuse occurred
  • Do not include information obtained from the investigating agency after the report
  • Do not speculate about the perpetrator’s motives

Legal Protections and Penalties

Good Faith Immunity

NH RSA 169-C:31 provides civil and criminal immunity to reporters who act in good faith. You cannot be sued for making a report in good faith, even if the investigation does not substantiate abuse.

Penalty for Failure to Report

Under NH RSA 169-C:39, failure to report by a mandated reporter is a misdemeanor. It can also result in professional licensing consequences, including disciplinary action by your licensing board.

The Law Protects Reporters
The law is designed to PROTECT reporters. You are protected when you report in good faith. You are at legal risk when you FAIL to report.

Frequently Asked Questions

“What if the client threatens to leave treatment?”

Your legal obligation to report supersedes the therapeutic relationship. Acknowledge the client’s feelings, explain why you must report, and emphasize that your concern is their safety (or the child’s safety). Many therapeutic relationships survive — and are strengthened by — mandated reports when handled with transparency and empathy.

“What if I’m not sure it’s abuse?”

Report it. The threshold is reasonable suspicion, not certainty. It is not your job to investigate or prove abuse. That is the role of DCYF or BEAS. When in doubt, always report.

“Do I need the client’s consent to report?”

No. Mandated reporting is an exception to confidentiality. You should inform the client that you are making a report (when safe to do so), but you do not need their permission.

“What happens after I report?”

DCYF or BEAS will investigate. They may contact you for additional information. You may or may not be informed of the outcome. Your role is to make the report — the investigation is not your responsibility.

“Can I be anonymous?”

As a mandated reporter, you are required to identify yourself. However, your identity is kept confidential and is not disclosed to the alleged perpetrator.

IEA Procedures

Involuntary Emergency Admission process, legal criteria, designated receiving facilities, and client rights

39 IEA (Involuntary Emergency Admission) Procedures

▼

NH RSA 135-C:27-33 governs the involuntary emergency admission (IEA) process. An IEA is used when a person with mental illness poses a likelihood of danger to self or others and is unwilling or unable to accept voluntary treatment. This is a significant deprivation of liberty and should only be used when less restrictive alternatives have been exhausted or are clearly inadequate.

Legal Basis and Criteria

Legal Basis: NH RSA 135-C:27-33

Two criteria must BOTH be met:

  • 1. Mental illness — The person has a “mental illness” as defined by RSA 135-C:2. This does NOT include intellectual disability, substance use disorder alone, or dementia alone, though these may co-occur with mental illness.
  • 2. Likelihood of danger — The person poses a “likelihood of danger to himself or others” — defined as a substantial risk of physical harm to self (including suicide attempts, serious threats, serious self-harm) or others (threats, attempts, or acts of violence).
Both Criteria Must Be Met
A person who is dangerous but does not have a mental illness (e.g., intoxication alone) does not meet IEA criteria. A person with mental illness who is not currently dangerous does not meet IEA criteria. Both must be present simultaneously.

Who Can Petition for an IEA

Any person can file a petition for IEA, including:

  • The clinician or therapist
  • A family member
  • A friend or concerned citizen
  • Law enforcement
  • A physician

The petition must describe the specific behaviors demonstrating danger and the basis for believing the person has a mental illness.

Step-by-Step IEA Process

Step 1: Conduct a clinical assessment. Document specific behaviors, statements, and observations that demonstrate imminent danger. Include: direct quotes from the client, observed behaviors, timeline of escalation, collateral information, and your clinical judgment about the level of risk.
Step 2: Attempt less restrictive alternatives first (when safe to do so). Consider: voluntary admission (client agrees to go to the hospital), mobile crisis team response (NH Rapid Response 1-833-710-6477), safety planning with lethal means restriction, or increased outpatient services. Document that alternatives were considered and why they are inadequate.
Step 3: Contact local police or the county sheriff’s department. Law enforcement is responsible for transporting the individual to a designated receiving facility (DRF). Explain the situation, provide your clinical assessment, and give a copy of the petition if you have completed one.
Nashua Police non-emergency: (603) 594-3500
Step 4: Complete the IEA petition. The petition form is available at courts, hospitals, and police departments. It must include: the person’s name and identifying information, specific facts demonstrating danger, the basis for believing the person has a mental illness, and your name and relationship to the person.
Step 5: The person is transported to a Designated Receiving Facility (DRF). Transport is by law enforcement. The person is taken to the nearest appropriate DRF. See the list of NH DRFs below.
Step 6: DRF physician examination within 24 hours. A physician at the DRF must examine the person within 24 hours of arrival. If the physician determines that the person meets IEA criteria, the person may be held. If not, the person must be released.
Step 7: Probable cause hearing within 3 days. If the person is held, a hearing must be held within 3 calendar days (excluding weekends and holidays). The person has the right to an attorney (one will be appointed if they cannot afford one), the right to present evidence, and the right to cross-examine witnesses.

  • Mobile Crisis Teams: NH Rapid Response (1-833-710-6477) can provide on-site crisis assessment and may be able to de-escalate the situation without hospitalization.
  • Crisis Stabilization Units: Some regions have short-term (under 72 hours) crisis stabilization programs as an alternative to inpatient hospitalization.
  • Increased Outpatient Services: More frequent sessions, medication adjustments, intensive outpatient programs, or partial hospitalization.
  • Safety Planning: If the client is willing to engage, a collaborative safety plan with lethal means restriction may be sufficient.

Client Rights During the IEA Process

  • Right to be informed of the reason for the hold
  • Right to an attorney (appointed if unable to afford one)
  • Right to a probable cause hearing within 3 days
  • Right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses at the hearing
  • Right to refuse medication (except in emergencies where the person poses an imminent threat to self or others)
  • Right to communicate with family, attorney, and advocacy organizations
  • Right to contact Disability Rights Center-NH: (603) 228-0432
  • Right to humane treatment and a safe environment

Clinician Frequently Asked Questions

“Am I liable if the person hurts themselves after I don’t pursue an IEA?”

Clinicians have a duty to assess danger and take appropriate action. Document your assessment thoroughly, including why you determined IEA was not warranted and what alternative interventions were implemented. Thorough documentation of your clinical reasoning is your best protection.

“Can I transport the client myself?”

No. Law enforcement is responsible for IEA transport. Do not transport clients in your personal vehicle. If the client voluntarily agrees to go to the hospital, transport should be by ambulance, family, or crisis team.

“What if the client leaves before police arrive?”

If the client leaves, contact police immediately and inform them that the client has left and may be a danger. Provide a description and last known direction of travel. Document everything.

“How long can a person be held involuntarily?”

The initial hold is up to the probable cause hearing (within 3 days). If the court finds probable cause, the person may be held for further evaluation and treatment. A full commitment hearing must be held within 15 days.

“What if the DRF releases the person and I disagree?”

The DRF physician makes an independent medical determination. If you have concerns, document them and discuss with your supervisor. You may contact the DRF to share additional clinical information.
