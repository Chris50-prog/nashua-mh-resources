For urgent needs, request expedited intake or crisis services

Step-by-Step Enrollment Process

▼
Go to nheasy.nh.gov and create an account (or visit DHHS at 137 Temple St, Nashua)
Complete application: household size, income, citizenship/immigration status, disability information
Upload verification documents: ID, proof of income (pay stubs, tax return), proof of residency, Social Security numbers
Processing: typically 30-45 days; faster for pregnant women and children
Once approved, choose a Managed Care Organization (MCO): NH Healthy Families, Well Sense, or AmeriHealth Caritas
If not chosen within 30 days, auto-assigned to an MCO (can switch within 90 days)

Clinician note: Many clients need hands-on help with the application. Harbor Care and ServiceLink can assist with enrollment. Homeless clients can use a shelter address or care-of address.

NH Healthy Families: 1-866-769-3085 | Well Sense: 1-877-957-1300 | AmeriHealth Caritas: 1-833-709-7390
nhhealthyfamilies.com | wellsense.org | amerihealthcaritasnh.com
All three MCOs cover: outpatient MH therapy, psychiatric medication management, PHP/IOP, inpatient psychiatric, substance use treatment, MAT, non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT). MCO Comparison Notes

▼

NH Healthy Families (Centene): Largest MCO in NH. Behavioral health managed through Centurion. Generally broad provider network.

Well Sense (BMC HealthNet): Strong in integrated care. Care management programs for high-need members. Good behavioral health network.

AmeriHealth Caritas: Newest NH MCO. Focus on social determinants. Community health worker programs available.

Clinician note: If a client is having trouble finding an in-network provider, call the MCO's behavioral health line. They are required to help with provider access. Document all access problems for potential single-case agreements.

Prior Authorization for MH Services

Call client's MCO (see above)
Requirements vary by MCO and service level
Typically NO prior auth needed: outpatient therapy (initial sessions), psychiatric evaluation, crisis services, medication management. Typically REQUIRES prior auth: PHP, IOP, residential treatment, neuropsychological testing, extended outpatient beyond session limits, ABA therapy, some specialized therapies. Auth turnaround: urgent 24-72 hours, standard 5-14 days.
Prior Auth MCO Requirements Clinician Resource

Prior Auth Tips for Clinicians

▼
Always verify current auth requirements by calling the MCO provider line — requirements change frequently

SSRIs / SNRIs

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
First-line for depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, PTSD
Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) and Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs) are typically first-line pharmacotherapy for depression and most anxiety disorders.
Antidepressant First-Line Depression Anxiety

Common SSRIs / SNRIs Reference

▼

SSRIs:

  • Sertraline (Zoloft): 25-200 mg/day. First-line for many conditions. Generally well tolerated. Common: GI upset, sexual dysfunction, insomnia. FDA-approved for MDD, OCD, panic, PTSD, social anxiety, PMDD.
  • Escitalopram (Lexapro): 5-20 mg/day. Clean side-effect profile. Common: nausea, headache, sexual dysfunction. Fewer drug interactions than some SSRIs.
  • Fluoxetine (Prozac): 10-80 mg/day. Long half-life (good if adherence is an issue, less withdrawal). Activating. Can be disruptive to sleep. More drug interactions (CYP2D6 inhibitor).
  • Paroxetine (Paxil): 10-60 mg/day. More sedating. Significant withdrawal if stopped abruptly. Higher anticholinergic effects. Avoid in pregnancy (Category D). Weight gain common.
  • Fluvoxamine (Luvox): 50-300 mg/day. Primarily used for OCD. Significant drug interactions (CYP1A2 inhibitor).
  • Citalopram (Celexa): 10-40 mg/day. Max 20 mg in patients over 60 (QTc prolongation risk). Similar to escitalopram but more side effects.

SNRIs:

  • Venlafaxine (Effexor XR): 37.5-225 mg/day. Also helps neuropathic pain. Monitor blood pressure at higher doses. Significant withdrawal if stopped abruptly (taper slowly). Common: nausea, dizziness, sweating.
  • Duloxetine (Cymbalta): 30-120 mg/day. Also approved for neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia. Avoid in hepatic impairment. Common: nausea, dry mouth, constipation.
  • Desvenlafaxine (Pristiq): 50 mg/day (fixed dose). Active metabolite of venlafaxine. Fewer drug interactions.

All SSRIs/SNRIs carry a Black Box Warning for increased suicidality in children, adolescents, and young adults (under 25). Monitor closely during initiation and dose changes. Serotonin syndrome risk if combined with MAOIs, triptans, or other serotonergic agents.

Antipsychotics (First & Second Generation)

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
Schizophrenia, bipolar, treatment-resistant depression, agitation
First-generation (typical) antipsychotics primarily block D2 receptors. Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics block both D2 and 5-HT2A receptors, generally with lower EPS risk but higher metabolic risk.
Antipsychotic Metabolic Monitoring EPS Risk

Antipsychotic Reference & Monitoring

▼

Second-Generation (Atypical) — most commonly prescribed:

  • Risperidone (Risperdal): 0.5-6 mg/day. EPS risk increases above 4 mg. Prolactin elevation common. Available as LAI (Risperdal Consta).
  • Quetiapine (Seroquel): 25-800 mg/day. Sedating (often used for sleep at low doses). Weight gain, metabolic syndrome. Low EPS risk. Also used for bipolar depression.
  • Olanzapine (Zyprexa): 5-20 mg/day. Effective but highest weight gain and metabolic risk. Monitor glucose and lipids closely. Available as LAI (Zyprexa Relprevv — requires REMS).
  • Aripiprazole (Abilify): 2-30 mg/day. Partial D2 agonist. Less weight gain and metabolic effects. Can be activating/akathisia. Available as LAI (Abilify Maintena, Aristada).
  • Paliperidone (Invega): 3-12 mg/day. Active metabolite of risperidone. Available as monthly LAI (Invega Sustenna) and 3-month LAI (Invega Trinza).
  • Clozapine (Clozaril): 150-900 mg/day. Gold standard for treatment-resistant schizophrenia. REQUIRES REMS: mandatory ANC monitoring (weekly x 6 months, then biweekly x 6 months, then monthly). Risk of agranulocytosis, myocarditis, seizures, metabolic syndrome.

First-Generation (Typical):

  • Haloperidol (Haldol): 0.5-20 mg/day. High EPS risk. Available IM for acute agitation. Decanoate (LAI) for maintenance.
  • Chlorpromazine (Thorazine): 50-800 mg/day. More sedating. Higher anticholinergic effects. Orthostatic hypotension.
  • Fluphenazine (Prolixin): 1-20 mg/day. Available as decanoate (LAI).

Metabolic Monitoring Protocol (ADA/APA consensus):

  • Baseline: weight/BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting glucose, fasting lipid panel
  • 4 weeks: weight/BMI
  • 8 weeks: weight/BMI
  • 12 weeks: weight/BMI, blood pressure, fasting glucose, fasting lipid panel
  • Annually: all of the above
  • Every 5 years (or sooner if risk factors): fasting lipids

All antipsychotics carry a Black Box Warning for increased mortality in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis. Not FDA-approved for this use. Monitor for tardive dyskinesia (AIMS exam at baseline and every 6-12 months).

Mood Stabilizers

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
Bipolar disorder, mood dysregulation, impulse control
Lithium and anticonvulsant mood stabilizers require regular lab monitoring. Narrow therapeutic indices mean drug levels must be checked routinely.
Mood Stabilizer Bipolar Lab Monitoring

Mood Stabilizer Reference & Labs

▼
  • Lithium (Lithobid): 600-1800 mg/day. Therapeutic level: 0.6-1.2 mEq/L (acute mania up to 1.5). Labs: lithium level (trough, 12h post-dose), renal function (BUN/Cr), thyroid (TSH) — at baseline, every 1-2 weeks during titration, then every 3-6 months. Toxicity risk with dehydration, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, diuretics. Signs of toxicity: tremor, GI distress, confusion, ataxia. Requires reliable hydration.
  • Valproic Acid / Divalproex (Depakote): 500-2500 mg/day. Therapeutic level: 50-125 mcg/mL. Labs: drug level, CBC with platelets, LFTs — at baseline, frequently during titration, then every 6-12 months. Hepatotoxicity risk (higher in children under 2). Teratogenic — avoid in women of childbearing potential (neural tube defects). Weight gain, hair loss, tremor, GI upset common.
  • Lamotrigine (Lamictal): 25-400 mg/day. Must titrate very slowly (risk of Stevens-Johnson Syndrome, especially with rapid titration or valproate co-administration). Typical titration: 25 mg/day x 2 weeks, then 50 mg/day x 2 weeks, then increase by 50 mg every 1-2 weeks. Better for bipolar depression than mania. Generally well tolerated (less weight gain, less sedation). No routine lab monitoring required.
  • Carbamazepine (Tegretol): 400-1600 mg/day. Therapeutic level: 4-12 mcg/mL. Labs: drug level, CBC, LFTs, electrolytes (hyponatremia risk) — at baseline and periodically. Potent CYP3A4 inducer (many drug interactions, reduces effectiveness of oral contraceptives). HLA-B*1502 screening recommended for patients of Asian descent (SJS risk).

Clinician note: Lithium and valproate both require pregnancy testing before initiation in women of childbearing age. Lamotrigine is the preferred mood stabilizer in pregnancy (Category C, lower teratogenic risk than valproate or lithium).

Anxiolytics / Benzodiazepines

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
Anxiety, panic, insomnia, acute agitation, seizures, alcohol withdrawal
Benzodiazepines are effective but carry significant risks of dependence, tolerance, and withdrawal. Generally recommended for short-term or PRN use only. SSRIs/SNRIs are first-line for chronic anxiety disorders.
Addiction Risk Anxiolytic Controlled Schedule IV

Benzodiazepine Reference & Warnings

▼

Black Box Warning: Concomitant use of benzodiazepines and opioids may result in profound sedation, respiratory depression, coma, and death.

  • Alprazolam (Xanax): 0.25-4 mg/day. Short-acting. High abuse potential. Difficult to discontinue. Avoid if possible.
  • Lorazepam (Ativan): 0.5-6 mg/day. Intermediate-acting. No active metabolites (safer in hepatic impairment). Available IM. Commonly used in acute settings.
  • Clonazepam (Klonopin): 0.25-4 mg/day. Longer-acting (less interdose rebound anxiety). Often used for panic disorder, social anxiety.
  • Diazepam (Valium): 2-40 mg/day. Long-acting with active metabolites. Used in alcohol withdrawal protocols (CIWA). Accumulates in elderly.

Non-Benzodiazepine Anxiolytics (preferred for chronic use):

  • Buspirone (Buspar): 10-60 mg/day (divided BID-TID). Takes 2-4 weeks for effect. No dependence risk. First-line non-SSRI for GAD. Does not work PRN.
  • Hydroxyzine (Vistaril/Atarax): 25-100 mg PRN. Antihistamine with anxiolytic properties. Sedating. No dependence. Alternative to benzodiazepines for PRN anxiety.
  • Gabapentin (Neurontin): 300-2400 mg/day. Used off-label for anxiety. Some abuse potential (now Schedule V in some states). Renal dosing required.

Clinician note: Before prescribing benzodiazepines, check the NH PDMP (Prescription Drug Monitoring Program). Taper benzodiazepines gradually — abrupt discontinuation can cause seizures. Generally reduce by 10-25% every 1-2 weeks. Elderly patients: increased fall risk, cognitive impairment, paradoxical agitation. Avoid if possible in patients with SUD history.

High-Risk Interaction Pairs

▼
  • SSRIs/SNRIs + MAOIs: Serotonin syndrome (potentially fatal). Minimum 14-day washout between MAOIs and most SSRIs. 5 weeks for fluoxetine (long half-life).
  • SSRIs/SNRIs + Triptans: Serotonin syndrome risk. Monitor if co-prescribed.
  • Benzodiazepines + Opioids: Respiratory depression, death. Black box warning on both classes.
  • Lithium + NSAIDs: Increased lithium levels (toxicity risk). Monitor levels if NSAIDs required.
  • Lithium + ACE inhibitors/ARBs: Increased lithium levels. Monitor closely.
  • Lithium + Diuretics (especially thiazides): Increased lithium levels. Loop diuretics safer but still require monitoring.
  • Carbamazepine + oral contraceptives: Reduced contraceptive effectiveness (enzyme induction). Counsel patients.
  • Fluvoxamine + clozapine: Dramatically increased clozapine levels (CYP1A2 inhibition). Avoid combination or reduce clozapine dose significantly.
  • Valproate + lamotrigine: Doubled lamotrigine levels. Must use slower titration schedule and lower target dose.
  • QTc-prolonging combinations: Citalopram/escitalopram + antipsychotics + methadone — additive QTc prolongation. ECG monitoring recommended.

Key Black Box Warnings

▼
  • Antidepressants (all): Increased risk of suicidal thinking and behavior in children, adolescents, and young adults (under 25) during initial treatment. Monitor closely for worsening, suicidality, or unusual behavior changes.
  • Antipsychotics (all): Increased risk of death in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis. Not approved for this indication.
  • Benzodiazepines + Opioids: Concomitant use risks profound sedation, respiratory depression, coma, death.
  • Valproate: Hepatotoxicity (especially children under 2), pancreatitis, and teratogenicity (neural tube defects). Contraindicated in pregnancy for migraine prevention.
  • Carbamazepine: Serious dermatologic reactions including Stevens-Johnson Syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis. Aplastic anemia, agranulocytosis.
  • Clozapine: Severe neutropenia (agranulocytosis). Requires REMS program with mandatory ANC monitoring. Also: seizures, myocarditis, cardiomyopathy.
  • Bupropion (Wellbutrin): Seizure risk (dose-dependent). Max 450 mg/day. Contraindicated in seizure disorders, bulimia, anorexia.
  • Atomoxetine (Strattera): Suicidal ideation in children and adolescents.
  • Stimulants (amphetamines, methylphenidate): High potential for abuse. Cardiovascular risks (sudden death reported). Not for patients with structural cardiac abnormalities.

Clinical Practice Considerations

▼
  • Use correct names and pronouns. Ask and document preferred name and pronouns. Use them consistently. Correct yourself and move on if you make a mistake.
  • Do not assume sexual orientation or gender identity. Use open-ended questions. Do not conflate sexual orientation with gender identity.
  • Understand minority stress. LGBTQ+ individuals face higher rates of depression, anxiety, suicidality, and substance use due to stigma, discrimination, and victimization — not because of their identity.
  • Conversion therapy is illegal in NH for minors (RSA 332-L). It is considered harmful and unethical by all major mental health professional organizations.
  • Family rejection is a major risk factor. Family therapy and PFLAG can help. Family rejection of LGBTQ+ youth is associated with 8.4x higher rates of suicide attempts.
  • Rural/suburban NH context: Some LGBTQ+ individuals in the Greater Nashua area may face isolation, especially in surrounding smaller towns. Telehealth and online communities can supplement in-person support.
  • Free training: National LGBTQIA+ Health Education Center offers free CME/CEU courses on affirming care.

Best Practices for Using Interpreters

▼
  • Always use professional interpreters for clinical sessions. Do not use family members (especially children) as interpreters — clinical accuracy and confidentiality are compromised.
  • Request interpreters trained in mental health terminology when possible.
  • Speak directly to the client, not the interpreter. Use short sentences.
  • Allow extra time — interpreted sessions take roughly twice as long.
  • Brief the interpreter before the session on topics to be covered and any sensitive content.
  • For Nashua-area sessions: Portuguese and Spanish interpreters are most commonly needed, followed by Bhutanese/Nepali, Swahili, Arabic, and French/Haitian Creole.

Cultural Considerations for Nashua-Area Populations

Clinician resource
Nashua has significant Brazilian, Bhutanese/Nepali, African, and other immigrant communities
Greater Nashua has one of NH's most diverse populations. Clinicians should be aware of cultural factors affecting mental health engagement and treatment.
Cultural Competency Clinician Resource Nashua

Key Cultural Considerations

▼
  • Brazilian community: Large Portuguese-speaking population in Nashua. Mental health stigma may be significant. Somatic complaints may be primary presenting concern. Church communities are important supports. Many community members may have uncertain immigration status — assure confidentiality.
  • Bhutanese/Nepali refugee community: Resettled Bhutanese refugees are a significant population in the Nashua-Manchester corridor. High rates of PTSD and depression from camp experiences. Suicide rate in Bhutanese refugee community has been elevated nationally. Culturally, mental illness may be attributed to spiritual causes. Interpreter services in Nepali critical.
  • African refugee communities: Diverse populations from multiple countries (Democratic Republic of Congo, Somalia, Burundi, others). Experiences of war, displacement, sexual violence. Mental health concepts may not translate directly. Community elders and religious leaders are important cultural brokers.
  • General considerations: Many refugee/immigrant clients have experienced torture, war, sexual violence, family separation, and/or detention. Immigration enforcement anxiety affects help-seeking behavior. Build trust slowly. Explain confidentiality protections clearly and repeatedly. Use trauma-informed care approaches.

Clinical Considerations

▼

  • Explain confidentiality in concrete terms: “I will not share information with immigration, police, or government agencies”

Why Vet Centers Matter

▼

Vet Centers are separate from the VA hospital system and maintain their own confidential records. Many veterans who distrust or avoid the VA system are willing to use Vet Centers because of their community-based, non-bureaucratic approach.

Key advantages:

  • No VA enrollment or service-connection required for eligible populations
  • Evening and some Saturday hours available
  • Records are separate from VA medical records (important for some veterans)
  • More informal, less clinical setting
  • Counselors are often veterans themselves

Clinician note: For veterans resistant to VA care, Vet Centers are often the best entry point. The 24/7 Vet Center Call Center (1-877-927-8387) can also connect veterans to support after hours.

Benefits Navigation Tips

▼

Key VA benefits relevant to MH clients:

  • VA Disability Compensation: Tax-free monthly payment for service-connected conditions (including PTSD, depression, TBI). Rating 0-100%. Higher ratings = higher payment. File with help from NH State Veterans Council or accredited VSO.
  • VA Pension: For wartime veterans with limited income. Need-based. Can include Aid & Attendance supplement for those requiring assistance with daily activities.
  • Vocational Rehabilitation (Chapter 31): Education and job training for veterans with service-connected disabilities that create employment barriers.
  • Caregiver Support Program: Stipend and support for caregivers of severely disabled veterans.
  • Veterans Treatment Court: Available through Nashua District Court for veteran defendants.

Clinician note: A strong nexus letter from a treating provider linking a mental health condition to military service can significantly impact a disability claim. Document the connection between symptoms and service-related events clearly. The NH State Veterans Council at (603) 624-9230 provides free claims assistance.
