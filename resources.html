▼

Provide client name, DOB, insurance info, and brief clinical summary
Client scheduled for intake assessment (typically 1-2 weeks; faster for urgent needs)
Intake assessment determines level of care and assigns treatment team
For urgent needs, request expedited intake or crisis services

Clinician note: GNMHC is mandated to serve individuals with SMI and cannot refuse service to this population.

SSRIs / SNRIs

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
First-line for depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, PTSD
Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) and Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs) are typically first-line pharmacotherapy for depression and most anxiety disorders.
Antidepressant First-Line Depression Anxiety

Common SSRIs / SNRIs Reference

▼

SSRIs:

  • Sertraline (Zoloft): 25-200 mg/day. First-line for many conditions. Generally well tolerated. Common: GI upset, sexual dysfunction, insomnia. FDA-approved for MDD, OCD, panic, PTSD, social anxiety, PMDD.
  • Escitalopram (Lexapro): 5-20 mg/day. Clean side-effect profile. Common: nausea, headache, sexual dysfunction. Fewer drug interactions than some SSRIs.
  • Fluoxetine (Prozac): 10-80 mg/day. Long half-life (good if adherence is an issue, less withdrawal). Activating. Can be disruptive to sleep. More drug interactions (CYP2D6 inhibitor).
  • Paroxetine (Paxil): 10-60 mg/day. More sedating. Significant withdrawal if stopped abruptly. Higher anticholinergic effects. Avoid in pregnancy (Category D). Weight gain common.
  • Fluvoxamine (Luvox): 50-300 mg/day. Primarily used for OCD. Significant drug interactions (CYP1A2 inhibitor).
  • Citalopram (Celexa): 10-40 mg/day. Max 20 mg in patients over 60 (QTc prolongation risk). Similar to escitalopram but more side effects.

SNRIs:

  • Venlafaxine (Effexor XR): 37.5-225 mg/day. Also helps neuropathic pain. Monitor blood pressure at higher doses. Significant withdrawal if stopped abruptly (taper slowly). Common: nausea, dizziness, sweating.
  • Duloxetine (Cymbalta): 30-120 mg/day. Also approved for neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia. Avoid in hepatic impairment. Common: nausea, dry mouth, constipation.
  • Desvenlafaxine (Pristiq): 50 mg/day (fixed dose). Active metabolite of venlafaxine. Fewer drug interactions.

All SSRIs/SNRIs carry a Black Box Warning for increased suicidality in children, adolescents, and young adults (under 25). Monitor closely during initiation and dose changes. Serotonin syndrome risk if combined with MAOIs, triptans, or other serotonergic agents.

Antipsychotics (First & Second Generation)

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
Schizophrenia, bipolar, treatment-resistant depression, agitation
First-generation (typical) antipsychotics primarily block D2 receptors. Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics block both D2 and 5-HT2A receptors, generally with lower EPS risk but higher metabolic risk.
Antipsychotic Metabolic Monitoring EPS Risk

Antipsychotic Reference & Monitoring

▼

Second-Generation (Atypical) — most commonly prescribed:

  • Risperidone (Risperdal): 0.5-6 mg/day. EPS risk increases above 4 mg. Prolactin elevation common. Available as LAI (Risperdal Consta).
  • Quetiapine (Seroquel): 25-800 mg/day. Sedating (often used for sleep at low doses). Weight gain, metabolic syndrome. Low EPS risk. Also used for bipolar depression.
  • Olanzapine (Zyprexa): 5-20 mg/day. Effective but highest weight gain and metabolic risk. Monitor glucose and lipids closely. Available as LAI (Zyprexa Relprevv — requires REMS).
  • Aripiprazole (Abilify): 2-30 mg/day. Partial D2 agonist. Less weight gain and metabolic effects. Can be activating/akathisia. Available as LAI (Abilify Maintena, Aristada).
  • Paliperidone (Invega): 3-12 mg/day. Active metabolite of risperidone. Available as monthly LAI (Invega Sustenna) and 3-month LAI (Invega Trinza).
  • Clozapine (Clozaril): 150-900 mg/day. Gold standard for treatment-resistant schizophrenia. REQUIRES REMS: mandatory ANC monitoring (weekly x 6 months, then biweekly x 6 months, then monthly). Risk of agranulocytosis, myocarditis, seizures, metabolic syndrome.

First-Generation (Typical):

  • Haloperidol (Haldol): 0.5-20 mg/day. High EPS risk. Available IM for acute agitation. Decanoate (LAI) for maintenance.
  • Chlorpromazine (Thorazine): 50-800 mg/day. More sedating. Higher anticholinergic effects. Orthostatic hypotension.
  • Fluphenazine (Prolixin): 1-20 mg/day. Available as decanoate (LAI).

Metabolic Monitoring Protocol (ADA/APA consensus):

  • Baseline: weight/BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting glucose, fasting lipid panel
  • 4 weeks: weight/BMI
  • 8 weeks: weight/BMI
  • 12 weeks: weight/BMI, blood pressure, fasting glucose, fasting lipid panel
  • Annually: all of the above
  • Every 5 years (or sooner if risk factors): fasting lipids

All antipsychotics carry a Black Box Warning for increased mortality in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis. Not FDA-approved for this use. Monitor for tardive dyskinesia (AIMS exam at baseline and every 6-12 months).

Mood Stabilizers

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
Bipolar disorder, mood dysregulation, impulse control
Lithium and anticonvulsant mood stabilizers require regular lab monitoring. Narrow therapeutic indices mean drug levels must be checked routinely.
Mood Stabilizer Bipolar Lab Monitoring

Mood Stabilizer Reference & Labs

▼
  • Lithium (Lithobid): 600-1800 mg/day. Therapeutic level: 0.6-1.2 mEq/L (acute mania up to 1.5). Labs: lithium level (trough, 12h post-dose), renal function (BUN/Cr), thyroid (TSH) — at baseline, every 1-2 weeks during titration, then every 3-6 months. Toxicity risk with dehydration, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, diuretics. Signs of toxicity: tremor, GI distress, confusion, ataxia. Requires reliable hydration.
  • Valproic Acid / Divalproex (Depakote): 500-2500 mg/day. Therapeutic level: 50-125 mcg/mL. Labs: drug level, CBC with platelets, LFTs — at baseline, frequently during titration, then every 6-12 months. Hepatotoxicity risk (higher in children under 2). Teratogenic — avoid in women of childbearing potential (neural tube defects). Weight gain, hair loss, tremor, GI upset common.
  • Lamotrigine (Lamictal): 25-400 mg/day. Must titrate very slowly (risk of Stevens-Johnson Syndrome, especially with rapid titration or valproate co-administration). Typical titration: 25 mg/day x 2 weeks, then 50 mg/day x 2 weeks, then increase by 50 mg every 1-2 weeks. Better for bipolar depression than mania. Generally well tolerated (less weight gain, less sedation). No routine lab monitoring required.
  • Carbamazepine (Tegretol): 400-1600 mg/day. Therapeutic level: 4-12 mcg/mL. Labs: drug level, CBC, LFTs, electrolytes (hyponatremia risk) — at baseline and periodically. Potent CYP3A4 inducer (many drug interactions, reduces effectiveness of oral contraceptives). HLA-B*1502 screening recommended for patients of Asian descent (SJS risk).

Clinician note: Lithium and valproate both require pregnancy testing before initiation in women of childbearing age. Lamotrigine is the preferred mood stabilizer in pregnancy (Category C, lower teratogenic risk than valproate or lithium).

Anxiolytics / Benzodiazepines

Prescribing info: dailymed.nlm.nih.gov
Anxiety, panic, insomnia, acute agitation, seizures, alcohol withdrawal
Benzodiazepines are effective but carry significant risks of dependence, tolerance, and withdrawal. Generally recommended for short-term or PRN use only. SSRIs/SNRIs are first-line for chronic anxiety disorders.
Addiction Risk Anxiolytic Controlled Schedule IV

Benzodiazepine Reference & Warnings

▼

Black Box Warning: Concomitant use of benzodiazepines and opioids may result in profound sedation, respiratory depression, coma, and death.

  • Alprazolam (Xanax): 0.25-4 mg/day. Short-acting. High abuse potential. Difficult to discontinue. Avoid if possible.
  • Lorazepam (Ativan): 0.5-6 mg/day. Intermediate-acting. No active metabolites (safer in hepatic impairment). Available IM. Commonly used in acute settings.
  • Clonazepam (Klonopin): 0.25-4 mg/day. Longer-acting (less interdose rebound anxiety). Often used for panic disorder, social anxiety.
  • Diazepam (Valium): 2-40 mg/day. Long-acting with active metabolites. Used in alcohol withdrawal protocols (CIWA). Accumulates in elderly.

Non-Benzodiazepine Anxiolytics (preferred for chronic use):

  • Buspirone (Buspar): 10-60 mg/day (divided BID-TID). Takes 2-4 weeks for effect. No dependence risk. First-line non-SSRI for GAD. Does not work PRN.
  • Hydroxyzine (Vistaril/Atarax): 25-100 mg PRN. Antihistamine with anxiolytic properties. Sedating. No dependence. Alternative to benzodiazepines for PRN anxiety.
  • Gabapentin (Neurontin): 300-2400 mg/day. Used off-label for anxiety. Some abuse potential (now Schedule V in some states). Renal dosing required.

Clinician note: Before prescribing benzodiazepines, check the NH PDMP (Prescription Drug Monitoring Program). Taper benzodiazepines gradually — abrupt discontinuation can cause seizures. Generally reduce by 10-25% every 1-2 weeks. Elderly patients: increased fall risk, cognitive impairment, paradoxical agitation. Avoid if possible in patients with SUD history.

High-Risk Interaction Pairs

▼
  • SSRIs/SNRIs + MAOIs: Serotonin syndrome (potentially fatal). Minimum 14-day washout between MAOIs and most SSRIs. 5 weeks for fluoxetine (long half-life).
  • SSRIs/SNRIs + Triptans: Serotonin syndrome risk. Monitor if co-prescribed.
  • Benzodiazepines + Opioids: Respiratory depression, death. Black box warning on both classes.
  • Lithium + NSAIDs: Increased lithium levels (toxicity risk). Monitor levels if NSAIDs required.
  • Lithium + ACE inhibitors/ARBs: Increased lithium levels. Monitor closely.
  • Lithium + Diuretics (especially thiazides): Increased lithium levels. Loop diuretics safer but still require monitoring.
  • Carbamazepine + oral contraceptives: Reduced contraceptive effectiveness (enzyme induction). Counsel patients.
  • Fluvoxamine + clozapine: Dramatically increased clozapine levels (CYP1A2 inhibition). Avoid combination or reduce clozapine dose significantly.
  • Valproate + lamotrigine: Doubled lamotrigine levels. Must use slower titration schedule and lower target dose.
  • QTc-prolonging combinations: Citalopram/escitalopram + antipsychotics + methadone — additive QTc prolongation. ECG monitoring recommended.

Key Black Box Warnings

▼
  • Antidepressants (all): Increased risk of suicidal thinking and behavior in children, adolescents, and young adults (under 25) during initial treatment. Monitor closely for worsening, suicidality, or unusual behavior changes.
  • Antipsychotics (all): Increased risk of death in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis. Not approved for this indication.
  • Benzodiazepines + Opioids: Concomitant use risks profound sedation, respiratory depression, coma, death.
  • Valproate: Hepatotoxicity (especially children under 2), pancreatitis, and teratogenicity (neural tube defects). Contraindicated in pregnancy for migraine prevention.
  • Carbamazepine: Serious dermatologic reactions including Stevens-Johnson Syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis. Aplastic anemia, agranulocytosis.
  • Clozapine: Severe neutropenia (agranulocytosis). Requires REMS program with mandatory ANC monitoring. Also: seizures, myocarditis, cardiomyopathy.
  • Bupropion (Wellbutrin): Seizure risk (dose-dependent). Max 450 mg/day. Contraindicated in seizure disorders, bulimia, anorexia.
  • Atomoxetine (Strattera): Suicidal ideation in children and adolescents.
  • Stimulants (amphetamines, methylphenidate): High potential for abuse. Cardiovascular risks (sudden death reported). Not for patients with structural cardiac abnormalities.

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

DSM-5-TR Section II | F32.x / F33.x
5+ symptoms during the same 2-week period; at least one must be depressed mood or anhedonia. Additional symptoms: changes in sleep, appetite/weight, energy (fatigue), concentration, psychomotor activity, guilt/worthlessness, suicidal ideation.
Mood Disorder PHQ-9

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria: Five or more of the following during a 2-week period, representing a change from previous functioning: (1) depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day; (2) markedly diminished interest or pleasure (anhedonia); (3) significant weight loss/gain or appetite change; (4) insomnia or hypersomnia; (5) psychomotor agitation or retardation; (6) fatigue or loss of energy; (7) feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt; (8) diminished concentration or indecisiveness; (9) recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation.

Differential Diagnosis: Bipolar disorder (screen with MDQ — always rule out prior manic/hypomanic episodes), bereavement/prolonged grief disorder, medical conditions (hypothyroidism, anemia, vitamin D deficiency), substance-induced depressive disorder.

Common Comorbidities: Anxiety disorders (50%+ co-occurrence), substance use disorders, chronic pain conditions, insomnia disorder.

Recommended Assessment Tools: PHQ-9 (severity and monitoring), Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS), Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II).

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

DSM-5-TR Section II | F41.1
Excessive anxiety and worry (apprehensive expectation) occurring more days than not for at least 6 months, about a number of events or activities. Difficult to control the worry. 3+ associated symptoms required.
Anxiety Disorder GAD-7

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria: Excessive worry more days than not for 6+ months, plus 3 or more of: (1) restlessness or feeling keyed up; (2) being easily fatigued; (3) difficulty concentrating or mind going blank; (4) irritability; (5) muscle tension; (6) sleep disturbance (difficulty falling/staying asleep, restless/unsatisfying sleep).

Differential Diagnosis: Panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, OCD, PTSD, medical conditions (hyperthyroidism, cardiac arrhythmia, pheochromocytoma), substance/caffeine-induced anxiety.

Common Comorbidities: Major depressive disorder (60%+ co-occurrence), other anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, somatic symptom disorders.

Recommended Assessment Tools: GAD-7 (screening and severity), Penn State Worry Questionnaire (PSWQ), Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI).

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

DSM-5-TR Section II | F43.10
Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. Requires symptoms from four clusters: intrusion, avoidance, negative cognitions/mood, and arousal/reactivity changes. Duration exceeds 1 month.
Trauma-Related PCL-5

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria: Criterion A: Exposure to trauma (direct experience, witnessing, learning about close other, repeated professional exposure). Criterion B: Intrusion symptoms (flashbacks, nightmares, distress at cues). Criterion C: Avoidance of trauma-related stimuli. Criterion D: Negative alterations in cognitions and mood (amnesia, negative beliefs, distorted blame, persistent negative emotions, diminished interest, detachment, inability to experience positive emotions). Criterion E: Marked alterations in arousal/reactivity (irritability, reckless behavior, hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, concentration problems, sleep disturbance). Duration >1 month.

Differential Diagnosis: Acute stress disorder (duration <1 month), adjustment disorder (non-Criterion A stressor), TBI (overlapping symptoms), dissociative disorders, major depressive disorder.

Common Comorbidities: Major depressive disorder, substance use disorders, anxiety disorders, traumatic brain injury.

Recommended Assessment Tools: PCL-5 (PTSD Checklist), CAPS-5 (Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale — gold standard), Life Events Checklist (LEC-5), Primary Care PTSD Screen (PC-PTSD-5).

Bipolar I and II Disorders

DSM-5-TR Section II | F31.x
Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode (7+ days or hospitalization). Bipolar II requires hypomanic episode (4+ days, no hospitalization) plus major depressive episode. Both involve distinct periods of abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with increased energy.
Mood Disorder MDQ

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria (Manic Episode): Elevated, expansive, or irritable mood AND increased goal-directed energy/activity lasting 7+ days (or any duration if hospitalization required), plus 3+ of (4+ if mood is only irritable): (1) inflated self-esteem or grandiosity; (2) decreased need for sleep; (3) more talkative or pressured speech; (4) flight of ideas or racing thoughts; (5) distractibility; (6) increase in goal-directed activity or psychomotor agitation; (7) excessive involvement in risky activities. Hypomanic episode: same criteria, 4+ consecutive days, not severe enough for hospitalization or psychotic features.

Differential Diagnosis: Major depressive disorder (most common misdiagnosis — always screen for mania), ADHD (overlapping hyperactivity/impulsivity), borderline personality disorder (mood instability), substance-induced mood disorder, schizoaffective disorder.

Common Comorbidities: Anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, ADHD, eating disorders.

Recommended Assessment Tools: Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ), Young Mania Rating Scale (YMRS), mood charting, Bipolar Spectrum Diagnostic Scale (BSDS).

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

DSM-5-TR Section II | F90.x
Persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development. 6+ symptoms (5+ for adults 17+) in one or both domains. Onset before age 12. Present in 2+ settings.
Neurodevelopmental ASRS

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria: Inattention symptoms include: fails to give close attention to details, difficulty sustaining attention, does not seem to listen, fails to finish tasks, difficulty organizing, avoids sustained mental effort, loses things, easily distracted, forgetful. Hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms include: fidgets, leaves seat, runs/climbs inappropriately, unable to play quietly, “on the go,” talks excessively, blurts out answers, difficulty waiting turn, interrupts/intrudes. Presentations: combined, predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive.

Differential Diagnosis: Anxiety disorders, depression, bipolar disorder, substance use disorders, sleep disorders, thyroid dysfunction, trauma/PTSD (especially in children).

Common Comorbidities: Anxiety disorders (25-50%), depression, oppositional defiant disorder/conduct disorder, substance use disorders, learning disabilities.

Recommended Assessment Tools: ASRS (Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale), Conners scales (child/adult), Vanderbilt Assessment Scale (child), Wender Utah Rating Scale (WURS, retrospective childhood symptoms).

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

DSM-5-TR Section II | F60.3
Pervasive pattern of instability of interpersonal relationships, self-image, and affects, and marked impulsivity, beginning by early adulthood. 5+ of 9 criteria required.
Personality Disorder MSI-BPD

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria (5+ of 9): (1) Frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment; (2) pattern of unstable and intense interpersonal relationships (idealization/devaluation); (3) identity disturbance — markedly unstable self-image or sense of self; (4) impulsivity in 2+ areas that are potentially self-damaging; (5) recurrent suicidal behavior, gestures, threats, or self-mutilating behavior; (6) affective instability — marked reactivity of mood; (7) chronic feelings of emptiness; (8) inappropriate, intense anger or difficulty controlling anger; (9) transient, stress-related paranoid ideation or severe dissociative symptoms.

Differential Diagnosis: Bipolar II disorder (episodic mood shifts vs. chronic pattern — key differentiator is time course), complex PTSD (overlapping symptoms, especially emotional dysregulation), major depressive disorder, histrionic personality disorder.

Common Comorbidities: Major depressive disorder, substance use disorders, eating disorders (especially bulimia), PTSD, other anxiety disorders.

Recommended Assessment Tools: MSI-BPD (McLean Screening Instrument for BPD), ZAN-BPD (Zanarini Rating Scale), Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5 Personality Disorders (SCID-5-PD).

Substance Use Disorders (General)

DSM-5-TR Section II | F10-F19 (varies by substance)
Problematic pattern of substance use leading to clinically significant impairment or distress. 2+ of 11 criteria within a 12-month period. Severity: mild (2-3), moderate (4-5), severe (6+).
SUD AUDIT SBIRT

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria (2+ of 11): Impaired Control: (1) taken in larger amounts/longer than intended; (2) persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down; (3) great deal of time obtaining/using/recovering; (4) craving. Social Impairment: (5) failure to fulfill major role obligations; (6) continued use despite persistent social/interpersonal problems; (7) important activities given up or reduced. Risky Use: (8) recurrent use in physically hazardous situations; (9) continued use despite knowing it causes/exacerbates physical or psychological problems. Pharmacological: (10) tolerance; (11) withdrawal.

Differential Diagnosis: Recreational/non-pathological use, medication-induced symptoms, other psychiatric disorders with behavioral overlap (e.g., bipolar impulsivity).

Common Comorbidities: Major depressive disorder, anxiety disorders, PTSD, bipolar disorder, antisocial personality disorder.

Recommended Assessment Tools: AUDIT (alcohol), DAST-10 (drugs), CAGE questionnaire, SBIRT framework (Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment), CIWA-Ar (alcohol withdrawal), COWS (opioid withdrawal).

Schizophrenia Spectrum Disorders

DSM-5-TR Section II | F20.x
2+ characteristic symptoms (delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized/catatonic behavior, negative symptoms) for a significant portion of a 1-month period. At least one must be delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech. Continuous signs for 6+ months.
Psychotic Disorder PANSS

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria: Two or more of the following, each present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period (at least one must be 1, 2, or 3): (1) delusions; (2) hallucinations; (3) disorganized speech (e.g., frequent derailment or incoherence); (4) grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior; (5) negative symptoms (diminished emotional expression, avolition, alogia, anhedonia, asociality). Level of functioning markedly below premorbid level. Continuous signs of disturbance for at least 6 months.

Differential Diagnosis: Brief psychotic disorder (<1 month), schizophreniform disorder (1-6 months), schizoaffective disorder, delusional disorder, substance-induced psychotic disorder, medical conditions (delirium, neurological conditions, autoimmune encephalitis).

Common Comorbidities: Substance use disorders (50%+ co-occurrence), major depressive disorder, anxiety disorders, metabolic syndrome (often treatment-related).

Recommended Assessment Tools: PANSS (Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale), BPRS (Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale), CGI-S (Clinical Global Impression — Severity), MATRICS Consensus Cognitive Battery (cognitive assessment).

Adjustment Disorders

DSM-5-TR Section II | F43.2x
Emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor occurring within 3 months of stressor onset. Symptoms are out of proportion to the stressor or cause significant functional impairment. Resolves within 6 months of stressor termination.
Trauma/Stressor-Related Clinical Interview

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria: Development of emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor within 3 months of onset of the stressor. Clinically significant as evidenced by either: (1) marked distress out of proportion to the severity of the stressor, or (2) significant impairment in social, occupational, or other areas of functioning. Does not meet criteria for another mental disorder and is not merely an exacerbation of a preexisting condition. Subtypes: with depressed mood, with anxiety, with mixed anxiety and depressed mood, with disturbance of conduct, with mixed disturbance of emotions and conduct, unspecified.

Differential Diagnosis: Major depressive disorder (duration and severity), generalized anxiety disorder (chronic course), PTSD (requires Criterion A trauma), acute stress disorder, normal stress response/grief.

Common Comorbidities: Pre-existing mental health conditions may be exacerbated; suicidal ideation can occur even with this “less severe” diagnosis — always assess.

Recommended Assessment Tools: Clinical interview (no widely-used standardized screening instrument), functional assessment, Adjustment Disorder New Module (ADNM-20 where available).

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

DSM-5-TR Section II | F42.x
Presence of obsessions (recurrent, persistent, intrusive, unwanted thoughts/urges/images causing marked anxiety) and/or compulsions (repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed to reduce anxiety). Time-consuming (1+ hour/day) or causes clinically significant distress or functional impairment.
OCD-Related Y-BOCS

Clinical Details

▼

Key Diagnostic Criteria: Obsessions: recurrent and persistent thoughts, urges, or images experienced as intrusive and unwanted, causing marked anxiety or distress. The individual attempts to ignore/suppress them or neutralize them with another thought or action (i.e., compulsion). Compulsions: repetitive behaviors (hand washing, ordering, checking) or mental acts (praying, counting, repeating words silently) performed in response to an obsession or according to rigid rules, aimed at preventing or reducing anxiety or a dreaded event. Time-consuming (1+ hour/day) or cause significant distress/impairment. Specify if with good/fair insight, poor insight, or absent insight/delusional beliefs.

Differential Diagnosis: Generalized anxiety disorder (worry vs. intrusive ego-dystonic thoughts), body dysmorphic disorder, hoarding disorder, trichotillomania, excoriation disorder, eating disorders (ritualistic eating), psychotic disorders (delusions vs. obsessions with preserved insight — key distinction).

Common Comorbidities: Major depressive disorder, other anxiety disorders, tic disorders (especially in childhood-onset OCD), eating disorders, body dysmorphic disorder.

Recommended Assessment Tools: Y-BOCS (Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale — gold standard), OCI-R (Obsessive-Compulsive Inventory — Revised), DOCS (Dimensional Obsessive-Compulsive Scale), CY-BOCS (children/adolescents).

Working with Interpreters — Best Practices

Clinical guidelines for effectively working with interpreters in mental health settings, including pre-session preparation, positioning, and debriefing.
Best Practices Clinical Guide

Practice Guide

▼

Pre-Session Briefing: Brief the interpreter on the session’s purpose, any sensitive topics that may arise, and relevant clinical terminology. Discuss confidentiality expectations. Clarify whether consecutive or simultaneous interpretation will be used.

During the Session: Speak directly to the client, not the interpreter (“How are you feeling?” not “Ask her how she’s feeling”). Speak in short segments to allow accurate interpretation. Manage pace — pause after 2-3 sentences. Avoid jargon, idioms, and metaphors that may not translate. Maintain eye contact with the client.

Positioning: Triangular seating arrangement with the interpreter slightly behind and to the side of the client, so the client and clinician face each other. For video/phone interpretation, position the device so the client can see the clinician clearly.

Handling Emotional Content: Allow time for emotional responses. Interpreters may experience secondary trauma — be attentive. Do not ask the interpreter to comfort the client or provide emotional support.

Post-Session Debriefing: Debrief with the interpreter, especially after difficult sessions. Check for interpreter well-being. Discuss any cultural context or nuances that may have been relevant. Document that an interpreter was used, the language, and the mode (in-person, phone, video).

Cultural Considerations in Interpreted Sessions

Cultural Considerations Guide

▼

Language and Mental Health Concepts: Many mental health concepts do not translate directly across languages and cultures. “Depression” may not have an equivalent word; somatic expressions of distress (headaches, stomach pain) may be more culturally normative than emotional descriptions. “Anxiety” may be described in terms of physical symptoms or spiritual distress.

Stigma: Mental health stigma varies significantly across cultures. Some clients may be reluctant to discuss psychological symptoms in front of any interpreter from their community. Consider using phone interpreters for greater anonymity when community size is small.

Family Dynamics: In many cultures, family involvement in healthcare is expected. Be aware of power dynamics when family members are present. Some family members may attempt to filter or modify what the client says.

Do Not Use Family Members as Interpreters: Especially children — this is harmful and unreliable. Family members may filter information, may not have the vocabulary for clinical concepts, and the dynamic distorts the clinical relationship. Use professional interpreters or language line services.

LEP Requirements: Organizations must: (1) identify LEP individuals who need language assistance, (2) provide language assistance services at no cost, (3) train staff on LEP policies, (4) provide notice of rights in languages understood by the community served, (5) monitor and update the language access plan.

Organizational Obligations: Develop a Language Access Plan (LAP). Translate vital documents into frequently encountered languages. Provide interpreter services for all client interactions — not just clinical sessions (scheduling, intake, billing, crisis). Budget for language services.

Documenting Language Preference: Record the client’s preferred language at intake. Document interpreter use at each encounter. Note if the client declines interpreter services (informed decision).

Complaints: Office for Civil Rights (OCR), U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. File within 180 days of the discriminatory act. OCR can investigate, mediate, or pursue enforcement action.
